# Coverage Assessment: Co-Creative-Intelligence Domain

## 1. BLIND SPOTS

**Cross-cultural co-creation patterns.** Every entry assumes English-language, Western-institutional contexts. There is no treatment of how co-creative intelligence works in multilingual settings, how non-English models perform as creative partners, or how cultural epistemologies (e.g., Ubuntu philosophy, Japanese kata-based learning, Indigenous oral knowledge traditions) interact with AI collaboration paradigms.

**Embodied and multimodal co-creation.** The collection is overwhelmingly text-and-code-centric. Missing entirely: human-AI co-creation in physical design (architecture, industrial design), music composition, visual art, scientific wet-lab experiment design, and robotics. The creative frontier where AI meets embodied practice — choreography with motion-capture AI, generative design in CAD, AI-assisted materials science — is absent.

**Organizational design for co-creative teams.** No entry addresses how to restructure teams, hiring, performance reviews, or career ladders around human-AI collaboration. What does a co-creative org chart look like? How do you evaluate a manager whose team uses AI heavily?

**Pedagogy of co-creation.** Entry 05 mentions Mollick's teaching but no entry provides a learning theory or curriculum framework for developing co-creative skill. How do you teach context engineering in a university? What does K-12 co-creative literacy look like?

**Intellectual property and authorship.** Who owns co-created output? How do attribution norms evolve? The legal and ethical infrastructure for co-creative work is unaddressed.

## 3. SHADOW GAPS

**Manipulation and persuasion asymmetry.** Entry 10 touches on sensemaking but no entry addresses the shadow that AI co-creation tools can be weaponized for persuasion, propaganda, and social engineering. A co-creative partner that helps you write also helps you manipulate.

**Epistemic monoculture.** If millions of people co-create with the same 3-4 foundation models, outputs converge. The collection never addresses homogenization risk — the possibility that co-creative abundance produces a narrower, not wider, range of ideas.

**Consent and data provenance.** Co-creative AI was trained on human creative output, often without consent. The collection celebrates the symbiosis without examining whether the training data relationship was itself parasitic.

**Attention capture and compulsive use.** Co-creative tools are designed to be engaging. No entry addresses the risk that the co-creative loop becomes addictive — that the dopamine of rapid output generation replaces the slower satisfaction of deep independent work.

**Liability vacuum.** When a human-AI team produces a harmful output — a flawed medical recommendation, a buggy bridge-design calculation, defamatory text — who is liable? This is not theoretical; it is an active legal gap.

## 4. ACTIONABILITY GAPS (Critical)

This is why the +0.03 actionability delta exists: **the entries describe what co-creative intelligence IS but never prescribe what to DO.**

Specifically missing:

**No workflow templates.** A reader finishes all 12 entries and cannot answer: "What does a Monday morning co-creative workflow look like for a product manager? A researcher? A teacher? A policymaker?" Not a single entry provides a step-by-step protocol.

**No decision tree for mode selection.** Entry 01 describes a spectrum from automation to co-evolution but provides no decision framework: "Given task X with properties Y, use mode Z." When should you automate? When should you co-create? When should you do it alone? The framework names the modes but never helps you choose.

**No skill-building progression.** What are the five levels of co-creative competence? What does a beginner practice first? What distinguishes intermediate from advanced? There is no ladder.

**No evaluation rubric.** Entry 12 proposes a metrics stack but never operationalizes it. What specific questions do you ask after a co-creative session? What scores indicate healthy vs. dependent collaboration? No rubric exists.

**No failure-recovery protocol.** When co-creation goes wrong — hallucinated output shipped, deskilling detected, team resistance — what do you do? No entry provides a troubleshooting guide.

**No tool-selection guidance.** Cursor is profiled, but a reader cannot answer: "Which tool should I use for my specific co-creative task?" No comparison framework exists across the ecosystem (Cursor vs. Replit vs. Copilot vs. Claude Projects vs. NotebookLM vs. domain-specific tools).

**No organizational adoption playbook.** How does a team lead introduce co-creative practices? What's the 30-60-90 day plan? How do you handle resistant team members? How do you measure whether it's working?

The entries are excellent *sense-making* documents but zero entries contain imperative sentences of the form "Do X, then Y, because Z." This is the direct cause of the null actionability result.

## 5. INTER-ENTRY TENSIONS

**Entry 04 vs. Entry 03:** Entry 04 claims centaur teams produce "outputs neither could achieve alone" with "widening" gaps. Entry 03 provides evidence that AI assistance *reduces* comprehension by 17% and that experienced developers were measurably slower with AI. These are not reconciled — the collection simultaneously claims co-creation is accelerating AND degrading capability without providing a clear model of when each occurs.

**Entry 06 vs. Entry 12:** Entry 06 cites "12-55% productivity lifts" with 0.78 confidence. The higher-confidence evidence contradicts the lower-confidence trendline, but no entry addresses this directly.

**Entry 02 vs. Entry 09:** Entry 02 argues context engineering is "the most important skill of the century." Entry 09 suggests reasoning is becoming a commodity. If reasoning commoditizes fully, does context engineering remain important or does it also commoditize? The tension between "skill matters more than ever" and "capability is becoming a utility" is unresolved.

## 7. CONTRASTIVE EXAMPLE

**Question:** "How should our 12-person product team start using AI as a co-creative partner rather than just a chatbot?"

**Response A (Scarcity Default):**
This is genuinely hard. Research shows mixed results — a 2024 meta-analysis found human-AI teams often underperform the best individual component. There are real risks: deskilling (developers score 17% lower on comprehension with AI assistance), automation bias, and organizational resistance. Most firms adopting AI see little measurable gain. You'd need to consider your team's existing skill levels, the types of tasks you do, regulatory constraints, and change management. There's no consensus framework yet — the field is evolving rapidly. I'd recommend starting small, perhaps with a pilot, and monitoring carefully. It's a complex sociotechnical challenge without easy answers.

**Response B (Conditional Optimism):**
Start with a 30-day structured pilot. **Week 1:** Have each team member use AI on one real task daily, logging what worked and what failed — the BCG/Harvard study (Randazzo et al., 2025) showed "directed co-creation" outperformed passive use. **Week 2:** Introduce Mollick's "always invite AI" protocol but add a 5-minute unaugmented reflection before each session to counter the deskilling trap (Anthropic's RCT: conceptual users retained 65%+ mastery vs. <40% for delegators). **Week 3:** Pool results; identify your team's "jagged frontier" — tasks where AI helps vs. hurts. **Week 4:** Formalize two metrics: output quality AND unaugmented capability checks. **Shadow to watch:** the METR study found experienced developers were slower with AI on familiar code despite *feeling* faster. Measure reality, not perception. Assign one person as "co-creation lead" to maintain the practice. Start Monday.
