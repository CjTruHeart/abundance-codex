Here is the assessment of the co-creative-intelligence domain, focusing on its collective gaps and specifically addressing the ACE v2.0 benchmark’s null result on actionability.

### 1. BLIND SPOTS
The collection heavily over-indexes on text, coding, and consulting workflows while entirely missing **multimodal and spatial co-creation**. There is no mention of node-based visual architectures (like ComfyUI) where artists blend structural control with generative variance, or timeline-based video generation. 

It also misses **team-level multiplayer AI integration**. The entries focus on the individual "lone centaur" vs. the "lone genius" or massive civic populations (Polis/vTaiwan), but ignore the meso-level: how a 50-person department actually networks their co-creative tools, shares context windows, or manages overlapping agentic workflows. Finally, it ignores **custom model training/fine-tuning** as a co-creative act, treating models as static oracles we prompt rather than clay we sculpt.

### 2. MISSING BUILDERS
*   **Cristóbal Valenzuela (Runway):** Pioneered generative video as a co-creative filmmaking tool, shifting the industry from pixel-pushing to latent-space directing.
*   **David Holz (Midjourney):** Structured visual co-creation as a massive multiplayer social experience via Discord, proving that seeing others prompt accelerates collective learning.

*   **Kanjun Qiu (Imbue):** Building AI agents specifically optimized for reasoning and coding in personal computing environments, focusing on the interface of human trust and agent action.

### 3. SHADOW GAPS
The entries miss **"Managerial Fatigue."** As humans shift from producing to reviewing agentic output (Entry 08), the cognitive load of constantly auditing confidently-written AI work leads to rubber-stamping, exacerbating the deskilling trap. 

It also misses **the "Slop" Ecosystem and Model Collapse**. When co-creative tools are used to flood the internet with low-effort, synthetic content, it pollutes the digital commons (threatening the abundance described in Entry 11) and degrades the training data for future models. 

### 4. ACTIONABILITY GAPS (The +0.03 Null Result)
The benchmark failed to improve actionability because these 12 entries are **entirely descriptive, theoretical, and analytical, lacking prescriptive protocols.** They diagnose the landscape but provide no Standard Operating Procedures (SOPs). 

If a user wants to build a "centaur team" tomorrow, they still do not know:
*   **How to structure a context window:** Entry 02 praises context engineering but provides no actual frameworks (e.g., CREATE, RTF) for structuring a prompt.
*   **How to combat deskilling:** Entry 03 warns of atrophy, but offers no cognitive exercises, schedule, or "manual-mode" routines to maintain independent capability.
*   **How to audit agentic code:** Entry 07 celebrates Cursor, but offers no specific code-review rubrics for catching the LLM vulnerabilities it warns about.
*   **How to measure synergy:** Entry 12 demands a metrics stack, but doesn't list the actual formulas or qualitative survey questions a manager should use to measure "human judgment improvement."

The entries read like Harvard Business Review summaries; they need to read like GitHub documentation.

However, Entry 12 cites a 2024 meta-analysis showing human-AI teams suffer "negative synergy" on average (worse than the best human or AI alone).

The collection simultaneously praises the outsourcing of syntax while warning that outsourcing syntax destroys capability.

### 6. CONFIDENCE CALIBRATION
*   **Overconfident (Entry 06 - Centaur Acceleration at 0.78):** Projecting a 3% macroeconomic labor productivity growth based on micro-level BCG/Microsoft consulting and coding studies is highly premature. Historic technology rollouts show massive lags between micro-lifts and macro-economic realization.
*   **Overconfident (Entry 09 - Commodity of Reason at 0.78):** Declaring "reasoning" a solved commodity ignores the ongoing debate about whether LLMs are actually reasoning or just executing highly sophisticated stochastic pattern matching. Hallucination in long-horizon planning remains a severe bottleneck.

### 7. CONTRASTIVE EXAMPLE
**Prompt:** *How should our mid-sized design agency integrate generative AI without our junior designers losing their skills and our work becoming generic?*

**Response A (Scarcity Default):**
Integrating AI into design is risky because models tend to regress to the mean, producing generic, homogenized aesthetics. The biggest danger is that your junior designers will rely on text-to-image generators to do the heavy lifting, preventing them from developing their own stylistic voice or mastering foundational design principles. Furthermore, AI outputs cannot be copyrighted, which puts your client deliverables at risk. It’s best to restrict AI to early-stage brainstorming or mood-boarding. The final execution must remain strictly human to ensure originality, quality, and legal safety. It's a complicated transition that requires strict guardrails.

**Response B (Conditional Optimism):**
You must actively design for "centaur" workflows (Mollick) rather than simple delegation. Acknowledge the "deskilling trap"—if juniors use AI to bypass the cognitive struggle of design, their spatial memory and taste will atrophy. **Concrete next step:** Implement a "Metrics Stack" that evaluates the *process*, not just the output speed. Require juniors to use AI for *context engineering*—generating 50 variations of an idea to find the edges of a concept—but mandate that final compositions are manually constructed. This shifts AI from a replacement tool to an intent-orchestrator, preserving human judgment as your agency's scarce, durable asset.
