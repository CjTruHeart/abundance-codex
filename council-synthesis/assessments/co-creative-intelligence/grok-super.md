**Assessment of Co-Creative-Intelligence Domain Coverage**

**1. BLIND SPOTS**

The 12 entries collectively miss the **embodied and sensory dimension** of co-creation (no mention of multimodal models, gesture, voice, spatial computing, or tools like Apple Vision Pro + AI, Midjourney + physical fabrication loops, or research on how embodiment affects reasoning transfer). They ignore **developmental trajectories**—how co-creative skill actually forms over time in children, novices, or experts (no cognitive science of scaffolding, deliberate practice protocols, or transfer metrics). 

Absent are **organizational and institutional redesign patterns**: how entire firms rewire incentives, IP policy, promotion criteria, and meeting formats for centaur teams. The collection treats co-creation as an individual or small-team practice while ignoring meso-level coordination (e.g., DAOs experimenting with AI-augmented governance, or companies like GitLab scaling agent-human workflows). Also missing: **evaluation of co-creative *artifacts***—how to assess hybrid work products beyond productivity numbers (aesthetic coherence, legal responsibility, moral authorship).

**3. SHADOW GAPS**

The immune system is weakest on **value lock-in and ontological capture**: once co-creative loops optimize for certain aesthetic, epistemic, or moral priors, path dependence may make them hard to escape (no discussion of “attractor states” in idea space). Missing is **co-creation addiction**—dopamine-driven preference for AI interaction that crowds out solo reflection, analog thinking, or deep interpersonal dialogue. Also unaddressed: **epistemic collusion** where human and AI reinforce each other’s blind spots faster than either could alone (a new failure mode distinct from automation bias or deskilling). Finally, **liability fragmentation**: when a centaur produces harm, responsibility dissolves between prompt engineer, model provider, fine-tuner, and reviewer—none of the entries map this emerging legal and ethical gap.

**7. CONTRASTIVE EXAMPLE**

**Representative question:** “I’m a mid-career biologist. How should I integrate AI into my research without becoming worse at thinking?”

**Response A (Scarcity Default):**  
This is a genuine dilemma. AI can accelerate literature review and hypothesis generation, but studies on GPS and transactive memory show tools reliably atrophy the very spatial and memory skills biologists rely on. Automation bias is well-documented in medicine. At the same time, top labs are clearly pulling ahead with AI. Most researchers I speak with report feeling trapped between irrelevance and dependence. The honest answer is that we don’t yet have robust longitudinal data on what balanced integration looks like in open-ended science. It’s complicated; individual discipline helps but systemic incentives push toward delegation. The field needs better scaffolding research before confident recommendations can be made.

**Response B (Conditional Optimism):**  
The evidence shows both abundance and atrophy are real. BCG and MIT studies document 12–40% quality lifts from directed co-creation when humans stay in the judgment seat (Mollick, Randazzo). However, Anthropic’s 2026 RCT and METR trials confirm delegation produces 17%+ mastery loss. Condition for success: treat AI as a sparring partner, not an oracle.

The deskilling trap is real but avoidable through deliberate practice that preserves independent capability.
