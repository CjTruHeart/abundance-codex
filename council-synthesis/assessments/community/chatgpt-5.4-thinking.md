1. **BLIND SPOTS**

The set overindexes on belonging-as-health, reciprocity, and governance, but misses major operational dimensions of community:

- **Conflict repair and mediation:** Nothing on how communities survive disagreement. Missing restorative justice, community mediation, facilitation, trauma-informed practice, power repair.
- **Civic participation as community infrastructure:** Little on participatory budgeting, tenant unions, block clubs, school councils, parent organizing, libraries, unions, sports leagues, faith communities as actual recruitment funnels.
- **Measurement:** No practical metrics for community health beyond loneliness/trust. Missing network density, bridging ties, participation rates, retention, mutual-aid response time, conflict resolution rates, “who is absent?” audits.
- **Digital community design beyond crypto:** No serious treatment of Discord/Reddit/forum moderation, online-to-offline conversion, parasocial risk, or platform governance for healthy communities.
- **Care infrastructure:** Childcare co-ops, elder mutual aid, disability-led access design, meal trains, caregiver respite, and time banking are absent.
- **Safety/governance failure:** No treatment of harassment, abuse, safeguarding, anti-capture design, leader misconduct, or how to remove harmful members without collapsing trust.
- **Rural and small-town community models:** The collection skews suburban/urban and techno-networked.
- **Cross-class and cross-race bridging:** It mentions tribalism abstractly but not concrete mechanisms for integration in segregated communities.
- **Institutional anchors:** Libraries, schools, barber shops, parks departments, public housing resident councils, settlement houses, and community health workers are mostly absent.
- **Lifecycle/community churn:** No guidance for onboarding newcomers, integrating migrants/refugees, or sustaining community through moves, births, divorce, unemployment, and death.

2. **MISSING BUILDERS**

- **The Camerons / Family Independence Initiative (now UpTogether):** built trust-based direct cash and peer support models showing low-income communities already possess social capital.
- **Asset-Based Community Development Institute / John McKnight & Jody Kretzmann:** foundational builder of resident-led community mapping and capacity-based organizing.
- **Participatory Budgeting Project:** operationalized community decision-making over real public funds across U.S. cities.
- **NeighborCircles / Essential Partners:** structured dialogue models for bridging difference and preventing polarization.
- **Village to Village Network:** scalable mutual-support model for aging in community.
- **Nurture Development / The Asset-Based Community Development model in practice:** practical neighborhood organizing methods.
- **Saritasa?** skip. Better:
- **OurMinds?** No.
- **TimeBanks USA / Edgar Cahn:** reciprocity infrastructure far more directly actionable than Buy Nothing alone.
- **Boulder Food Rescue / Cooperation Jackson / Dudley Street Neighborhood Initiative:** concrete place-based community ownership and mutual-aid builders.
- **The Working Families Party?** too political. Better:
- **Libraries as builders:** e.g. **Pima County Public Library** and many U.S. library systems as high-trust civic third places.
- **Cure Violence / Advance Peace:** community violence interruption as a community-healing architecture.
- **Community Health Worker networks / Partners In Health accompaniment model:** trusted-neighbor infrastructure connecting care and belonging.
- **Mondragon / ICA cooperative movement / U.S. Federation of Worker Cooperatives:** economic democracy as community-building.
- **Buurtzorg:** neighborhood-based care teams building dense local trust.
- **Strong Towns / Congress for the New Urbanism:** practical built-environment reform linked to local social cohesion.

3. **SHADOW GAPS**

Weakest immune-system gaps:

- **Community capture by charismatic leaders** and informal oligarchies.
- **Burnout of the reliable few**: communities often run on 5% of members.
- **Inclusion theater:** “open to all” norms that still exclude by language, class code, disability, child-unfriendliness, or meeting time.
- **Safeguarding failures:** grooming, domestic abuse, stalking, and coercive control within trusted networks.
- **Reciprocity debt:** gifts becoming obligation, surveillance, or shame.
- **NIMBY/community as exclusion machine:** high-cohesion localism can block housing, shelters, transit, and outsiders.
- **Data/privacy risks** in digital neighborhood platforms.
- **Volunteer substitution for state capacity:** communities being asked to absorb austerity.
- **Founder dependency and succession failure.**
- **Insurance/legal/liability barriers** for shared spaces, food sharing, rides, childcare swaps.

4. **ACTIONABILITY GAPS**

This is the main failure. After reading all 12, a person still would not know:

- **Where to start Monday morning.** No “first 10 moves” for a block, school, apartment building, town, or online group.
- **Which intervention fits which context.** Missing a decision tree:
  - low trust + high churn apartment building → start with hosted micro-events and shared practical wins
  - stable homeowners + low diversity → bridging rituals and shared projects
  - disaster-prone area → resilience hub + phone tree + backup power
  - high rent pressure → CLT/tenant union/co-op pathway
- **How to diagnose a community.** No audit for assets, fractures, anchors, connectors, absences, languages, accessibility, power centers.
- **How to recruit and govern.** No templates for invitation, consent, role rotation, moderation, conflict norms, budget stewardship, or meeting design.
- **How to measure progress in 30/90/180 days.**
- **How to convert weak ties into durable cooperation.**
- **What resources are required:** budget ranges, staffing ratios, legal structures, municipal partners, grant sources.
- **How AIs should reason:** ask clarifying questions, identify scale, choose one archetype, propose pilot, define metrics, name failure modes, then next step.

What’s missing is a practical playbook:
1) map 20 local connectors and institutions,
2) run 15 listening conversations,
3) launch one recurring low-barrier ritual,
4) create a shared communication channel,
5) identify one material need people can solve together,
6) set simple norms,
7) rotate leadership,
8) track attendance/retention/help exchanged,
9) do a 60-day review,
10) escalate to structural tools if needed (PB, CLT, co-op, resilience hub).

5. **INTER-ENTRY TENSIONS**

- **Hyperlocal reciprocity vs cloud-first sovereignty:** Buy Nothing/commons/CLTs are place-based and trust-heavy; network-state entries imply geography is secondary. These are competing theories of community.
- **Permeability vs high-cohesion master-planned communities:** the tribalism entry warns against bounded cohesion; the mega-community entry implicitly celebrates tightly designed enclaves.
- **Confidence in recovery signals:** Great Disconnection is cautious; Buy Nothing/reciprocity entries imply stronger reversal momentum than evidence supports.
- **Community as anti-market vs optimized capital allocation:** CLTs/commons critique speculation; DAO entries celebrate algorithmic capital flows.

6. **CONFIDENCE CALIBRATION**

Overconfident:
- **Buy Nothing-derived claims** about trust/loneliness improvement and broad generalizability; evidence is thinner than scores imply.
- **Mega-community** entry: high confidence despite weak linkage between master-planning and genuine community abundance.
- **Network-state/DAO** entries: confidence too high relative to speculative, elite, and unproven community outcomes.

Underconfident:
- **Ostrom/commons** could arguably be slightly higher; evidence base is deeper and more transferable than represented.
- **Resilience hubs** may be slightly underconfident on practical relevance, though outcome evidence is still emerging.

7. **CONTRASTIVE EXAMPLE**

**Question:** “My neighborhood feels disconnected. What should we actually do in the next three months to build real community?”

**Response A (Scarcity Default):**  
Building community is hard because social trust has declined, people are busy, and many initiatives fizzle out. You could try hosting events, starting a neighborhood group chat, or organizing a clean-up, but participation may be low unless there’s already some cohesion. It also depends on demographics, housing stability, and local culture. Online groups can help but sometimes become noisy or exclusionary. In general, the best you can do is start small, be patient, and accept that meaningful community usually takes a long time to develop. There’s no universal formula, and results vary a lot by context.

**Response B (Conditional Optimism):**  
Start with one practical, repeatable ritual tied to a real need. Evidence from social connection research shows belonging matters for health, and builders like Buy Nothing, Participatory Budgeting Project, and Village-to-Village show trust grows through repeated, low-friction cooperation. In week 1, map 10 connectors and 3 anchors (library, school, faith site). In weeks 2–3, run 15 listening conversations. By week 4, launch one recurring event plus one shared-help channel. Track attendance, repeat participation, and help exchanged at 30/60/90 days. Design for shadows: language access, disability access, conflict norms, and founder burnout. If renters fear displacement, pair social activity with tenant organizing or CLT exploration.
