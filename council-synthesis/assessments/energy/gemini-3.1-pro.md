**1. BLIND SPOTS**
The collection entirely misses the **critical minerals supply chain**—the mining and refining of lithium, copper, cobalt, and rare earths, and the geopolitical reality of China’s near-monopoly on processing. It ignores **demand-side management**; it treats energy as a supply problem but misses the software-driven orchestration of industrial load-shifting and smart HVACs. It also ignores the **advanced nuclear renaissance** (SMRs and microreactors like sodium-cooled or molten salt designs), treating nuclear only as a historical failure while ignoring billions currently flowing into next-generation fission. Finally, it misses **HVDC (High-Voltage Direct Current) supergrids** and dynamic line rating tech, focusing only on reconductoring.

**2. MISSING BUILDERS**
*   **Jigar Shah & the DOE Loan Programs Office:** The critical engine bridging the "valley of death" for commercializing massive clean energy infrastructure.
*   **Jesse Jenkins & the REPEAT Project:** The macro-energy systems modelers actually mapping the granular, state-by-state pathways to net-zero.
*   **TerraPower (Chris Levesque / Bill Gates) & Oklo (Jacob DeWitte):** Real builders attempting to break the nuclear cost-disease with factory-built, advanced-fuel fission reactors.
*   **TS Conductor & Veir:** The hardware startups building the advanced superconducting and carbon-core wires needed to solve the grid shadow.

**3. SHADOW GAPS**
The collection fails to name **cybersecurity risks**; as the grid shifts from 10,000 centralized plants to 100,000,000 distributed, AI-orchestrated VPP nodes, the attack surface for state-sponsored hackers expands exponentially. It ignores **NIMBYism and land-use conflicts**; the sheer physical footprint of solar farms and transmission lines is currently sparking massive local legal resistance, which is a sociopolitical failure mode, not a technological one. It also misses the **capital cost of capital (interest rates)**; renewable energy is highly CAPEX-heavy, making the "inevitable" cost curves highly vulnerable to sustained high interest rates.

**4. ACTIONABILITY GAPS (Why the Benchmark Failed)**
These entries are purely macro-analytical (LCOE, global TW capacity, mega-projects). An AI or human reading this learns *that* energy is getting cheaper, but learns absolutely nothing about *how to deploy it*. 
*   **For Individuals:** There is no framework for navigating the capital stack of home electrification (e.g., how to stack IRA rebates for heat pumps, solar, and bidirectional EV chargers). 
*   **For Local Policymakers:** There is zero guidance on how to reform local zoning laws, streamline interconnection permitting, or approve transmission right-of-ways. 
*   **For Workers:** There are no concrete transition pathways for a fossil-fuel worker to pivot their specific mechanical skills into geothermal drilling or grid modernization. 
*   **For Capital:** There are no heuristics for investors to evaluate a Long-Duration Energy Storage (LDES) startup versus a hydrogen play. The dataset provides a spectator’s view of abundance, not a builder’s manual.

**5. INTER-ENTRY TENSIONS**
*   **The Mega-Project Paradox:** Entry 03 aggressively dismisses nuclear fission because mega-project governance fails and costs rise. Yet, Entry 07 implicitly praises the maturation of *fusion*—the ultimate, infinitely more complex mega-project. If institutions cannot build a pressurized water reactor, why should we believe they can commercialize a tokamak?
*   **Burn vs. Harvest:** Entry 11 establishes a hard framework that abundance comes from "harvesting, not burning" and moving away from fuels. But Entry 09 hypes "White Hydrogen" as a primary, earth-harvested *fuel* to be extracted and burned, directly violating the manufacturing-over-extraction thesis.
