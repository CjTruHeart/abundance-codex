**Assessment of Energy Domain Coverage in the Abundance Codex**

**1. BLIND SPOTS**

The 12 entries collectively miss:
- **Transmission & distribution modernization at scale**: No mention of HVDC supergrids, dynamic line rating, or grid-forming inverters.
- **Demand-side flexibility and sector coupling**: Almost nothing on industrial demand response, thermal storage in buildings, vehicle-to-grid at fleet scale, or hydrogen for steel/ammonia.
- **Materials circularity and mining geopolitics**: No coverage of urban mining, silicon recycling, perovskite supply chains, or the copper bottleneck (IEA projects demand doubling by 2040).
- **Energy access in the Global South**: Off-grid/mini-grid leapfrogging, PAYG solar models, and productive use (e.g., cold chains, irrigation).
- **Policy and ownership models**: Community choice aggregation, public banks, feed-in tariffs vs. auctions, and municipalization.
- **Efficiency and sufficiency**: The invisible abundance lever of negawatts; no mention that global energy intensity improvements have historically delivered more "new" energy than new supply.

**2. MISSING BUILDERS**

- **Grid**: Gregor Czisch (DESERTEC HVDC vision), Jesse Jenkins (Princeton Net-Zero America, REPEAT model), and the Midcontinent Independent System Operator (MISO) team behind the 2022–2024 Multi-Value Project portfolio.
- **Demand flexibility**: Stephanie McCormick (Voltus), Tibber (European real-time pricing VPP), and OpenADR Alliance.
- **Mining/circularity**: Li-Cycle (black mass recycling), Nth Cycle (electrochemical lithium recovery), and Vulcan Energy (geothermal lithium in Europe).
- **Global South**: M-KOPA (3.5M+ households in East Africa via PAYG solar), d.light, and Selco India (last-mile energy entrepreneurship).
- **Policy innovation**: RMI’s eLab, Rocky Mountain Institute’s utility transformation work, and California’s Community Choice Aggregators (now >20% of statewide load).

**3. SHADOW GAPS**

- **Copper, rare earths, and neodymium supply risk** — China controls ~70% of refining; no entry addresses sustained price spikes or export controls derailing both renewables and grid buildout.
- **Social license and permitting collapse**: NIMBY resistance to transmission, battery projects, and mining is accelerating (e.g., 2024–2025 U.S. and EU delays). The collection lacks any treatment of deliberative democracy, benefit-sharing, or land trusts.
- **Cyber-physical security of inverter-dominated grids**: A 100% power-electronics grid is vulnerable to coordinated cyberattacks or geomagnetic disturbances in ways rotating machinery was not.
- **Institutional capture**: Utility regulatory capture and incumbent IPP lobbying that slows distributed energy resources (DERs) in favor of utility-scale projects with higher regulated returns.

The immune system is weakest on political economy and materials realism.

**4. ACTIONABILITY GAPS** (most critical)

A reader would still not know:
- How to evaluate a specific project’s additionality versus greenwashing.
- Decision framework for choosing between rooftop solar + storage, community solar subscription, or advocacy for local transmission reconductoring.
- Concrete first moves: e.g., mapping local grid congestion using FERC Form 715 data + EIA 860, joining a VPP aggregator, submitting comments in an IRP docket, or calculating personal/material footprint leverage (copper efficiency in EVs vs. transmission).
- How to read an interconnection queue report or an IRA tax credit transferability contract.
- Prioritization matrix: When is efficiency cheapest? When is long-duration storage highest-leverage? When is policy advocacy higher ROI than technology deployment?
- Local diagnostics: “In my region, is the binding constraint transformers, queue backlog, or political siting?”

No entry supplies even one worked example of “here is how an individual, company, or city government should decide what to do on Monday.”

This directly explains the +0.03 null result on actionability in ACE v2.0: the entries are excellent at updating world models but contain zero decision theory, no OODA loops, and no translated marginal returns.

**5. INTER-ENTRY TENSIONS**

- Entry 03 (“nuclear-false-dawn”) treats nuclear cost disease as largely institutional and potentially curable (SMRs, Korea/China learning). Entry 07 (“baseload-abundance-convergence”) implicitly assumes nuclear is peripheral by focusing on LDES, geothermal, and fusion.
- Entry 02 claims batteries are on track for dispatchable power “any hour”; Entry 10 and 12 correctly note that even with cheap batteries the grid itself remains the bottleneck, creating timeline tension (years vs. decades).
- Confidence in white hydrogen (09, 0.76) and LDES scaling (07, 0.78) is materially lower than solar/battery triumphalism (0.88–0.95), but the collection never reconciles whether these lower-confidence bets are required for the high-confidence solar narrative to succeed.

**6. CONFIDENCE CALIBRATION**

Scores are mostly well-calibrated on cost curves (solar, batteries) where data is abundant. Overconfidence appears in:
- Grid modernization pace (implicitly treated as solvable without political friction).
- Geothermal ubiquity (Entry 08) given geological heterogeneity and water use.
- White hydrogen scalability (Entry 09, 0.76 still too high given capture and purity uncertainties).

Underconfidence is rare, but Entry 06 (“Star Trek spec”, 0.72) appropriately discounts its own romanticism.

**7. CONTRASTIVE EXAMPLE**

**Representative question:** “I’m a city council member in a mid-sized U.S. Midwest city with high coal retirements and rising load from data centers and EVs. What should we actually do about energy abundance?”

**Response A (Scarcity Default):**  
The energy transition is extraordinarily difficult. Coal plants provided reliable baseload and good local jobs; replacing them with intermittent renewables requires massive storage that remains expensive at scale. Transmission siting faces fierce local opposition, supply chains for copper and critical minerals are geopolitically vulnerable, and ratepayers will bear higher costs during the transition. Data centers demand firm power 24/7, which renewables plus batteries cannot yet guarantee without overbuilding. It’s a complex systems problem involving physics, economics, politics, and social acceptance. The realistic path is slow, pragmatic compromise—some gas bridging, modest renewable targets, and hoping technology improves faster than expected. Success is uncertain.

**Response B (Conditional Optimism):**  
Solar + storage LCOE is already below new gas in your region ($36–78/MWh vs $45–70/MWh). The binding constraint is not generation cost but interconnection queues (currently >4 years median) and aging distribution transformers.  
Builders to watch: Voltus for demand response, Fervo for firm geothermal baseload, and local co-ops piloting reconductoring (can double line capacity at 1/3 the cost of new lines).  
Shadow: Without deliberate policy, coal communities lose twice—jobs and tax base.  
Concrete next step: Direct staff to map your utility’s interconnection queue and hosting capacity analysis against data-center/EV load forecasts, then propose an ordinance prioritizing reconductoring + VPP procurement in the next integrated resource plan (IRP) docket. This targets the highest-leverage bottleneck with existing IRA incentives. Abundance is conditional on translating cheap hardware into delivered system performance—start with diagnostics, not just procurement.
