# Longevity Domain Coverage Assessment

## 1. BLIND SPOTS

**Senolytics as a therapeutic class** receive zero dedicated coverage despite being arguably closer to clinical translation than epigenetic reprogramming. Unity Biotechnology's and Senolytic Therapeutics' dasatinib+quercetin trials, the Mayo Clinic's AFFIRM trial results, and the broader senolytic pipeline are absent. This is a glaring omission — senolytics target one of the 12 hallmarks directly and have human data.

**GLP-1 receptor agonists as accidental longevity drugs** — semaglutide's emerging cardiovascular, renal, and potentially neurodegenerative benefits represent the largest real-world longevity intervention happening *right now* across tens of millions of patients. No entry addresses this.

**Caloric restriction and fasting interventions** with human data (CALERIE trial showing 2-3% reduction in biological aging pace) are absent despite being the most robustly studied longevity intervention across species.

**Microbiome-aging axis** — the gut-brain-immune connection to aging, fecal microbiota transplant rejuvenation studies in mice, and the role of microbial diversity decline with age.

**Mitochondrial dysfunction therapies** — urolithin A (Timeline/Amazentis), NAD+ metabolism beyond supplement critique, mitochondrial transplantation research.

**Sleep architecture and aging** — Matthew Walker's work, the bidirectional relationship between sleep disruption and neurodegeneration, glymphatic clearance.

**Cryonics, brain preservation, and digital legacy** — whether one considers these serious or speculative, they represent a significant cultural and financial phenomenon in the longevity space that shapes public perception.

## 3. SHADOW GAPS

**Regulatory capture and FDA structural limitations** — the FDA still does not recognize aging as an indication. The TAME trial's struggle to get approved illustrates this. No entry examines how regulatory architecture itself blocks longevity therapeutics.

**Longevity escape velocity as ideological capture** — the concept that each year of research buys more than a year of life extension creates unfalsifiable optimism loops that resist correction.

**Cognitive decline without physical decline** — entries focus on biological age and physical healthspan but largely ignore dementia, which is the most feared aging outcome and the one where therapeutic progress has been slowest.

**Insurance and reimbursement barriers** — even if therapies work, who pays? No entry addresses the health economics of longevity interventions or the actuarial disruption.

**Geopolitical competition in longevity** — China's aging crisis (400M over 60 by 2035), Japan's demographic collapse, and how different nations' desperation may drive reckless or accelerated regulatory approaches.

**Iatrogenic harm from longevity interventions** — polypharmacy in aging populations, the risk of stacking unproven interventions, and the absence of pharmacovigilance infrastructure for longevity protocols.

## 4. ACTIONABILITY GAPS (Critical)

The null actionability result (+0.03) is predictable: these entries explain *what is happening* and *why it matters* but never tell anyone *what to do*. Specifically missing:

**For individuals:**
- No evidence-ranked protocol: "These 5 interventions have the strongest human evidence for healthspan extension: (1) 150+ min/week moderate exercise [RR 0.60-0.70], (2) Mediterranean/plant-forward diet [RR 0.79], (3) 7-8 hours sleep [U-shaped mortality curve], (4) maintain social connections [OR 1.50 survival], (5) don't smoke/limit alcohol [RR varies]." This simple hierarchy is never stated.
- No decision tree for supplement evaluation: "Before taking any longevity supplement, check: (a) Is there a Phase III human RCT? (b) What is the effect size? (c) What is the NNT? (d) What are known risks?"
- No biological age testing guide: "If you want to measure your biological age, here are the options ranked by validation: (1) clinical-grade epigenetic clocks [GrimAge, DunedinPACE], (2) blood biomarker panels, (3) wearable-derived estimates. Cost ranges: $300-$500 for methylation, $100-$200 for blood panels."
- No age-specific action frameworks: what a 30-year-old vs. 50-year-old vs. 70-year-old should prioritize differs enormously.

**For policymakers:**
- No model legislation or policy templates for recognizing aging as a medical condition
- No framework for pension/retirement system redesign given extended healthspans
- No cost-effectiveness comparison: "$1 invested in exercise infrastructure vs. $1 in reprogramming research"

**For builders/entrepreneurs:**
- No map of underfunded intervention categories
- No regulatory pathway guide (IND for aging indication, surrogate endpoint selection)
- No "if you have $X to invest in longevity, here's where marginal dollars have highest expected impact"

**For AI agents:**
- No structured decision schema (IF user_age > X AND risk_factor = Y THEN recommend Z)
- No confidence-weighted recommendation engine

## 5. INTER-ENTRY TENSIONS

**Entry 06 vs. Entry 09:** Both present the "Information Theory of Aging" but Entry 06 assigns confidence 0.72 while Entry 09 gives 0.90 to essentially the same claim. This is a 0.18 confidence gap on identical intellectual content, reflecting model disagreement about the theory's maturity.

**Entry 03 vs. Entry 05:** Entry 03 critiques the longevity grift ecosystem and specifically flags David Sinclair's orbit (Sirtris failure, supplement hype). Entry 05 profiles Life Biosciences — co-founded by Sinclair — as a breakthrough builder with 0.82 confidence. The collection never reconciles whether Sinclair's track record strengthens or weakens confidence in Life Biosciences.

**Entry 10 vs. Entry 01:** Entry 10 argues longevity is primarily ecological/social (Blue Zones). Entry 01 frames it as engineering problem (12 hallmarks). These are not contradictory but represent fundamentally different theories of change — population-level environmental design vs. individual-level molecular intervention — and no entry synthesizes them or helps a reader decide where to allocate attention.

**Entry 07 confidence (0.88) vs. Entry 06 confidence (0.72):** Entry 07 treats FDA clearance of ER-100 as a high-confidence breakthrough, while Entry 06 — which provides the theoretical foundation for that same therapy — is rated much lower. The clinical milestone is rated more confidently than the science underlying it, which is epistemically backwards.

## 6. CONFIDENCE CALIBRATION

**Overconfident:** Entry 07 (0.88) treats a Phase 1 trial clearance as a "breakthrough" — most Phase 1 drugs fail. The base rate for Phase 1 oncology/novel-mechanism drugs reaching approval is ~5-10%. Even calling this 0.88 confidence as a *regulatory* milestone overstates its therapeutic significance. Entry 08 (0.80) on the Miami bioscience ecosystem reads more like regional economic boosterism than a longevity entry; its confidence should be lower or it should be reframed.

**Underconfident:** Entry 02 (0.90) on the survival curve trendline is arguably the most robustly evidenced entry in the entire collection — UN demographic data is about as solid as evidence gets. This could be 0.95+. Entry 10 on Blue Zones/social determinants (0.79) undersells the strength of the social connection–mortality meta-analysis (148 studies, OR 1.50), which is among the most replicated findings in health research.

**Miscalibrated:** Entry 05 and Entry 07 cover nearly identical ground (Life Biosciences ER-100 trial) but are rated 0.82 and 0.88 respectively. The same event should not receive meaningfully different confidence scores across entries.

## 7. CONTRASTIVE EXAMPLE

**Question:** "I'm 45 years old and healthy. What should I actually do now to maximize my chances of living a long, healthy life?"

**Response A (Scarcity Default):**
Longevity science is advancing but remains largely preclinical. Epigenetic reprogramming shows promise in mice, but human trials are just beginning and outcomes are uncertain. Most anti-aging supplements lack rigorous clinical evidence. The honest answer is that we don't yet have proven interventions that meaningfully extend human lifespan beyond what's achievable through basic health behaviors. The field is complicated by hype, commercial interests, and a significant gap between animal and human results. Your best bet is general health maintenance — exercise, diet, sleep — but there's no evidence-based "longevity protocol" available today. Consult your physician for age-appropriate screenings.
