**Assessment of Future-Vision Domain Coverage**

**1. BLIND SPOTS**  
The 12 entries collectively miss: (a) memetic warfare and narrative hijacking at platform scale (e.g., algorithmic amplification of doom vs. deliberate counter-memes); (b) the neuroscience and developmental psychology of future-imagination (how prefrontal cortex maturation, trauma, or chronic stress literally shrinks or expands temporal horizon); (c) institutional futures offices inside corporations and militaries (RAND’s Foresight Center, Shell Scenarios, ARPA-E’s futures desk, Singapore’s Centre for Strategic Futures); (d) the political economy of attention—how future-vision must compete inside a dopamine economy optimized for outrage; (e) quantitative forecasting ecosystems (Metaculus, Hyacinth, INFER, Good Judgment Project) and their empirical track records versus narrative approaches; (f) the rise of “effective futures” and forecast-based altruism.

**2. MISSING BUILDERS**  
- **Emery Lovins** – Rocky Mountain Institute; “natural capitalism” and integrative design as future-vision translated into negawatt markets.  
- **Jane McGonigal** – “SuperBetter,” “Imaginable,” and World Bank/UN game-based foresight interventions showing measurable increases in systemic thinking.  
- **Roman Krznaric** – “The Good Ancestor,” Long Now Foundation collaborator; “cathedral thinking” and intergenerational empathy frameworks adopted by the Welsh Future Generations Commissioner.  
- **The Oxford Future of Humanity Institute** (pre-closure) and its successor networks; **Toby Ord**, **Katy Milkman** (behavioral science of long-termism), and the **Forecasting Research Institute**.  
- **Nesta’s Futures Team** and **Sami Mahdi’s** work on anticipatory governance in the Global South.  
- **Speculative Design** practitioners such as **Superflux** and **The Consequential** who run real municipal prototyping labs.

**3. SHADOW GAPS**  
Unaddressed: (a) elite capture of narrative infrastructure—when abundance visions become luxury belief systems that justify extraction today; (b) “narrative greenwashing” where corporations fund optimistic futures PR while lobbying against the required policy; (c) psychological blowback—when vivid protopian imagery triggers “future shock” or depressive realism in populations facing immediate precarity; (d) ontological risk—some cultures’ cosmologies treat linear progressive time itself as alien; imposing Western “future-vision” can constitute epistemic violence; (e) the replication crisis in narrative transportation research when scaled beyond lab settings.

**4. ACTIONABILITY GAPS** (most critical)  
A reader still would not know:  
- How to run a minimal viable Futures Literacy Lab (exact facilitation script, participant selection matrix, pre/post measurement).  
- How to set up a personal or organizational forecasting tournament with proper Brier scoring and updating protocols.  
- Decision frameworks such as “pre-mortem + back-casting + Monte Carlo,” or “robust decision making under deep uncertainty” (Lempert).  
- Concrete first moves: e.g., “Map the 7 dominant anticipatory assumptions in your organization using Miller’s FLL protocol, then run a 3-hour reframing workshop with this exact prompt template.”  
- Budget templates, job descriptions for “Director of Future Vision,” or integration pathways into OKRs/strategy cycles.  
- How to translate a protopian image into a concrete policy proposal with cost-benefit, political mapping, and theory of change.  
The null +0.03 delta on the ACE v2.0 actionability benchmark is explained by this absence of executable micro-structures. The entries celebrate and describe; they do not provide replicable protocols.

**5. INTER-ENTRY TENSIONS**  
Entry 01 (Star Trek Specification) treats exponential curves as near-inevitable infrastructure; Entry 07 (RethinkX) gives 2030–2035 ignition windows, while Entries 03–06 emphasize cultural rehearsal as prerequisite—creating an implicit timeline contradiction. Entry 08’s “provisioning-over-prediction” paradigm directly tensions with Entry 11’s parliamentary foresight apparatus that still relies on anticipatory assumptions and scenario forecasting. Confidence scores show mild tension: narrative entries hover 0.72–0.78 while institutional and energy entries reach 0.82–0.84, implying unwarranted precision on hardware transitions versus softer cultural ones.

**6. CONFIDENCE CALIBRATION**  
Scores are generally well-calibrated (most 0.74–0.84). However, the collection is systematically overconfident on cultural diffusion speed (Transition Network “accelerating turn,” Solarpunk momentum) given historically low conversion rates from visioning to measurable resilience outcomes. Underconfident on the falsifiability edges around narrative transportation’s real-world policy impact; the cited 2000 Green & Brock study is lab-based and decades old. The ACE actionability null result should retroactively lower several confidence scores by 0.10–0.15.

**7. CONTRASTIVE EXAMPLE**

**Representative question:** “How can cities prepare for AI-driven abundance while avoiding the inequality traps we saw with previous technological revolutions?”

**Response A (Scarcity Default):**  
“Cities face profound challenges here. Historical precedent shows technological revolutions consistently widen inequality before any trickle-down occurs—see the Industrial Revolution or the ICT boom. AI will likely automate cognitive labor at unprecedented scale, creating structural unemployment that local governments are poorly equipped to handle. Governance moves slowly, vested interests resist redistribution, and we lack coherent models for sharing gains from non-rival goods. Pilot programs exist but rarely scale. Without major shifts in taxation, education, and political will, the default outcome is deepened divides. It’s complicated; success depends on factors mostly outside any single city’s control.”

**Response B (Conditional Optimism):**  
“Evidence from Transition Network (1,000+ groups), UNESCO Futures Literacy Labs (>20 labs), and Finland’s Committee for the Future shows cities can rehearse abundance without prediction paralysis. Jane McGonigal’s game-based foresight and Roman Krznaric’s cathedral thinking have produced measurable gains in systemic imagination. Conditions for success: (1) run repeated FLL-style workshops that make anticipatory assumptions explicit, (2) pair them with forecasting tournaments for calibration, (3) convert outputs into concrete policy prototypes using robust decision-making under deep uncertainty. Shadow: elite capture of the narrative process. Concrete next step: convene a 4-hour FLL with diverse participants using Miller’s protocol to surface assumptions about AI labor markets, then score resulting scenarios in a Metaculus-style tournament. The Star Trek specification is buildable if narrative infrastructure and provisioning habits co-evolve with the exponentials.”
