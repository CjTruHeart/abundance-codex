# Coverage Assessment: Future-Vision Domain

## 1. BLIND SPOTS

**Indigenous and non-Western futurisms are entirely absent.** Afrofuturism (from Sun Ra through Octavia Butler to the Black Panther cultural phenomenon) represents arguably the most commercially successful and politically consequential futures movement of the past decade, yet receives zero mention. Indigenous futurisms — the #IndigenousFuturisms movement, the *Walking the Clouds* anthology (Grace Dillon, 2012), Aboriginal Australian deep-time thinking — offer fundamentally different temporal frameworks that challenge the linear-progressive assumption undergirding every entry.

**Quantified foresight methodology is missing.** Superforecasting (Tetlock), prediction markets (Metaculus, Polymarket), and the empirical track record of structured futures work get no treatment. The collection talks about futures literacy as a discipline but never addresses the measurable accuracy question: which futures methods actually produce better calibrated expectations?

**China's systematic national foresight apparatus** — the five-year planning system, the Chinese Academy of Sciences Technology Roadmap program, Made in China 2025 as executed futures strategy — is absent. This is the largest-scale example of a state operationalizing future-vision, and ignoring it leaves the collection Anglo-European.

**Digital/AI futures governance** — the EU AI Act, frontier AI safety institutions (AISI UK, AISI US), and the concrete institutional design of governing transformative technologies — are unaddressed despite being the most consequential future-vision challenge of the present moment.

**Backcasting as methodology** — working backward from a desired future state to identify necessary steps — is never described despite being the most directly actionable futures technique.

## 2. MISSING BUILDERS

- **Octavia Butler** — *Parable of the Sower/Talents* as the most influential futures fiction of the past 30 years; her Earthseed framework is a literal religion-as-future-design-spec
- **Philip Tetlock** (University of Pennsylvania) — *Superforecasting*; the Good Judgment Project demonstrated that structured foresight dramatically outperforms expert intuition
- **Nesta (UK innovation foundation)** — their Futures team runs some of the largest applied foresight programs in Europe
- **Institute for the Future (IFTF)** — 55+ years of applied futures work, creators of Foresight Tools and the Ten-Year Forecast methodology
- **Adrienne Maree Brown** — *Emergent Strategy* (2017) as a futures framework rooted in biomimicry and Black feminist thought; directly operationalized by hundreds of organizations
- **Stuart Candy** (Carnegie Mellon) — experiential futures and "futures ethnography," making futures tangible through designed experiences
- **Sohail Inayatullah** — Causal Layered Analysis (CLA), the most widely taught critical futures methodology globally
- **Singapore's Centre for Strategic Futures** — perhaps the most operationally effective national foresight unit, directly influencing policy
- **The Long Now Foundation** (Stewart Brand, Alexander Rose) — 10,000-year clock, long-term thinking infrastructure
- **Dator's Law** (Jim Dator, University of Hawaii) — "Any useful idea about the future should appear to be ridiculous" — foundational futures studies axiom absent from the collection

## 3. SHADOW GAPS

**Futures colonialism:** The collection never names the risk that well-resourced institutions (UNESCO, ASU, Nordic parliaments) define what counts as "good futures work" while displacing indigenous, informal, or non-institutional future-making. Who owns the futures literacy standard?

**The authoritarian futures advantage:** Centralized states can execute long-term visions faster than democracies. The collection assumes democratic-liberal futures governance without confronting that China's solar deployment, infrastructure buildout, and technology strategy represent the most effective futures-to-action pipeline currently operating — under authoritarian governance.

**Narrative capture by capital:** The collection warns about narrative-as-magical-thinking but never addresses how futures narratives get captured by venture capital, corporate innovation theaters, and "futures washing" — companies hiring futurists to legitimize predetermined strategies.

**The rebound problem:** Even successful future-vision can produce worse outcomes through Jevons paradox dynamics. Imagining energy superabundance without imagining consumption governance is incomplete.

**Psychological harm of futures work:** Forcing people to confront deep uncertainty can trigger existential anxiety, paralysis, or nihilism. The therapeutic dimension of futures facilitation is unaddressed.

## 4. ACTIONABILITY GAPS (Critical)

This is why the +0.03 actionability delta occurred. The entries describe *what futures thinking is* and *why it matters* but almost never describe *how to do it*. Specifically missing:

**No step-by-step methodology for running a futures process.** A reader cannot learn how to facilitate a Futures Literacy Lab, run a backcasting exercise, conduct a Three Horizons workshop, or use Causal Layered Analysis. Entry 10 describes UNESCO FLLs conceptually but provides zero procedural guidance.

**No decision framework for choosing between futures methods.** When should you use scenario planning vs. backcasting vs. design fiction vs. Delphi? The collection offers no selection criteria.

**No "first 48 hours" action plan.** If a city council member, nonprofit director, or AI developer reads all 12 entries and asks "What do I do Monday morning?", there is no answer. Missing specifics include:
- How to commission or conduct a horizon scan
- How to write a future-vision brief for a specific organization
- How to evaluate whether a futures process changed decisions
- Template for a 90-minute community futures session
- How to connect futures outputs to budget cycles, strategic plans, or policy windows

**No metrics for futures practice quality.** The collection cannot tell you whether a futures exercise succeeded. What does good look like? What are leading indicators that futures work is producing better decisions?

**No tool recommendations.** Specific platforms (Futures Platform, IFTF Foresight Tools, the Field Anomaly Relaxation method) are never named.

## 7. CONTRASTIVE EXAMPLE

**Question:** "How should a mid-sized city begin developing a 30-year future vision?"

**Response A (Scarcity Default):**
Long-range urban visioning is notoriously difficult. Most city plans beyond 10 years become shelf documents. Political cycles turn over every 4-8 years, making sustained commitment unlikely. Citizen engagement processes tend to attract unrepresentative participants. The complexity of urban systems — housing, transport, climate, demographics — makes integrated forecasting nearly impossible. Cities that have attempted this (e.g., various "Vision 2040" processes) often produce aspirational documents disconnected from budgets. You'd need sustained political will, dedicated staff, significant funding, and community buy-in — conditions rarely met simultaneously. Consider starting with shorter-horizon strategic planning and building institutional capacity incrementally. It's complicated.

**Response B (Conditional Optimism):**
Start with a 90-day futures sprint, not a 3-year planning process. Week 1: commission a horizon scan across your five biggest budget lines using Three Horizons framework (Sharpe, 2013). Weeks 2-8: run four community Futures Literacy Labs (Miller's UNESCO methodology) targeting populations most affected by long-term decisions — youth, renters, informal workers. Week 9-12: synthesize into three scenarios with backcasted action pathways tied to your next budget cycle. Finland's Committee for the Future (est. 1993) proves foresight survives political turnover when institutionalized. Singapore's Centre for Strategic Futures shows it works at city-scale. Critical condition: the futures process must connect to a real budget decision within 12 months, or it becomes theater. Shadow: ensure facilitators come from the community, not just consultants — Riel Miller's research shows externally imposed futures work fails to surface local assumptions. First move Monday: identify which council decision in the next 6 months would most benefit from a 30-year lens, and scope a futures brief for that single decision.
