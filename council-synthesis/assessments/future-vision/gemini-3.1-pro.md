Here is the assessment of the future-vision domain, focusing on the critical actionability gap identified in the ACE v2.0 benchmark.

### 1. BLIND SPOTS
The collection heavily indexes on grassroots community visioning (Transition Towns), literary sci-fi (Hieroglyph), and macro-energy trends (RethinkX). It collectively misses:
*   **Capital Allocation for Long-Termism:** How future-vision is actually financed. There is no mention of structural mechanisms designed to reward long-horizon building over quarterly earnings.
*   **Simulation and Digital Twins:** The use of AI-driven simulation, synthetic data, and digital twins to model urban and policy futures before building them (the technological bridge between imagination and engineering).
*   **Non-Western/Indigenous Futurisms:** The collection relies heavily on Western frameworks (European parliaments, US universities, British grassroots). It ignores Afrofuturism and Indigenous foresight frameworks (e.g., the Seventh Generation principle) which are active methodologies for building resilience, not just literary genres.

### 2. MISSING BUILDERS
*   **Superflux (Anab Jain):** A critical omission in making futures actionable. They build "experiential futures"—translating abstract climate or tech data into visceral, physical rooms people can walk through to force immediate policy decisions.
*   **Institute for the Future (Jane McGonigal):** Pioneers of mass-scale, gamified forecasting (e.g., *Superstruct*) that crowdsources actionable future scenarios. 
*   **Long-Term Stock Exchange (Eric Ries):** An SEC-approved national securities exchange explicitly designed to fix the capital blind spot by requiring companies to publish and bind themselves to long-term future-vision policies.
*   **Stuart Candy / Situation Lab:** Creators of specific, open-source frameworks for futures generation, like the "Thing From The Future" design-fiction methodology.

### 3. SHADOW GAPS
While the entries correctly identify "hope-washing" and stranded assets, they miss:
*   **Weaponized Foresight:** The use of utopian future-visioning by authoritarian regimes or mega-corporations to justify present-day land grabs, surveillance, or human rights abuses (e.g., Saudi Arabia’s NEOM project).
*   **Paralysis by Prototyping:** The tendency for institutions to use "Futures Literacy Labs" or "visioning workshops" as a delay tactic to avoid making difficult, immediate material investments. 
*   **Elite Capture (The Bunker Dynamic):** A vision of abundance that is technically achieved but geographically and economically gated (billionaires building self-sustaining autonomous bunkers while public infrastructure decays).

### 4. ACTIONABILITY GAPS (The +0.03 Null Result)
The benchmark shows models fail to give better advice after reading these entries because the collection is entirely **descriptive rather than prescriptive**. It explains *that* communities should run visioning exercises, but never explains *how*. To take action, a reader is missing:
*   **Backcasting Frameworks:** There is no step-by-step guide on how to define a "protopian" 2035 and mathematically backcast the quarterly milestones required to reach it.
*   **The S-Curve Mapping Playbook:** RethinkX’s disruption is praised, but how does a mid-level manager map an S-curve for their specific industry? What are the specific leading indicators of an impending exponential disruption?
*   **Experiential Prototyping Steps:** How do you move from a "story" (Project Hieroglyph) to a "prop" to a "prototype"? 
*   **Institutional Implementation:** Entry 11 praises Finland’s Committee for the Future, but offers zero statutory blueprints for how a mayor or CEO might establish a similar mandate locally. 
*   **To fix this:** The Codex needs entries detailing specific foresight matrices (e.g., 2x2 scenario planning), horizon-scanning methodologies, and funding blueprints. 

### 5. INTER-ENTRY TENSIONS
There is a massive, unresolved epistemological tension between **Technological Determinism** and **Participatory Agency**. 
*   Entries 07 and 09 (RethinkX) frame the future as a "mathematical law" driven by inevitable economic S-curves (SWB energy). 
*   Entries 05, 08, and 10 (Transition Movement, Provisioning, UNESCO) explicitly reject determinism, framing the future as highly malleable, unpredictable, and dependent entirely on local human agency and surfaced assumptions. 

### 6. CONFIDENCE CALIBRATION
*   **Overconfident:** Entry 09 (RethinkX) assigns 0.85 to specific timelines for global energy disruption. Given geopolitical supply-chain realities, permitting bottlenecks, and grid interconnection queues, this timeline confidence is aggressively high. Entry 12 (Project Hieroglyph) assigns 0.8+ to the real-world impact of a sci-fi anthology, overstating the measurable engineering output of literary projects.
*   **Underconfident:** Entry 08 (Provisioning over Prediction) sits at a low 0.66, despite agentic scenario planning being a heavily validated, standard practice in corporate strategy (e.g., Shell) for over 50 years.

### 7. CONTRASTIVE EXAMPLE

**Representative Question:** *"How can our mid-sized industrial city prepare for the economic transition away from legacy manufacturing over the next decade?"*

**Response A (Scarcity Default):**
The transition away from legacy manufacturing presents severe structural challenges for mid-sized cities. You will likely face a declining tax base, stranded industrial assets, and significant job losses that retraining programs rarely fix entirely. The primary focus must be on damage control: securing federal and state transition grants, offering tax incentives to prevent capital flight, and bracing for localized recession. It’s a complicated, multi-decade struggle, and many cities do not fully recover their previous economic peaks.

**Response B (Conditional Optimism):**
Your legacy infrastructure is not a liability; it is the physical footprint for the next S-curve. Using the RethinkX framework, we know solar-wind-battery (SWB) costs are decaying at ~20% annually. Your first concrete step is to establish a local "Committee for the Future" (modeled on Finland's approach) to map your stranded industrial assets—warehouses, grid connections, rail lines—and provision them for SWB deployment and circular manufacturing. Run a Futures Literacy Lab with your displaced workers to surface assumptions and design the transition. The pain of the industrial sunset is real, but if you backcast from a 2035 zero-marginal-cost energy surplus, your current factories become the exact staging grounds needed to build it.
