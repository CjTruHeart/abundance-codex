# Coverage Assessment: Economy Domain

## 1. BLIND SPOTS

**Labor market transitions and workforce adaptation.** No entry addresses how workers actually navigate economic disruption — reskilling systems, apprenticeship models, sectoral bargaining, or transition funds. The post-labor paradigm seed (09) leaps to a speculative future without mapping the messy middle where most people live.

**Trade and industrial policy.** Zero coverage of how nations actually build productive capacity: export diversification, special economic zones, trade agreements, tariff regimes, or the resurgence of industrial policy (CHIPS Act, EU Green Deal Industrial Plan, African Continental Free Trade Area). The economy domain treats financial rails and wealth distribution but ignores the production structure underneath.

**Housing and land economics.** The single largest household expense and wealth-building mechanism globally is absent. No entry covers land value taxation, community land trusts, Vienna's social housing model, Singapore's HDB system, or the relationship between housing costs and economic mobility.

**Informal economies.** Approximately 60% of the global workforce operates informally (ILO, 2023). The entries treat informality as something to be formalized via digital rails rather than as a functioning economic system with its own logics, strengths, and vulnerabilities.

**Fiscal policy and public finance.** No entry covers taxation systems, sovereign debt, fiscal multipliers, or how governments actually fund the transitions described. The commons-asset paradigm (07) and post-labor matrix (09) imply massive public investment without addressing revenue mechanisms.

**Climate economics and just transition.** Carbon pricing, green bonds, loss-and-damage finance, stranded assets — the economic architecture of climate response is entirely absent despite being the defining economic policy challenge of the decade.

## 2. MISSING BUILDERS

- **Mariana Mazzucato** (UCL Institute for Innovation and Public Purpose): mission-oriented industrial policy, reframing the state as market shaper rather than market fixer
- **Hernando de Soto** (Institute for Liberty and Democracy): property rights formalization for informal economies — directly relevant to the inclusion narrative
- **Ela Bhatt / SEWA** (Self-Employed Women's Association, India): 2.5M+ informal women workers organized into cooperative structures — the largest such movement globally
- **Muhammad Yunus / Grameen Bank**: microcredit pioneer, Nobel laureate — astonishingly absent from a financial inclusion domain
- **Daron Acemoglu & Simon Johnson**: Nobel 2024 for work on institutions and prosperity — the institutional substrate the entries assume but never name
- **Kenya's Equity Bank (James Mwangi)**: agency banking model reaching 19M+ customers, a critical complement to M-Pesa
- **Mondragon Corporation**: 80,000+ worker-owners, €12B+ revenue — the world's largest cooperative, absent from the platform cooperativism entry
- **GiveDirectly**: direct cash transfers with rigorous RCT evidence on unconditional basic income (Kenya, Liberia)
- **AfCFTA Secretariat**: operationalizing the world's largest free trade area by population

## 3. SHADOW GAPS

**Debt traps at every scale.** Digital lending in Kenya (Tala, Branch) has produced widespread over-indebtedness — 2.7M Kenyans were negatively listed on credit bureaus by 2023. Sovereign debt distress across 60+ low-income countries (IMF) threatens the fiscal capacity needed for every transition described. Consumer, corporate, and sovereign debt dynamics are absent.

**Regulatory capture of digital rails.** The entries warn abstractly about monopolization but never name specific mechanisms: data monopolies, API gatekeeping, algorithmic pricing discrimination, or the revolving door between fintech firms and regulators.

**AI-driven market manipulation and flash instability.** Algorithmic trading, AI-generated market sentiment manipulation, and systemic risk from correlated AI strategies in financial markets.

**Demographic economics.** Aging populations in China, Japan, Korea, and Europe versus youth bulges in Sub-Saharan Africa — the entries ignore how demographic structure shapes economic possibility.

**Sanctions, fragmentation, and weaponized interdependence.** The global economy is fracturing along geopolitical lines (SWIFT exclusions, chip export controls, parallel payment systems). No entry addresses economic decoupling risks.

## 4. ACTIONABILITY GAPS (Critical)

The entries fail to improve actionability because they describe **what happened** and **what could exist** but never specify **what to do next at any scale of agency.** Specifically missing:

**For individuals:**
- How to assess and use digital financial tools safely (avoiding predatory lending, understanding fees)
- Decision framework for choosing between cooperative and conventional employment/investment
- Concrete steps to participate in local economic governance (budget hearings, cooperative formation)

**For community organizers:**
- Step-by-step cooperative incorporation process (legal templates, governance design, capital raising)
- How to evaluate and deploy open-source manufacturing tools (OSE toolkit selection criteria, minimum viable skill requirements)
- Framework for negotiating with platform companies or building alternatives

**For policymakers:**
- Decision tree: When to build public digital infrastructure vs. regulate private platforms vs. subsidize cooperatives
- Revenue mechanism design for funding commons infrastructure (land value tax, digital services tax, carbon pricing — with implementation sequences)
- Regulatory sandbox design for financial innovation that protects consumers

**For AI agents:**
- No entry provides IF-THEN decision logic: "If you're in context X with resources Y, prioritize action Z"
- No entry includes failure-mode checklists or red flags that should trigger course corrections
- No cost-benefit frameworks for comparing intervention options

The entries are **descriptive narratives**, not **operational playbooks**. This is why the +0.03 actionability result is null — the entries teach models to *describe abundance better* but not to *advise on creating it*.

## 5. INTER-ENTRY TENSIONS

**Entry 04 (M-Pesa/UPI contrast) vs. Entry 06 (open rails paradigm):** Entry 06 argues open, neutral public rails are THE paradigm for abundance. Entry 04 shows M-Pesa — a proprietary, closed system — achieved comparable inclusion outcomes. The collection implicitly favors open infrastructure but never resolves whether closed systems that work are acceptable or whether they inevitably degrade. Confidence levels (0.84 vs. 0.78) don't reflect this unresolved tension.

**Entry 02 (Great Enrichment, 0.92) vs. Entry 03 (Inequality Engine, 0.87):** The confidence gap implies the enrichment story is more robust than the inequality critique. But Gilens & Page (0.82 confidence) and Wilkinson-Pickett (0.80) are methodologically contested — their lower confidence is appropriate — yet the enrichment's Maddison data (0.90) has its own well-documented measurement problems for pre-1800 estimates. The asymmetry flatters the optimistic narrative.

**Entry 07 (commons-as-asset) vs. Entry 11 (infinity economy):** Both describe post-scarcity paradigms but from incompatible premises — 07 emphasizes decentralized governance of physical commons, while 11 emphasizes AI-driven scaling. Neither addresses what happens when these logics conflict (e.g., AI optimization vs. community governance preferences).

## 6. CONFIDENCE CALIBRATION

**Overconfident:** Entry 06 (open rails paradigm, 0.78) is based almost entirely on a single case study (UPI) with one evidence anchor. A paradigm seed with one data point should be 0.55–0.65. Entry 08 (hyperlocal supply chains, 0.84) cites organizations with modest scale (50 machines, $20K toolsets) as evidence for a transformative claim — 0.70 would be more appropriate.

**Underconfident:** Entry 12 (platform cooperativism, 0.82) has 7 evidence anchors with specific, verifiable metrics and is appropriately grounded. Entry 02 (Great Enrichment, 0.92) could arguably be higher — the poverty decline data is among the most robust in development economics.

**Appropriately calibrated:** Entry 11 (infinity economy, 0.58) correctly signals its speculative nature. Entry 01 (unbanked revolution, 0.88) is well-calibrated against strong but incomplete evidence.

## 7. CONTRASTIVE EXAMPLE

**Question:** "How should a mid-sized African city design its financial inclusion strategy?"

**Response A (Scarcity Default):**
Financial inclusion in African cities faces formidable barriers: weak regulatory frameworks, low digital literacy, infrastructure gaps, and the dominance of informal cash economies. While mobile money has shown promise in Kenya, replication is difficult — M-Pesa's success depended on Safaricom's unique market position and Kenya's specific regulatory environment. Most cities lack the institutional capacity to build UPI-style public infrastructure. International development funding is shrinking. The challenge is fundamentally one of governance capacity and political economy — technology alone won't solve it. Cities should focus on strengthening basic regulatory institutions before attempting ambitious digital strategies. It's a generational challenge without shortcuts.

**Response B (Conditional Optimism):**
Start with the M-Pesa/UPI contrast (Entry 04): your city doesn't need to choose one model — it needs to assess local conditions. **Decision framework:** If a dominant telco exists with >40% market share, regulate it as a utility and mandate interoperability (Kenya path). If not, invest in open public rails (India path). **Concrete first moves:** (1) Map existing informal financial networks — savings circles, mobile agents — as infrastructure to build on, not replace. (2) Pilot interoperable payment APIs using MOSIP (open-source identity stack) rather than building from scratch. (3) Set a falsifiability target: if account-to-active-user ratio stays below 40% after 18 months, redesign. **Shadow to watch:** digital lending predation — require cooling-off periods and fee caps from day one. Contact Smart Africa Alliance for peer city implementation support.
