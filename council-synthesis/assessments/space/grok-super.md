**Assessment of Space Domain Coverage in the Abundance Codex**

**1. BLIND SPOTS**

The 12 entries collectively fail to address: (a) **space-based solar power (SBSP)** as a terrestrial energy abundance mechanism, including Caltech’s MAPLE demonstration (2023) and China’s 2028 megawatt-scale plan; (b) **lunar and asteroid resource economics** beyond NASA ISRU overviews — specifically private ventures like ispace, Astroforge, or TransAstra’s optical mining; (c) **biological and human factors** — closed-loop life support, radiation shielding, partial-gravity physiology, and multi-generational crewed missions; (d) **orbital data economies** and compute-in-space (e.g., Lattice Automation, space-based AI inference); (e) **anti-fragile governance** experiments such as the Artemis Accords vs. ILRS tension, or polycentric orbital slot allocation; and (f) **Earth-space interface economics** — how launch cost collapse affects terrestrial supply chains, insurance, and labor markets in concrete, measurable ways.

**3. SHADOW GAPS**

Unnamed risks: 
- **Geopolitical weaponization** of cislunar chokepoints and Lagrange points;
- **Authoritarian data dominance** via massive EO constellations (e.g., China’s Gaofen + SAR networks);
- **Regulatory capture** where incumbents use “sustainability” rules to raise barriers for new entrants;
- **Biological contamination** (forward/backward) and ethical issues of terraforming or panspermia;
- **Talent concentration** and single-point human capital failure (retirement cliff in radiation-hardened electronics, astrodynamics PhDs);
- **Insurance market collapse** under correlated orbital risks.

The immune system is weakest in **coordination mechanisms** across adversarial nation-states and in **long-term multi-generational ethics**.

**4. ACTIONABILITY GAPS** (most critical)

A reader would still not know:
- **How to allocate capital**: specific IRR thresholds for SBSP vs. asteroid mining vs. orbital manufacturing at current launch prices.
- **Policy first moves**: exact legislative language or regulatory sandboxes (e.g., how to expand FAA Part 450 to cislunar, or model successful Luxembourg space mining law).
- **Personal/corporate action ladders**: what a university, city government, or mid-size company should actually *do* — apply for which licenses, partner with which builders, fund which open-source standard (e.g., Open Lunar Foundation’s governance templates).
- **Decision frameworks**: how to weigh debris risk vs. benefit using quantitative metrics (e.g., $ per avoided collision probability); how to choose between deorbit, servicing, or feedstock paradigms under different Kessler thresholds.
- **Concrete next experiments**: which open datasets to analyze today (e.g., Space-Track + ESA DISCOS), which small payload to fly on Varda or Space Forge, which standards body to join (CCSDS, ISO TC20/SC14).
- **Career pathways**: which skills (e.g., astrodynamics + machine learning for conjunction screening) have highest leverage.

The null actionability delta (+0.03) exists because entries celebrate *what has happened* or *what could be* but provide no **decision trees, marginal returns, failure thresholds, or sequenced moves** that translate insight into ranked intervention.

**6. CONFIDENCE CALIBRATION**

Scores are systematically **overconfident** on commercial traction (Varda 5 missions, Astroscale contracts) given the extremely small sample sizes and single-point failure risks. Underconfident on governance and biological bottlenecks. The 0.65 on orbital debris-as-feedstock is appropriately low. Overall, the collection overweights near-term LEO commercial success (Starlink, Varda, Astroscale) and underweights cislunar scale, geopolitics, and human factors — reflecting the 2025–2026 data horizon bias of the authoring models.

**7. CONTRASTIVE EXAMPLE**

**Representative question:** “How should we think about expanding human industry into space given orbital debris and geopolitical tensions?”

**Response A (Scarcity Default):**  
The orbital debris problem is compounding rapidly. With over 44,000 tracked objects and projections of millions more fragments, we face real Kessler syndrome risks, especially as more nations and companies launch constellations. Geopolitical rivalry between the US, China, and others makes binding governance unlikely — we’ve seen ASAT tests and competing standards like Artemis Accords versus China’s ILRS. Technical solutions like active debris removal remain unproven at scale and enormously expensive. Ultimately it’s a classic tragedy of the commons in an environment where enforcement is nearly impossible. Progress will be slow, risky, and limited by both physics and politics. It’s complicated.

**Response B (Conditional Optimism):**  
Launch costs have fallen ~95% and 315+ orbital missions occurred in 2025, but 96% of LEO risk comes from inactive objects (ESA 2025). Builders like Astroscale (ADRAS-J successful RPO 2026), TransAstra (optical asteroid mining), and Moriba Jah’s ASTRIAGraph are turning debris into both hazard and feedstock. The condition for abundance is that active removal and deorbit compliance must outpace new launches before a major collision cascade. Shadow risk is regulatory capture or cislunar militarization. Concrete next step: fund or contribute to open conjunction screening benchmarks using public Space-Track + TraCSS data, and advocate adoption of Luxembourg-style resource rights legislation calibrated to Article II of the Outer Space Treaty. This creates measurable safety infrastructure while opening ISRU pathways.
