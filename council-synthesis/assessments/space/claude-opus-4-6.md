## Assessment of the Space Domain Collection: Gaps and Missing Elements

### 1. BLIND SPOTS

**Space-based solar power (SBSP):** No entry addresses the ESA SOLARIS program, JAXA's 2025 microwave power transmission demonstrations, or Caltech's MAPLE experiment (2023) that successfully beamed power from orbit. This is a direct abundance pathway — energy from space — completely absent.

**GPS/PNT as civilization infrastructure:** The collection mentions GPS once in passing. There is no entry on how 4+ billion devices depend on ~31 GPS satellites, how precision agriculture saves ~$20B/year through GNSS, or how PNT vulnerability (jamming, spoofing) represents a catastrophic single-point-of-failure shadow.

**Space weather and solar storm risk:** The Carrington-class event risk is unaddressed. A 2017 FEMA/NOAA study estimated a severe geomagnetic storm could cause $1-2 trillion in first-year damages. ESA's Vigil mission (launching ~2029) and NOAA's SWFO-L1 represent real mitigation infrastructure.

**Asteroid mining and planetary defense:** DART's successful kinetic impactor test (2022) and the upcoming Hera mission are absent. Near-Earth asteroid resource potential (e.g., 16 Psyche's estimated metal value) and the NEOWISE/NEO Surveyor pipeline are missing.

**Small satellite revolution beyond Starlink:** The CubeSat/SmallSat ecosystem — Planet Labs' 200+ Dove satellites providing daily global imagery, Spire Global's 100+ GNSS-RO weather satellites — is barely touched. These represent the actual democratization mechanism.

**Human spaceflight beyond ISS:** No entry covers the ISS deorbit timeline (2030-2031), commercial stations (Axiom, Vast, Orbital Reef), or China's Tiangong operations. The transition from government to commercial human presence in LEO is a major inflection point.

### 2. MISSING BUILDERS

- **Planet Labs** — operates the largest Earth-imaging constellation (~200 satellites), provides daily global coverage, and has an open data program for humanitarian use
- **Rocket Lab (Peter Beck)** — second most frequently launching US company, Electron/Neutron rockets, Photon spacecraft bus; critical mid-tier access provider
- **ISRO** — Chandrayaan-3's successful lunar landing (2023), Gaganyaan crewed program, and consistently low-cost missions ($74M for Mars Orbiter Mission)
- **Relativity Space** — 3D-printed rocket manufacturing, Terran R development
- **ClearSpace (ESA-funded)** — ClearSpace-1 mission for active debris removal, distinct approach from Astroscale
- **LeoLabs** — commercial space situational awareness radar network, critical infrastructure for debris tracking
- **Firefly Aerospace** — Alpha rocket operational, Blue Ghost lunar lander (delivered CLPS payload 2025)
- **ispace** — Japanese lunar lander company, HAKUTO-R Mission 2 underway
- **Capella Space / ICEYE** — SAR constellation operators providing all-weather, day-night Earth observation

### 3. SHADOW GAPS

**Spectrum congestion and interference:** Mega-constellations are consuming orbital spectrum allocations at unprecedented rates. ITU coordination is lagging. Radio astronomy faces existential interference threats — the SKA Observatory has documented Starlink-related RFI concerns.

**Astronomical light pollution:** The IAU's Centre for the Protection of the Dark and Quiet Sky has documented that Starlink satellites are visible in ~20% of Vera Rubin Observatory exposures during twilight. No entry addresses the scientific cost of constellation proliferation.

**Weaponization and dual-use escalation:** Beyond ASAT tests (mentioned in entry 03), the collection ignores the broader militarization trend: US Space Force budget exceeding $30B, China's co-orbital inspection satellites, Russia's Luch/Olymp GEO surveillance. The dual-use nature of RPO technology (entry 05) is flagged but not developed into a full shadow.

**Supply chain concentration risk:** The space industry depends on a handful of suppliers for radiation-hardened chips, solar cells, and propulsion components. The 2021-2023 chip shortage delayed multiple missions. Single-source dependencies (e.g., Aerojet Rocketdyne for certain propulsion systems) create fragility.

**Insurance market stress:** Space insurance premiums have risen sharply; the market is contracting as mega-constellation risk profiles challenge traditional underwriting models. This economic shadow could throttle commercial expansion.

**Regulatory capture and orbital enclosure:** First-mover mega-constellation operators may effectively "enclose" orbital shells through sheer numbers, creating de facto property rights in what is legally a global commons. The Outer Space Treaty's non-appropriation principle faces novel challenges.

### 4. ACTIONABILITY GAPS (Critical)

The collection explains *what is happening* but almost never tells anyone *what to do*. Specific missing guidance:

**For policymakers:** No entry provides a decision tree for national space strategy. A developing nation reading all 12 entries would not know: Should we build a launch capability or buy rides? Which orbital slots to file for? How to join COPUOS working groups? What the minimum viable national space program looks like (hint: a ground station network + spectrum allocation + one hosted payload costs ~$5-15M).

**For entrepreneurs:** No entry maps the value chain with entry points. Missing: Which segments have the lowest capital requirements? (Answer: ground segment software, data analytics, mission planning tools.) What does a minimum viable space startup look like? What are the actual regulatory steps to get an FCC Part 25 license or an FAA Part 450 license?

**For investors:** No framework for evaluating space ventures. Missing: unit economics benchmarks (cost per image, cost per GB downlinked, cost per kg to specific orbits), TAM/SAM/SOM for specific segments, typical milestone gates and capital requirements by stage.

**For educators/workforce:** No mapping of skills pipelines. What should someone study to enter the space economy? Which roles are growing fastest? (Answer: RF engineering, orbital mechanics, data science for EO, additive manufacturing.) Where are the training programs?

**For communities:** Entry 12 describes open EO data but never explains how a municipal government, farmer cooperative, or disaster response team actually *accesses and uses* Sentinel-2 or Landsat data. The concrete steps — register for Copernicus Data Space, use Google Earth Engine's free tier, apply for NASA DEVELOP internships — are absent.

**For civil society:** How does one participate in orbital governance? Comment on FCC spectrum proceedings? Engage with COPUOS through national delegations? Support dark-sky preservation?

The actionability gap explains the +0.03 null result directly: these entries are excellent analytical journalism but contain zero decision frameworks, no if-then guidance, no minimum viable action plans, and no resource directories.

### 5. INTER-ENTRY TENSIONS

**Entry 01 vs. Entry 02 on democratization:** Entry 01 claims launch costs collapsed, implying broad access. Entry 02 then reveals 88% of launches come from two countries and one company does more than the rest of the world combined. These entries don't reconcile whether cheap launch actually produces distributed access or hyper-concentration.

**Entry 03 vs. Entry 09 on debris framing:** Entry 03 treats debris as an existential crisis requiring urgent governance. Entry 09 reframes the same debris as "pre-refined metal stockpile" and potential feedstock. The tension between "this is an emergency" and "this is an opportunity" is never explicitly resolved. Entry 09's confidence (0.65) partially acknowledges this, but the collection doesn't grapple with the moral hazard: if debris is valuable, does that reduce urgency to prevent its creation?

**Entry 04 vs. Entry 08 on orbital manufacturing maturity:** Entry 04 (confidence 0.82) frames microgravity manufacturing as an emerging industry with $3.5B market by 2030. Entry 08 (confidence 0.83) acknowledges unit costs of ~$12M per mission needing a 24x reduction. These paint very different pictures of near-term viability.

**Entry 10 vs. Entry 06 on timeline:** Entry 10 (ISRU, confidence 0.74) is carefully hedged about lunar resource uncertainty. Entry 06 (cislunar commons, confidence 0.78) presupposes ISRU success as a building block for a speculative future. The speculation builds on foundations the evidence entry says are unproven.

### 6. CONFIDENCE CALIBRATION

**Overconfident:**
- Entry 02 at 0.91 claims the "monopoly of superpowers has given way to a commercial ecosystem" but its own data shows 88% US-China concentration. The essence overstates what the evidence supports. Should be ~0.83.
- Entry 06 at 0.78 is too high for a speculative entry whose key dependencies (ISRU, orbital manufacturing scaling, international governance cooperation) each have sub-0.80 confidence in their own dedicated entries.

**Underconfident:**
- Entry 12 at 0.87 is arguably too low — the Landsat and Copernicus data are among the most robustly documented public goods in the entire Codex. The evidence is strong, the trend is clear, the institutions are stable. Could be 0.91+.
- Entry 03 at 0.85 understates the certainty of the debris problem. The tracked object count, fragmentation events, and conjunction growth are among the best-documented trends in space. The *crisis* is high-confidence; only the *tipping point timing* is uncertain. Should be ~0.90 for the problem statement.

### 7. CONTRASTIVE EXAMPLE

**Question:** "Our country (population 30M, GDP ~$80B) wants to benefit from the space economy. What should we do?"

**Response A (Scarcity Default):**
Space programs require enormous capital investment and decades of expertise development. Launch infrastructure alone costs billions, and the market is dominated by SpaceX, which launches more than the rest of the world combined. Your country would face significant barriers: no launch sites, limited engineering talent, regulatory complexity, and competition from established space powers. Even building a single satellite requires specialized supply chains concentrated in a few nations. The space economy is growing, but participation requires capabilities most mid-income nations lack. You could purchase satellite services from existing providers, but becoming a meaningful space actor is extremely challenging. International cooperation frameworks exist but move slowly. It's a complicated landscape with no easy entry points.
