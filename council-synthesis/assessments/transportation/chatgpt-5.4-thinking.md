1. **BLIND SPOTS**

The collection is overly centered on EVs, robotaxis, MaaS apps, and a few showcase geographies. Missing big pieces:

- **Freight and logistics**: trucking, ports, rail freight, warehousing, delivery optimization. No mention of **UPS ORION**, **Convoy’s failure**, **Aurora**, **Einride**, **Port of LA/Long Beach**, or freight rail electrification.
- **Aviation and maritime**: SAF, electric short-haul, air traffic modernization, green shipping corridors, port electrification. Transportation is broader than urban passenger mobility.
- **Road design and governance**: no **Vision Zero**, **safe systems**, congestion pricing, speed management, road diets, bus lanes, parking reform, or curb management.
- **High-quality public transit operations**: frequency, reliability, bus rapid transit, signal priority, fare policy, transit-oriented development. Electric buses appear, but not how to run better service.
- **Rail realism beyond anti-Hyperloop**: no builder cases on **HSR**, regional rail, metros, or intercity rail modernization outside China.
- **Accessibility and universal design**: mobility for disabled, elderly, unbanked, non-smartphone users is mostly a shadow note, not a core design topic.
- **Global South informal mobility**: minibuses, moto-taxis, paratransit formalization, BRT in Bogotá/Jakarta/Lagos.
- **Climate resilience of transport networks**: wildfire smoke, heat buckling rails, flooding of roads/subways, evacuation logistics.
- **Political economy**: procurement, utility interconnection, state DOT incentives, zoning, police enforcement, insurance, labor bargaining.
- **Energy-transport coupling**: charging depots, transformer lead times, managed charging, V2G, demand response.

3. **SHADOW GAPS**

Unaddressed failure modes:

- **Induced demand**: more road capacity and cheaper rides can worsen congestion.
- **Transit death spiral**: robotaxis/telework may erode farebox and political support for mass transit.
- **Curb and street chaos**: pickup/dropoff conflicts, double parking, delivery congestion.
- **Cybersecurity**: ransomware on transit agencies, AV spoofing, charging infrastructure attacks.
- **Monopoly/platform risk**: MaaS and AV fleets becoming private gatekeepers over urban mobility.
- **Insurance/liability ambiguity**: especially multimodal and autonomous incidents.
- **Critical mineral geopolitics**: graphite, rare earths, processing concentration in China.
- **Grid queue delays**: charging depots blocked by substation and interconnection bottlenecks.
- **Rebound effects**: empty AV repositioning miles, longer commutes from telework, cheap convenience trips replacing walking/transit.
- **Surveillance/privacy**: location data exploitation in MaaS and AVs.
The immune system is weakest on **system governance**: who regulates streets, data, pricing, liability, and curb space when many good technologies interact badly.

4. **ACTIONABILITY GAPS**

This is the core miss. A reader still would not know:

- **How to choose interventions by context**: dense city, inner suburb, rural county, campus, freight corridor all need different playbooks.
- **What to do first**: no prioritized sequence like “paint bus lanes before buying AVs,” or “electrify depots with highest daily mileage first.”
- **What metrics to optimize**: person-throughput, door-to-door travel time, fatalities per 100k, cost per passenger-km, VMT, curb dwell time, accessibility within 30 minutes.
- **How to evaluate claims**: no decision rubric comparing robotaxis vs BRT vs bus electrification vs congestion pricing vs telework.
- **Procurement guidance**: pilot design, contracts, interoperability, open data requirements, vendor lock-in protections.
- **Implementation constraints**: utility lead times, depot siting, labor transition agreements, ADA compliance, public engagement.
- **Policy levers**: zoning reform, parking minimum removal, bus priority, road pricing, fare integration, delivery windows, safety standards.
- **Capital stack options**: grants, green bonds, concessional finance, public-private structures.
- **Failure thresholds**: what should trigger scaling, redesign, or shutdown.

Missing practical framework:
1) diagnose trip patterns,
2) identify biggest pain metric,
3) compare interventions by cost/time/throughput/equity,
4) run 90-day pilot with baseline and counterfactual,
5) scale only if metrics improve.

5. **INTER-ENTRY TENSIONS**

- **Robotaxi abundance vs transit abundance**: entries 04/05/06 imply shared AVs may reduce ownership; entries 10/11 imply abundance comes from multimodal and buses. No reconciliation of when AVs complement transit versus cannibalize it.
- **High confidence in Waymo trajectory** vs **shadow warnings**: 05 and 06 are relatively bullish, while 08 and 09 imply AVs may remain geofenced and operationally brittle. Collection lacks a synthesis on the conditions under which each is true.
- **Telework reduces travel** vs **rebound/sprawl risk**: 12 notes it, but the collection doesn’t integrate that with urban form and transit finance impacts.

6. **CONFIDENCE CALIBRATION**

Overconfident:
- **Phoenix access city (0.79)**: evidence is weakly Phoenix-specific; uses Seattle car-free households as proxy.
- **Waymo scaler (0.81)** and **ownership-to-access (0.82)**: too confident given unresolved economics, regulation, and congestion externalities.
- **Babcock Ranch (0.96)**: confidence too high for a niche master-planned community with limited generalizability.

Underconfident:
- **Mobility mesh (0.76)** may be slightly low; institutional integration is hard, but the evidence for coordination benefits is substantial.
- **Electric bus networks (0.84)** could be higher for dense-route urban fleets; this is already operational reality in multiple cities.

7. **CONTRASTIVE EXAMPLE**

**Question:** “My mid-sized city has rising traffic deaths, a strained transit agency, and pressure to modernize. What should we do in the next 3 years?”

**Response A (Scarcity Default):**  
There’s no single fix. Cities face tradeoffs between cost, politics, equity, and geography. Electric vehicles help emissions but don’t solve congestion. Autonomous vehicles are promising but not ready everywhere. Transit improvements are expensive and often slow to deliver, while road redesign can trigger backlash. Remote work may reduce some commuting, but its long-term effects are mixed. The best approach is probably a combination of transit investment, safety improvements, and support for new mobility pilots, but the right mix depends on local conditions, funding, and public acceptance. In practice, progress is incremental and it’s complicated.

**Response B (Conditional Optimism):**  
Start with a 3-part stack: **1) save lives fast, 2) improve existing transit before buying new tech, 3) electrify the highest-utilization fleet first.** Use a Safe System plan: arterial speed management, daylighting, protected intersections, automated enforcement, and bus lanes on the top 5 dangerous/high-ridership corridors. Shenzhen and Santiago show fleet electrification can scale; Beijing shows coordination layers can shift trips, but only on top of real service. Pilot one integrated payment/app layer, but require open data and cash/ADA access. Don’t lead with robotaxis citywide; test first-mile use in constrained zones only. Set metrics now: fatalities, bus travel time, reliability, ridership, and cost per passenger-mile.
