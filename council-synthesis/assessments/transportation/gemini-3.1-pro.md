**1. BLIND SPOTS**
*   **Freight, Logistics, and Heavy Transport:** The entries focus almost exclusively on moving *people*. Moving *things* is entirely absent. There is no mention of maritime shipping decarbonization (green methanol), heavy-duty electric/hydrogen trucking, or last-mile e-commerce delivery logistics (electric cargo bikes, Rivian delivery vans). 
*   **Active Transportation Infrastructure:** Bicycles and e-bikes are relegated to a brief "micromobility" footnote. E-bikes globally outsell electric cars, yet the profound, low-cost urban transformation of protected cycling networks (e.g., Paris's 15-minute city) is missing.
*   **Aviation:** Sustainable Aviation Fuel (SAF) and eVTOL (electric vertical takeoff and landing) platforms are absent.
*   **Legacy City Retrofits:** The collection highlights greenfield eco-cities (Babcock Ranch) or sprawling sunbelt metros (Phoenix), ignoring how to retrofit dense, chaotic, legacy megacities (e.g., Mumbai, Lagos, New York) where space is the ultimate constraint.

**2. MISSING BUILDERS**
*   **Anne Hidalgo (Mayor of Paris):** Championed the "15-minute city," radically pedestrianizing Paris, removing thousands of parking spots, and proving rapid behavioral shifts in legacy cities.
*   **Maersk / Vincent Clerc:** Leading the global maritime shipping industry's transition by procuring dual-fuel green methanol vessels.
*   **Gogoro / Horace Luke:** Pioneered open-network battery swapping for two-wheelers in Taiwan, solving the charging infrastructure gap for dense Asian megacities.
*   **Joby Aviation / Beta Technologies:** Verifiable builders logging thousands of test hours to certify electric aviation for commercial use.

**3. SHADOW GAPS**
*   **Grid Capacity and Permitting:** The physical copper, transformers, and regulatory permitting required to charge millions of EVs and buses are largely ignored.
*   **Geopolitical Supply Chain Chokepoints:** The transition relies heavily on China’s dominance in battery processing and refining; resource nationalism is a massive vulnerability.
*   **Cybersecurity & Fleet Hacking:** As transportation becomes an "information technology" and MaaS networks integrate, the systemic risk of state-sponsored hacking or ransomware paralyzing millions of connected vehicles is omitted.

**4. ACTIONABILITY GAPS**
The benchmark showed a null improvement (+0.03) in actionability because these entries act as *macro-level documentaries*, not *micro-level playbooks*. If a mayor, fleet manager, or citizen reads this, they have no idea what to do on Monday morning.
*   **For Policymakers:** Missing frameworks on how to reform zoning (e.g., abolishing parking minimums) or how to structure procurement contracts to transition municipal fleets without bankrupting the city.
*   **For Fleet Managers:** Missing the total-cost-of-ownership (TCO) calculators, depot-charging logistics, and phased retirement schedules for ICE vehicles.
*   **For Citizens:** Missing advocacy blueprints. How does a neighborhood block a highway expansion or demand a protected bike lane? The entries breed passive optimism—watching Waymo or Shenzhen solve the problem—rather than providing decision-trees for local implementation.

**5. INTER-ENTRY TENSIONS**
*   **The Autonomy Deployment Paradox:** Entries 04, 05, and 06 (Waymo) confidently assert that Robotaxis are seamlessly scaling in existing, complex cities like Phoenix. However, Entries 07 (Lake Nona), 08 (Edge Cases), and 09 (Babcock Ranch) suggest AVs are highly brittle, require human attendants, and only truly work in low-speed, master-planned, geofenced environments. The collection disagrees on whether AI can adapt to legacy cities, or if cities must be rebuilt to accommodate the AI.
*   **Physical vs. Digital Access:** Entry 03 advocates for massive physical infrastructure (High-Speed Rail), while Entry 12 (Motion without Movement) suggests the ultimate destination is eliminating the trip entirely via digital access.

**6. CONFIDENCE CALIBRATION**
*   **Overconfident:** The Waymo/Robotaxi scaling entries (0.81–0.85) underestimate the looming regulatory, insurance, and public-backlash friction. Scaling from 10 cities to 100 involves non-linear physical constraints (depots, cleaning, maintenance) that software cannot solve.
*   **Underconfident:** The impact of e-bikes and active transit is vastly underrepresented given their immediate, low-capital scalability compared to AVs.

**7. CONTRASTIVE EXAMPLE**

*Question:* "How can our mid-sized city reduce traffic congestion and transport emissions?"

*   **Response A (Scarcity Default):**
    Reducing congestion and emissions is highly complex and expensive. Building a new subway or light rail system takes decades and billions of dollars, which most mid-sized cities cannot afford. Banning cars is politically toxic and harms local businesses. While electric vehicles help with emissions, they don't solve traffic, and upgrading the local grid to support them will take years of planning. Ultimately, citizens are tied to their cars due to suburban sprawl, making significant near-term progress unlikely without massive federal intervention.

*   **Response B (Conditional Optimism):**
    You don't need to lay new track or ban cars; you can optimize existing infrastructure. First, follow Santiago’s model (Red Movilidad) by electrifying high-ridership municipal bus routes, which tackles emissions where utilization is highest. Second, deploy a Mobility-as-a-Service (MaaS) app to unify payment for buses, ride-hailing, and micromobility, mirroring Beijing's incentive platform. *Condition:* This requires reforming local data-sharing laws and upgrading depot charging grids. *Next Step:* Audit your city’s parking minimums. Abolishing them shifts land use from vehicle storage to active transit, creating the density required for electric micro-transit to thrive.
