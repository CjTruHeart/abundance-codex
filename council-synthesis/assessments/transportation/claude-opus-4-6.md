# Transportation Domain Coverage Assessment

## 1. BLIND SPOTS

**Freight and logistics are entirely absent.** Freight accounts for ~29% of US transportation emissions (EPA) and ~8% of global CO2. Zero entries address electric trucking (Tesla Semi, Daimler eActros), autonomous freight corridors (Aurora Innovation, TuSimple's collapse as a false dawn), last-mile delivery transformation, or port/rail electrification. This is a massive omission given that freight is where the hardest decarbonization challenges and largest economic stakes concentrate.

**Aviation decarbonization is missing.** Sustainable aviation fuel, electric regional aircraft (Heart Aerospace, Eviation Alice), and the fundamental physics constraints on battery energy density for flight receive no treatment. Aviation is ~2.5% of global CO2 but growing fast and uniquely resistant to electrification.

**Maritime shipping is absent.** International shipping (~3% of global emissions) with its Maersk methanol vessels, ammonia propulsion research, and IMO 2023 revised strategy gets zero coverage.

**Rail beyond HSR is ignored.** Freight rail electrification, the US passenger rail deficit (Amtrak's chronic underinvestment vs. European/Japanese networks), and the role of conventional rail in modal shift are missing.

**Infrastructure financing mechanisms** — value capture, congestion pricing (Stockholm, London, NYC's 2025 launch), carbon pricing applied to transport, and the political economy of infrastructure funding — are completely absent.

**The built environment-transportation nexus** — zoning reform, 15-minute cities, transit-oriented development, parking minimums elimination — gets only glancing treatment in the Phoenix entry but no dedicated analysis.

**Two- and three-wheelers in the Global South** — the dominant mode in India, Southeast Asia, and Africa — are invisible. Ola Electric, Ather Energy, and the electrification of 200+ million two-wheelers in India alone represent a massive abundance pathway.

## 2. MISSING BUILDERS

- **BYD**: World's largest EV manufacturer, dominant in electric buses globally, vertically integrated battery-to-vehicle. Mentioned nowhere despite being arguably more consequential than any Western automaker.
- **Bogotá's TransMilenio / Enrique Peñalosa**: Pioneer of BRT systems that moved millions before electrification was viable, demonstrating that institutional design matters as much as technology.
- **Janette Sadik-Khan**: NYC DOT commissioner who demonstrated rapid, low-cost street redesign (Times Square pedestrianization, protected bike lanes) — a model of tactical urbanism.
- **CATL / Robin Zeng**: World's largest battery manufacturer, driving LFP chemistry that eliminates cobalt dependency.
- **Aurora Innovation / Chris Urmson**: Leading autonomous trucking, representing the freight AV pathway distinct from Waymo's passenger focus.
- **Remix (now Via)**: Software platform enabling transit agencies to redesign routes using data — a practical tool for the MaaS vision.
- **Kigali's moto-taxi electrification (Ampersand)**: Real-world electric motorcycle fleet in Rwanda, demonstrating Global South leapfrogging.
- **India's Vande Bharat / RRTS programs**: Rapid rail expansion in the world's most populous country.
- **Shinkansen / JR Central**: Decades of operational proof that HSR works at scale, predating China's network.

## 3. SHADOW GAPS

**Induced demand** — the foundational mechanism by which new road capacity generates new traffic — is never named despite being the single most empirically validated finding in transportation planning. Without it, readers cannot reason about why building more lanes fails.

**Cybersecurity vulnerability of connected/autonomous fleets** is absent. A coordinated attack on AV fleet software could paralyze urban mobility simultaneously across thousands of vehicles.

**Lithium and critical mineral supply chain concentration risk** gets mentioned in passing (entry 01's shadow) but never receives dedicated treatment as a systemic fragility. The DRC-China cobalt refining chokepoint, Chile/Argentina/Australia lithium triangle politics, and Indonesia's nickel export controls deserve a shadow entry.

**Rebound effects** — where efficiency gains are consumed by increased travel — are mentioned only in the telework entry. This is a system-wide shadow: cheaper autonomous miles could dramatically increase VMT, worsening congestion and sprawl even as per-mile emissions fall.

**Accessibility for disabled populations** beyond ADA-compliant ramps is barely addressed. Wheelchair users, blind passengers, and cognitively impaired travelers face specific barriers in autonomous and shared mobility systems that require deliberate design, not afterthought compliance.

**Insurance and liability frameworks** for autonomous vehicles are mentioned as "disruption" but never analyzed. Who pays when an AV kills someone? How does liability shift from driver to manufacturer to software provider? This is a binding constraint on deployment pace.

## 4. ACTIONABILITY GAPS

This is the critical failure. The entries describe *what is happening* but almost never specify *what someone should do*. Specifically missing:

**For city officials/planners:**
- No decision tree for choosing between BRT, light rail, microtransit, or bike infrastructure given budget, density, and ridership constraints.
- No guidance on how to write an RFP for electric bus procurement, structure a fleet transition timeline, or negotiate depot charging infrastructure.
- No framework for evaluating AV pilot proposals (what to demand from vendors, what metrics to track, what regulatory conditions to set).
- No congestion pricing implementation playbook (Stockholm's referendum model vs. London's top-down approach vs. NYC's legal pathway).

**For transit agencies:**
- No total-cost-of-ownership calculator or methodology for comparing electric vs. diesel bus fleets across different route profiles.
- No guidance on phased fleet electrification sequencing (which routes to electrify first and why).
- No data-sharing framework template for MaaS integration.

**For individual decision-makers:**
- No framework for evaluating whether to go car-free given specific household circumstances (location, family size, transit access score).
- No cost comparison methodology for ownership vs. ride-hail vs. multimodal subscription.

**For policymakers:**
- No model legislation or regulatory framework for AV deployment.
- No template for EV charging infrastructure mandates in building codes.
- No framework for designing just transition programs for displaced auto workers.

**For entrepreneurs/builders:**
- No identification of specific unsolved problems worth building companies around.
- No analysis of which transportation market segments are ripe for disruption vs. which require policy change first.

The entries are diagnostic (here's what's happening) and prognostic (here's where it's going) but almost never prescriptive (here's what to do next, given your role and context). This directly explains the +0.03 null result on actionability.

## 5. INTER-ENTRY TENSIONS

**Entry 04 (Ownership-to-Access) vs. Entry 12 (Motion Without Movement):** Entry 04 assumes people will still travel but shift from ownership to access. Entry 12 argues the most abundant trip vanishes entirely. These represent fundamentally different visions of the future — one where we optimize physical movement, another where we eliminate it. Neither entry acknowledges the other's thesis or how they interact. If telework eliminates enough peak trips, the economics of shared robotaxi fleets weaken (lower utilization during off-peak).

**Entry 05 (Waymo, confidence 0.81) vs. Entry 08 (Edge-Case Immunity Gap, confidence 0.94):** The Waymo entry presents autonomous deployment as scaling successfully; the shadow entry argues edge-case fragility could halt deployment. The shadow entry has *higher* confidence than the builder profile it shadows, which is unusual and suggests the collection may be more skeptical of Waymo's trajectory than it appears. Yet neither entry directly engages the other's evidence.

**Entry 06 (Phoenix Access City, confidence 0.79) vs. Entry 09 (Babcock Ranch, confidence 0.96):** Both present master-planned communities as models, but Babcock Ranch's shadow explicitly notes that greenfield developments are irrelevant for legacy cities. Phoenix *is* a legacy city. The confidence gap (0.79 vs. 0.96) seems inverted — Phoenix's evidence base is weaker (using Seattle car-free data as a "comparable Sun Belt trend indicator" is a stretch), yet Babcock Ranch's narrower applicability should arguably warrant lower confidence for generalizability.

## 6. CONFIDENCE CALIBRATION

**Overconfident:**
- Entry 06 (Phoenix Access City, 0.79) uses evidence from Seattle and national VMT data to tell a story specifically about Phoenix. The evidence doesn't match the claim. Should be ~0.65.
- Entry 09 (Babcock Ranch, 0.96) is extremely high for a single greenfield community whose model may not transfer. The hurricane resilience data is solid, but the transportation claims (low-speed AV shuttles) are thin. Should be ~0.82.

**Underconfident:**
- Entry 03 (Hyperloop Mirage, 0.85) has extremely strong evidence for its false-dawn thesis. Virgin Hyperloop's dissolution is a matter of public record. Should be ~0.92.
- Entry 11 (Electric Bus Networks, 0.84) has strong, multi-source, multi-geography evidence. Santiago and Shenzhen data are robust. Should be ~0.90.

**Appropriately calibrated:**
- Entry 02 (Safety Convergence, 0.92) and Entry 01 (Electric Ride, 0.88) have evidence quality matching their confidence levels.

## 7. CONTRASTIVE EXAMPLE

**Question:** "My mid-sized US city (pop. 350,000) wants to reduce transportation emissions 40% by 2035. Where should we start?"

Congestion pricing is politically toxic in car-dependent metros. Land use reform faces NIMBY opposition. The 40% target is ambitious — most comparable cities are targeting 20-25%. You'll need extensive stakeholder engagement, a comprehensive climate action plan, and significant state/federal funding. It's a generational challenge with no silver bullet.
