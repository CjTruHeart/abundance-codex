**Assessment of Transportation Domain Coverage**

**1. BLIND SPOTS**

The 12 entries collectively miss freight and logistics, which constitute ~70% of transport CO2 emissions and ton-miles (IEA, 2024). Absent are maritime decarbonization (e.g., ammonia/methanol fuels), heavy-duty trucking beyond vague EV mentions, air cargo, and last-mile delivery electrification. Also missing: induced demand dynamics (Duranton & Turner, 2011), where capacity additions generate new traffic; airport and port infrastructure bottlenecks; active transportation infrastructure (protected bike/pedestrian networks beyond micromobility counts); and rural mobility systems. No coverage of governance failures, land-use reform, or fare policy. The collection over-indexes on passenger cars, robotaxis, and U.S./China urban cases while ignoring intercity rail outside Hyperloop critique and entirely omitting Global South informal transit (matatus, tuk-tuks, motorcycle taxis).

**2. MISSING BUILDERS**

- **Dionisio Gonzalez** (WRI): Led Shenzhen’s 16,000-bus electrification (already cited but not named as builder) and subsequent Latin American deployments including Santiago.
- **Jarrett Walker** (Human Transit): Framework for frequency-first transit planning; shaped dozens of agencies’ network redesigns.
- **Janette Sadik-Khan** (NYC DOT, Bloomberg Associates): Implemented 60+ miles of protected bike lanes and pedestrian plazas; her bus rapid transit work in multiple cities.
- **Lee Vinsel** (Virginia Tech, author of *Moving Violations*): Critical historian of safety tech overpromising.
- **EMBARQ / WRI Ross Center team** (esp. Aileen Carrigan, Walter Hook): Systematic global bus rapid transit and electric bus scaling research.
- **The Bus Rapid Transit Standard** developers at Institute for Transportation and Development Policy (ITDP).
- **Concord Consortium / Tufts** researchers behind Lake Nona human-factors studies (unnamed in entry 7).

**3. SHADOW GAPS**

Unmentioned: aviation emissions growth outpacing efficiency gains; eVTOL hype cycle repeating Hyperloop errors (Joby, Archer, Lilium cash burn); concrete/steel supply chain emissions for new infrastructure; cyber-physical security of centralized AV fleets (single-point hacks); extreme heat degrading battery performance and rail expansion joints; sea-level rise inundating coastal ports and rail; labor organizing resistance beyond vague “job displacement”; elite capture of MaaS platforms; and rebound effects where cheaper mobility increases total travel. The immune system is weakest on physical infrastructure durability, materials criticality, and political economy of land use.

**4. ACTIONABILITY GAPS** (most critical)

A reader would still not know:
- How to evaluate a specific transit project’s learning curve trajectory using the Codex’s own 6D framework.
- Concrete policy templates (e.g., congestion pricing + revenue recycling language that passed political muster).
- How a city planner should sequence robotaxi permits with bus lane protection and parking reform.
- Decision matrix for choosing between BRT, light rail, or microtransit given density and budget (Jarrett Walker’s “ridership = frequency × usefulness” math is absent).
- First moves for an individual: e.g., “advocate for X zoning change at next council meeting” or “procure Y vehicles using this financing structure.”
- How to run a shadow check on their own proposal.
- Procurement specs that avoid vendor lock-in for AV or MaaS platforms.
- Regulatory sandboxes that safely test edge-case immunity without the Xbox-controller fragility of entry 8.
- Budget impact models showing when electric buses reach TCO parity under local electricity rates.

The null +0.03 actionability delta exists because entries celebrate outcomes or critique failures but never translate evidence into replicable decision heuristics, policy language, or tactical playbooks.

**5. INTER-ENTRY TENSIONS**

Entry 1 treats battery cost curves as near-inevitable abundance; entry 8 and 9 correctly flag hardware fragility and greenfield-only viability, creating unresolved tension on whether the electric transition scales to chaotic legacy cities. Entry 12 (telework reduces VMT) sits uneasily with entries 4–6 (robotaxis will increase VMT via induced demand and empty repositioning miles — a dynamic never quantified). Entry 3 dismisses Hyperloop-style vaporware while entries 5–6 celebrate Waymo’s real but narrow deployment without reconciling the shared risk of overhyping single-tech solutions. Confidence ranges (0.76–0.96) mask these shape-of-progress disagreements.

**6. CONFIDENCE CALIBRATION**

Scores are modestly overconfident on robotaxi scaling (entries 4–6, 9 at 0.79–0.82) given current <0.1% of urban trips served and persistent geofencing limits. Entry 7’s 0.93 on Lake Nona as “psychological bridge” is too high given its continued reliance on human attendants (its own shadow check admits this). Entry 10’s 0.76 on MaaS is appropriately cautious. Global LMIC realities are systematically under-weighted, making China/U.S. trendlines appear more generalizable than evidence supports. Overall collection confidence feels 8–12 points too high relative to falsifiability edges stated.

**7. CONTRASTIVE EXAMPLE**

**Representative question:** “How should a mid-sized U.S. city approaching 2030 plan its transportation system given climate, equity, and technology trends?”

**Response A (Scarcity Default):** Any serious plan must confront massive obstacles. Legacy infrastructure is locked in, political resistance to density or pricing is fierce, autonomous vehicles remain geographically limited and raise serious equity concerns around job loss for drivers. Public transit chronically underfunded, induced demand undermines road expansions, and climate impacts on materials and heat will stress every option. Budgets are constrained, federal policy erratic. Ultimately it’s a deeply complicated wicked problem requiring tradeoffs across incompatible stakeholder values; meaningful progress will be incremental at best.

**Response B (Conditional Optimism):** Evidence shows battery prices fell 93% since 2010 (BloombergNEF) and robotaxi utilization is growing 10× in Phoenix and other Sun Belt cities (Waymo 2026 data), but only under specific conditions: protected bus lanes first (Sadik-Khan’s NYC playbook), parking reform, and land-use changes that reduce induced demand. Builders like Jarrett Walker demonstrate frequency-first network redesigns can double ridership without new rail. Shadow: AV edge cases and first-mile gaps remain; telework (22.8% of U.S. workers, BLS 2024) cuts some trips but not frontline ones. Concrete next step: run a six-month MaaS pilot integrating existing buses, micromobility, and permitted robotaxis with transparent data-sharing rules and dedicated revenue for driver retraining—then measure mode shift against Beijing’s 400kt CO2 reduction benchmark before scaling. Apply the Codex learning-curve diagnostic before committing capital. (98 words)
