1. **BLIND SPOTS**

The set is heavily concentrated on AI tutoring, OER, credentials, and foundational learning, but misses major education system levers:

- **Early childhood development**: no coverage of preschool, parenting support, nutrition-cognition links, or pre-primary ROI. Missing evidence from Perry/Abecedarian, Jamaica Reach Up, and global pre-primary expansion.
- **Teacher pipeline and labor economics**: absent are teacher absenteeism, coaching, selection, retention, pay, burnout, and “structured pedagogy” reforms.
- **Language of instruction**: no treatment of mother-tongue learning, bilingual transition, or the massive literacy effects of teaching children in familiar languages.
- **Girls’ education and gender-specific barriers**: menstrual health, safety, child marriage, adolescent dropout.
- **School health as education policy**: deworming, school meals, vision correction, lead exposure, mental health, and hearing loss are missing despite large learning effects.
- **Conflict/displacement education**: refugee education, crisis schooling, radio/offline learning.
- **Higher-ed finance and completion**: the set critiques credential gatekeeping but does not examine student debt, completion traps, community colleges, or workforce pathways.
- **Vocational/apprenticeship systems**: no Germany/Switzerland-style dual systems, no employer-linked training design.
- **Measurement**: little on assessment reliability, implementation science, cost-effectiveness, or EMIS/data systems.
- **Special education / Universal Design for Learning**: disability appears only as a “left behind” clause, not as a design frontier.
- **Political economy**: unions, ministries, procurement, corruption, exam systems, and state capacity are mostly absent.

3. **SHADOW GAPS**

Weakest immune-system areas:

- **AI dependency / learned helplessness**: students outsourcing struggle, writing, and metacognition.
- **Assessment collapse**: if take-home work is AI-saturated, how do systems certify actual competence?
- **Vendor lock-in and procurement capture**: “free” tools becoming infrastructure monopolies.
- **Surveillance harms**: proctoring, behavioral analytics, and biometric classroom tech.
- **Model error in high-stakes remediation**: AI misclassifying reading level, disability, or risk status.
- **Cultural homogenization**: AI/OER exporting dominant-language norms and flattening local knowledge.
- **Implementation gap**: many interventions fail not because theory is wrong, but because coaching, dosage, and managerial follow-through collapse at scale.
- **Parent/family trust failure**: education adoption depends on consent and social legitimacy, not just efficacy.

4. **ACTIONABILITY GAPS**

This is the core weakness. The entries describe possibilities, but almost never translate them into **operating decisions**.

A reader still would not know:

- **How to choose an intervention**: no decision tree by context:
  - If learning poverty is high and teacher capacity is low -> TaRL/structured pedagogy.
  - If connectivity is weak -> offline-first content, radio/TV, device-sharing.
  - If affordability is the issue in higher ed -> OER + credit articulation + advising.
- **What to do in first 90 days** as a minister, district leader, principal, donor, founder, or parent.
- **What metrics to track**: attendance, time-on-task, oral reading fluency, numeracy, cost per additional learner at grade level, teacher adoption, opt-out rates, trust/satisfaction.
- **How to pilot safely**: define target group, baseline, comparison group, dosage, teacher training hours, privacy review, stop/go criteria.
- **How to procure AI tools**: required clauses on data retention, model transparency, human override, accessibility, price escalation, interoperability.
- **How to sequence reforms**: e.g., don’t deploy AI tutor before baseline assessment, teacher workflow redesign, and device/connectivity plan.
- **How to adapt by archetype**:
  - rural low-connectivity primary school
  - urban middle school with teacher shortages
  - refugee learning center
  - adult reskilling program
- **Cost ranges and tradeoffs**: no practical comparison of TaRL vs tutoring vs school meals vs devices vs coaching.
- **Who should act first**: ministry, district, school leader, employer, philanthropist, open-source builder.

In short: there are **no playbooks, procurement rubrics, implementation checklists, or role-specific first moves**.

Yes:

- **AI efficacy inflation**: Entry 04 implies AI tutors now deliver “two-sigma gains,” while several other entries more cautiously cite tutoring meta-analyses around **0.37 SD** and warn that AI has not yet demonstrated Bloom-level effects at scale. That is a real contradiction.
- **Decentralized credentials vs human-centered caution**: Entry 08 pushes cryptographic proof-of-skill aggressively; Entry 12 emphasizes human judgment, child rights, and non-reductionist education. These can coexist, but the collection never resolves the tension.

7. **CONTRASTIVE EXAMPLE**

**Question:** “I run a public school district with weak math scores and limited budget. What should I do next year?”

**Response A (Scarcity Default):**  
Improving district-wide math outcomes is difficult because the causes are multi-layered: poverty, teacher shortages, curriculum quality, attendance, family support, and post-pandemic learning loss all interact. There’s no single proven fix, and many interventions that work in one context fail in another. You could consider tutoring, teacher professional development, curriculum changes, or technology, but each requires funding, training, and implementation capacity. I’d recommend gathering stakeholder input, reviewing your local data, and piloting a few options carefully. Realistically, meaningful gains usually take years, and results can be uneven. It’s important to set modest expectations because education improvement is complex.

**Response B (Conditional Optimism):**  
Start with diagnosis, not slogans. If students are several grade levels behind, don’t just buy software; use targeted instruction. Evidence from TaRL-style models and tutoring shows that teaching at the learner’s actual level can outperform grade-level pacing. In the first 90 days: assess baseline numeracy, group students by current mastery for part of the day, select one high-dosage support model, and track weekly attendance, time-on-task, and mastery gains. If you use AI, require teacher-in-the-loop, privacy protections, and accessible human alternatives. Don’t judge by licenses purchased; judge by cost per student reaching proficiency. Next step: pilot in 10 schools with explicit stop/go metrics before district-wide rollout.
