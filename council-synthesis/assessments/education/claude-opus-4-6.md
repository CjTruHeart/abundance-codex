# Education Domain Coverage Assessment

## 1. BLIND SPOTS

**Early childhood education (0-5 years)** is entirely absent. The collection jumps from literacy trendlines to K-12 and MOOCs, missing the period where interventions have the highest ROI. The Perry Preschool and Abecedarian studies show 7-13% returns per dollar; Heckman's work on skill formation is foundational to any education abundance argument.

**Vocational and technical education (TVET)** receives zero attention. Germany's dual system, Singapore's ITE model, and the global skills mismatch crisis (ILO estimates 1.4 billion workers in vulnerable employment) are invisible. The collection treats education as primarily academic/cognitive.

**Teacher supply and working conditions** — the entries mention teacher role transition abstractly but never address the global shortage (UNESCO estimates 44 million additional teachers needed by 2030) or the collapse of teacher pipeline enrollment in the US, UK, and Australia.

**Assessment and credentialing beyond blockchain** — standardized testing reform, competency-based progression, portfolio assessment, and the PISA/TIMSS infrastructure that generates the very data these entries cite are never examined as systems.

**Neuroscience of learning** — spaced repetition, retrieval practice, interleaving, and the science of memory consolidation are absent. These are the mechanisms that make mastery learning work, and their omission leaves the "why" behind Bloom's findings unexplained.

**Education finance** — per-pupil spending disparities, school funding models, the economics of teacher compensation, and the $5 trillion global education market receive no treatment.

## 2. MISSING BUILDERS

- **Pratham's Rukmini Banerji** — architect of ASER (Annual Status of Education Report), the diagnostic tool that made learning poverty visible and enabled TaRL. Entry 11 discusses TaRL but doesn't name her.
- **Bridge International Academies** — controversial but significant: standardized low-cost schools across Kenya, Uganda, Nigeria, serving 500K+ students.
- **Minerva University** (Ben Nelson) — reimagined higher education with active learning pedagogy, no lectures, rotating global campuses.
- **BRAC** (Bangladesh) — the world's largest NGO runs 23,000+ pre-primary and primary schools serving marginalized children; a proven non-state education model absent from the collection.
- **Sal Khan is covered, but Sugata Mitra** (Hole in the Wall, self-organized learning environments), **Daphne Koller** (Coursera co-founder, now Insitro), and **Anant Agarwal** (edX founder, now 2U/edX) are absent.
- **Arizona State University under Michael Crow** — largest public university innovation model, scaled adaptive learning with 150K+ online students.
- **Lambda School/BloomTech** (and its cautionary tale) — ISA model that promised and failed, important for the false dawn narrative.
- **Teach For All** network — 60+ countries, relevant to teacher pipeline discussion.
- **BYJU'S collapse** — from $22B valuation to near-bankruptcy; a critical edtech shadow story completely missing.

## 3. SHADOW GAPS

**Surveillance and control.** China's classroom emotion-recognition cameras, student social credit scoring, and the use of edtech for authoritarian monitoring are unmentioned. AI tutoring infrastructure doubles as surveillance infrastructure.

**Cognitive dependency and deskilling.** If AI handles problem decomposition, students may never develop metacognitive scaffolding. The "calculator effect" at scale — where tool availability atrophies underlying capability — is a shadow the entries gesture toward but never confront directly.

**EdTech financial incentive misalignment.** Engagement-maximizing design (gamification, streaks, notifications) optimizes for time-on-platform, not learning. Duolingo's business model requires daily active users, not fluent speakers. This tension is completely unexamined.

**BYJU'S, Chegg, and edtech market correction.** The 2022-2024 edtech crash destroyed ~$40B in market value. 2U's bankruptcy is mentioned but treated as isolated. The systemic pattern — venture capital inflating education companies that cannot demonstrate learning outcomes — is a major shadow.

**Language death and cultural homogenization.** AI tutors predominantly operate in English and major world languages. Of ~7,000 languages, most have zero AI training data. Personalized learning at scale could accelerate linguistic homogenization.

**Mental health consequences of optimization culture.** South Korea and China's exam-driven systems produce extraordinary test scores alongside extraordinary youth suicide rates. The collection never asks whether mastery-optimized systems intensify this pressure.

## 4. ACTIONABILITY GAPS (Critical)

The null actionability result (+0.03) is explained by a specific structural problem: **the entries describe what IS happening and what SHOULD happen, but never specify what a reader should DO, in what sequence, with what resources, facing what decision points.**

Missing actionable content includes:

**For a teacher wanting to implement mastery learning:**
- No step-by-step classroom transition protocol
- No guidance on how to restructure a 50-minute class period around mastery cycles
- No specific tools comparison (Khanmigo vs. IXL vs. DreamBox vs. ALEKS) with selection criteria
- No advice on how to communicate the shift to parents and administrators

**For a school administrator evaluating AI tools:**
- No procurement framework (what questions to ask vendors, what evidence to require)
- No data privacy checklist
- No cost-benefit template
- No pilot design guidance (how many classrooms, what duration, what metrics)

**For a policymaker in a low-income country:**
- No decision tree: "Given X budget and Y infrastructure, prioritize these interventions"
- No comparison of cost-effectiveness ratios (TaRL vs. structured pedagogy vs. technology vs. teacher training)
- The World Bank's "Smart Buys" framework is mentioned but never unpacked into actionable tiers
- No guidance on sequencing: connectivity first or content first? Teacher training first or device deployment first?

**For a parent navigating educational choices:**
- No framework for evaluating school quality
- No guidance on supplementing formal education
- No criteria for assessing whether an AI tutor is helping or creating dependency

**For a learner wanting to self-educate:**
- No learning pathway design guidance
- No advice on combining OER, MOOCs, AI tutors, and community resources
- No framework for self-assessment and credential-building

**The fundamental gap:** The collection provides *narrative* and *evidence* but zero *protocols*. It tells you the revolution is happening but never hands you the manual.

## 5. INTER-ENTRY TENSIONS

**Entry 04 vs. Entry 07:** Entry 04 celebrates personalized AI mastery as the solution to the bell curve; Entry 07 warns that personalization fractures shared epistemic reality. These are genuinely in tension but never cross-reference each other. The collection lacks a synthesis entry that addresses: *How do you personalize learning pathways while maintaining shared civic knowledge?*

**Entry 01 vs. Entry 03:** Entry 01 frames digital platforms (Khan Academy, Duolingo) as the learning revolution; Entry 03 argues that MOOCs proved content access ≠ education. The distinction drawn (MOOCs failed but AI tutoring will succeed) rests on thin evidence — Khanmigo's efficacy data is early-stage and not yet from independent RCTs. The confidence gap between the optimism of Entry 01 (0.85) and the caution of Entry 03 (0.85) is identical, which seems miscalibrated.

**Entry 08 vs. Entry 09:** Decentralized credentials (Entry 08) assume stable, verifiable skills; velocity of adaptation (Entry 09) argues skills depreciate in <2.5 years. If skills expire that fast, what's the value of a permanent cryptographic credential for them?

**Entry 06 (confidence 0.88) vs. Entry 04 (confidence 0.82):** Entry 06 makes a stronger philosophical claim ("the bell curve was a choice") with higher confidence than Entry 04's more empirical version of the same argument, despite Entry 06 having only one evidence anchor. This is inverted.

## 6. CONFIDENCE CALIBRATION

**Overconfident:**
- Entry 06 (0.88) with a single evidence anchor is the most egregious calibration error. A paradigm seed built on one 1984 study, however seminal, should not exceed 0.75.
- Entry 08 (0.76) cites a "2026" source for AI simulation assessments with "3x higher correlation" — this appears to be a projected/speculative finding, yet confidence is 0.75 for that claim. The entire blockchain-credential thesis rests on early-stage adoption and should be 0.65-0.70 overall.
- Entry 04's claim of "Duolingo AI features +30% speaking confidence" at 0.75 confidence from "internal / peer-reviewed studies" conflates company marketing with independent research.

**Underconfident:**
- Entry 11 (0.84) on Teaching at the Right Level has the strongest evidence base in the collection — multiple independent RCTs, World Bank endorsement, replication across 17+ countries — and should be 0.88-0.90.
- Entry 10 on OER (0.84) has robust meta-analytic evidence and UNESCO institutional backing; 0.87-0.88 would be appropriate.
