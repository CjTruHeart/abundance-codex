# Governance Domain Coverage Assessment

## 1. BLIND SPOTS

**Fiscal governance and public financial management** are entirely absent. No entry addresses tax system design, public procurement reform, budget transparency (e.g., the Open Budget Survey showing only 14 of 120 countries provide adequate budget oversight), or sovereign debt governance — despite these being the primary mechanisms through which governance actually allocates resources.

**Judicial independence and rule of law** receive zero dedicated treatment. Courts are the enforcement backbone of every other governance innovation described, yet the collection has no entry on judicial reform, legal aid access, or the erosion of judicial independence in countries like Poland, Hungary, and Israel.

**Subnational and municipal governance** — where most governance actually happens — is mentioned only in passing (participatory budgeting statistics). No entry covers metropolitan governance, federalism design, or the specific dynamics of city-level innovation (where ~200 cities now have chief innovation officers implementing governance experiments).

**Anti-corruption infrastructure** beyond regulatory capture is missing. No mention of beneficial ownership registries, asset declaration systems, whistleblower protection frameworks, or the FATF mutual evaluation process. The Pandora Papers, FinCEN Files, and their institutional aftermath are absent.

**Conflict and post-conflict governance** — how governance is rebuilt after state failure — is entirely unaddressed. No entry on transitional justice, peace agreements, DDR programs, or the governance challenges in fragile states that house ~1.9 billion people.

**AI governance specifically** — not AI-generated code governance (entry 07) but the governance of AI systems themselves — is barely touched. No entry on the EU AI Act, NIST AI Risk Management Framework, or the global regulatory fragmentation problem.

## 3. SHADOW GAPS

**Digital authoritarianism** as a coherent governance model is unexamined. China's social credit system, surveillance exports to 80+ countries (Carnegie's AI Global Surveillance Index), and the emergence of "authoritarian tech" as a governance export product represent a shadow the collection doesn't name.

**Information ecosystem collapse** as a governance threat: no entry addresses how misinformation, deepfakes, and platform manipulation undermine the epistemic foundations governance requires. Deliberative democracy entries assume good-faith information environments without addressing what happens when they're poisoned.

**Elite state capture beyond lobbying**: oligarchic governance where wealth directly controls state institutions (not just influences regulators) — visible in Russia, parts of Latin America, and increasingly in democracies where billionaires directly hold office or fund judicial appointments.

**Governance of migration**: 281 million international migrants, climate displacement accelerating, and no entry addresses how governance systems handle population movement — one of the most politically destabilizing governance challenges globally.

**Institutional decay through defunding**: the deliberate strategy of starving public institutions of resources to manufacture failure (IRS enforcement budget cuts, EPA staffing reductions, USPS operational degradation) — a shadow distinct from regulatory capture.

## 4. ACTIONABILITY GAPS — THE CRITICAL FAILURE

This is why the +0.03 actionability result occurred. The entries describe *what exists* and *what could exist* but almost never specify *how to get from here to there*. Specifically:

**No entry provides a decision tree for selecting governance interventions.** A mayor, civic technologist, or funder reading all 12 entries cannot determine: "Given my context (population size, existing institutions, digital infrastructure, political constraints), which governance innovation should I pursue first?" There's no triage framework.

**No sequencing guidance.** Estonia digitized, Taiwan built vTaiwan, Ireland ran assemblies — but in what order should a reformer act? The entries never address: Do you build digital infrastructure before participatory platforms? Do you need transparency before participation? What are the prerequisites?

**No failure-mode playbook.** The shadow checks identify risks but never say: "When you encounter X failure pattern, do Y." For example: "When a citizens' assembly recommendation is ignored by the legislature, the next step is [specific escalation mechanism]." This operational guidance is completely absent.

**No cost estimates or resource requirements.** How much does it cost to run a citizens' assembly? (Roughly €500K-€2M for a national process.) What's the minimum viable team for deploying a civic tech platform? What's the timeline from decision to implementation? These practical parameters are never stated.

**No stakeholder mapping guidance.** Who must be persuaded, neutralized, or bypassed to implement each reform? The entries mention "transition pain" abstractly but never map the specific political economy: "Regulatory capture reform requires X coalition, faces opposition from Y, and the leverage point is Z."

**Missing: "First 90 days" playbook.** If someone is appointed to lead governance reform in a city, state, or country, what are the concrete first moves? The entries provide no prioritized action sequences.

**Missing: Measurement frameworks.** How do you know if your governance reform is working? What leading indicators should you track? The entries cite outcome metrics but never specify monitoring protocols for practitioners.

## 5. INTER-ENTRY TENSIONS

**Entry 09 (Forking Society) vs. Entry 10 (Polycentric Commons):** Entry 09 envisions "permissionless branching" into parallel governance realities, while Entry 10's Ostrom framework requires *bounded* communities with clear membership, shared monitoring, and graduated sanctions. Forking dissolves the boundaries that commons governance requires. These frameworks are in genuine tension about whether governance should be exit-based or voice-based.

**Entry 06 (Star Trek Spec, 0.72 confidence) vs. Entry 02 (Democratic Expansion, 0.90 confidence):** The speculative entry imagines "zero coercion and maximum wisdom" governance, while the trendline entry documents that 74% of humanity lives under autocracy and democratic recession has lasted 20 years. The spec doesn't engage with the trendline's evidence about how difficult governance transformation actually is.

**Entry 12 (DAOs, 0.74) vs. Entry 03 (Regulatory Capture, 0.87):** DAOs are presented as a governance framework, but the regulatory capture entry's logic applies directly to DAOs — whale concentration IS capture. The DAO entry acknowledges this in its shadow check but doesn't resolve the tension: if capture is structural, why would tokenized governance escape it?

**Entry 01 (Estonia) vs. Entry 08 (Civic Provenance Ledger):** Estonia's X-Road is a centralized interoperability layer; the Civic Provenance Ledger implies decentralized, cryptographic transparency. These are architecturally different visions that the collection doesn't reconcile.

## 6. CONFIDENCE CALIBRATION

**Overconfident:** Entry 07 (Algorithmic Bureaucracy Shadow) at 0.90 is too high — its evidence base is narrow (OSS code review) and the generalization to all governance coordination is a significant inferential leap. Entry 08 (Civic Provenance Ledger) at 0.88 is overconfident given that its evidence comes almost entirely from software supply chain contexts, not civic governance.

**Underconfident:** Entry 11 (Global Citizens' Assemblies) at 0.82 is arguably too low — it has the strongest empirical base (716 OECD cases, 80,000+ participants, 66 research studies) and the most robust cross-national evidence of any entry in the collection.

**Appropriately calibrated:** Entry 06 (Star Trek Spec) at 0.72 correctly reflects its speculative nature. Entry 02 (Democratic Expansion) at 0.90 is well-calibrated given its reliance on Freedom House and V-Dem data.

## 7. CONTRASTIVE EXAMPLE

**Question:** "Our city of 400,000 has declining trust in local government and low voter turnout. What governance reforms should we prioritize?"

**Response A (Scarcity Default):**
Declining civic trust is a widespread challenge with deep structural roots. Research shows trust erosion correlates with inequality, media fragmentation, and perceived government ineffectiveness. Some cities have experimented with participatory budgeting or digital engagement platforms, but evidence on their effectiveness is mixed. Citizens' assemblies show promise in some European contexts but face questions about scalability and whether recommendations are actually implemented. The fundamental challenge is that trust is easier to destroy than rebuild, and no single intervention reliably reverses the trend. Any reform effort must contend with political resistance, resource constraints, and the risk that superficial participation exercises could deepen cynicism. A comprehensive diagnostic of your specific trust drivers would be the essential first step.

**Response B (Conditional Optimism):**
Start with a sequenced approach grounded in evidence. **Month 1-3:** Commission an independent trust diagnostic — the Edelman framework shows 61% report grievance, but your city's specific drivers matter. **Month 3-6:** Launch a citizens' assembly on one contested local issue (OECD data: 716 cases across 28 countries show knowledge gains and depolarization; budget ~€200K-500K for a municipal process). Critical condition: commit publicly *before* the assembly to a binding response timeline — Germann et al. (2024) found legitimacy gains depend on visible follow-through. **Parallel track:** Deploy a Pol.is-style platform for ongoing issue identification (Taiwan's model achieved 80%+ resolution rates on contested issues). **Shadow to watch:** Ensure assembly recruitment compensates participants adequately and provides childcare/accessibility — weak compensation excludes exactly the populations
