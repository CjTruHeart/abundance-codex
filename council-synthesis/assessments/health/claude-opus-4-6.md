# Health Domain Coverage Assessment: Gap Analysis

## 1. BLIND SPOTS

**Maternal and reproductive health** is entirely absent despite being foundational to health systems — no mention of the 287,000 annual maternal deaths, the transformative impact of misoprostol/oxytocin access, or the political contestation of reproductive autonomy that shapes health policy globally.

**Cardiovascular disease** kills 17.9 million people annually — the leading cause of death worldwide — yet receives zero dedicated entries. Hypertension management, polypill strategies (the WHO HEARTS initiative), and the statin access revolution are invisible.

**Environmental and occupational health** — air pollution causes 6.7 million deaths/year (WHO 2024). No entry addresses the health-environment nexus: lead exposure, PFAS contamination, heat-related mortality from climate change, or indoor air quality in LMICs.

**Aging and neurodegeneration** — dementia affects 55 million people, projected to reach 139 million by 2050. No entry covers Alzheimer's disease biology, lecanemab/donanemab debates, or the caregiving economy. The entire longevity science field (senolytics, epigenetic clocks, Altos Labs, caloric restriction mimetics) is missing.

**Oral health** affects 3.5 billion people globally and is among the most prevalent conditions on Earth, yet is entirely absent — a classic "invisible" health domain.

**Health financing and universal health coverage** — no entry addresses the mechanics of how health systems are paid for. UHC, out-of-pocket catastrophic spending (pushing 100 million into poverty annually), or insurance design are absent.

**Nutrition and food systems** — stunting affects 148 million children under 5. Micronutrient deficiency, biofortification (HarvestPlus), and the double burden of malnutrition get no treatment.

- **Atul Gawande** — the Checklist Manifesto's impact on surgical safety; now leading Ariadne Labs' work on systems improvement.
- **Raj Panjabi / Last Mile Health** — built Liberia's national CHW program; former head of the President's Malaria Initiative. Directly relevant to entry 12.

- **FIND (Foundation for Innovative New Diagnostics)** — critical to diagnostic access in LMICs, directly relevant to entry 03.

- **Thailand's NHSO** — achieved near-universal coverage at ~$200/capita/year; a replicable model absent from the collection.
- **Eric Topol** — leading voice on AI in medicine, author of *Deep Medicine*; conspicuously absent from entry 03.
- **Demis Hassabis / DeepMind's AlphaFold** — protein structure prediction transforming drug discovery; no entry covers this.

## 3. SHADOW GAPS

**Medical misinformation and trust erosion** — the entries mention anti-vaccine sentiment in passing but never confront the systematic collapse of institutional health trust. The infodemic is a structural threat to every intervention described.

**Regulatory capture and corruption** — no entry addresses how pharmaceutical lobbying, regulatory revolving doors, or counterfeit medicines (estimated 10% of medicines in LMICs are substandard/falsified per WHO) undermine health gains.

**Data colonialism in health AI** — entries 03 and 07 note bias but miss the deeper dynamic: health data extracted from African and South Asian populations training models that generate profits in wealthy nations, with no benefit-sharing.

**Iatrogenic harm and overtreatment** — the collection is almost entirely about expanding access and capability, never about the shadow of medical excess. Overdiagnosis, unnecessary procedures, and the opioid crisis (which killed 80,000+ Americans in 2023) are absent.

**Climate-health nexus as threat multiplier** — heat stress, vector-borne disease range expansion, food system disruption, and health infrastructure vulnerability to extreme weather events receive no coverage.

**Workforce mental health** — physician/nurse burnout and suicide rates are absent despite being a core constraint on every health system described.

## 4. ACTIONABILITY GAPS (Critical)

This is where the collection most clearly fails, explaining the +0.03 null result on actionability:

**No decision frameworks for individuals.** A person reading all 12 entries cannot answer: "What should I do about my health?" There is no entry on evidence-based personal health optimization — screening schedules, exercise dose-response, sleep hygiene, when to seek care. The collection is entirely structural/systemic.

**No implementation playbooks.** Entry 12 describes CHW systems abstractly but never specifies: How do you start one? What's the minimum viable team? What supervision ratio works? What does the first 90 days look like? Compare this to the specificity of, say, the Living Goods or Medic Mobile implementation guides.

**No policy action menus.** A health minister reading these entries gets no prioritized action list. Missing: "If you have $10M for health, here's the evidence-ranked allocation" — the kind of framework Disease Control Priorities (DCP3) provides.

**No procurement or supply chain guidance.** Multiple entries flag distribution as the bottleneck, but none describe how to solve it. No mention of pooled procurement (PAHO Revolving Fund), Gavi's market-shaping, or cold-chain innovations (Arktek, controlled-temperature chain).

**No advocacy or coalition-building tactics.** How do you build political will for AMR stewardship? How do you counter vaccine misinformation? How do you get mental health funded? The entries diagnose problems without prescribing civic action.

**No personal risk-assessment tools.** No entry helps someone evaluate: Should I take a GLP-1? Am I a candidate for mRNA cancer vaccines? How do I interpret an AI diagnostic result? The gap between "this technology exists" and "here's how to access and evaluate it for your situation" is vast.

**Missing: "If you can do one thing" directives.** Each entry should end with tiered action steps — for individuals, clinicians, policymakers, funders, and technologists. None do.

## 5. INTER-ENTRY TENSIONS

**Entries 01 and 05 vs. Entry 10:** The mRNA entries project an expanding platform solving infectious disease, while entry 10 documents a shrinking antibiotic pipeline and growing resistance. These exist in tension — mRNA vaccines prevent some infections, but AMR threatens the entire surgical/chemotherapy infrastructure that mRNA cancer vaccines depend on. A patient receiving myeloablative conditioning for Casgevy (entry 04) is profoundly vulnerable to resistant infections (entry 10). This interdependence is never made explicit.

**Entry 03 (AI diagnostics, 0.82) vs. Entry 07 (sovereign health agent, 0.82):** These overlap substantially but frame the same phenomenon differently — entry 03 as diagnostic tool augmentation, entry 07 as autonomous agent replacement. Entry 03 explicitly says "AI does not replace doctors"; entry 07 frames medical expertise as "infinitely scalable synthetic utility." These are meaningfully different claims about the human role.

**Entry 06 (GLP-1s, 0.84) vs. Entry 12 (CHW framework, 0.85):** GLP-1s represent high-cost pharmaceutical intervention requiring specialty pharmacy infrastructure; CHW systems represent low-cost task-shifting. Both claim to address health abundance but through fundamentally incompatible resource assumptions. No entry reconciles when each approach is appropriate.

**Entry 02's confidence (0.90) vs. its own data:** The entry documents the convergence slowing (child mortality ARR dropping from 3.9% to 1.5%) and COVID erasing 1.7 years of gains in one year. A 0.90 confidence for a trendline that's demonstrably decelerating seems miscalibrated.

## 6. CONFIDENCE CALIBRATION

**Overconfident:**
- Entry 07 (sovereign health agent, 0.82): The evidence anchors are weak — a commercial voice-bot deployment, a bias paper, and two lab techniques (CODEX, metagenomics) that belong to other entries. The "breakthrough" claim rests on thin clinical evidence for autonomous health agents actually improving outcomes.
- Entry 04 (sickle cell, 0.76): Paradoxically, this may be *under*confident on the cure claim (93.5% crisis-free is extraordinary) but the confidence doesn't capture the access chasm — only ~50 patients treated commercially by mid-2025 against 300,000 annual births.
- Entry 05 (BioNTech, 0.79): Claims 15 Phase 3 trials by end-2026 at 0.92 confidence based on a company investor announcement — corporate pipeline projections routinely slip.

**Underconfident:**
- Entry 11 (Friendship Bench, 0.84): The RCT evidence is strong (JAMA publication, large effect sizes, replication across settings). This deserves higher confidence for the core intervention claim, even if scale claims are uncertain.

## 7. CONTRASTIVE EXAMPLE

**Question:** "What can a district health officer in rural Kenya do to reduce preventable deaths in the next 12 months?"

**Response A (Scarcity Default):**
This is an enormously complex challenge shaped by resource constraints, workforce shortages, supply chain fragility, and competing disease burdens. Kenya's health system faces chronic underfunding — county health budgets often fall below the Abuja Declaration's 15% target. Rural facilities lack diagnostics, essential medicines, and trained staff. While community health volunteers exist, they're largely unpaid and poorly supervised. Addressing preventable deaths requires systemic reform: better financing, workforce investment, infrastructure, and political commitment. International evidence suggests integrated primary care strengthens outcomes, but implementation depends on context-specific factors that resist generalization. The honest answer is that meaningful change requires sustained multi-year investment at levels not currently available, and any short-term intervention risks being a band-aid on structural wounds.
