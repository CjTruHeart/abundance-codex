**1. BLIND SPOTS**
The collection heavily indexes on wilderness recovery, top-down policy, and digital sensing, but completely ignores **urban ecology and green infrastructure** (e.g., sponge cities, urban canopy equity). It misses the **circular economy and waste processing**—there is no mention of plastic pollution remediation, e-waste mining, or materials recycling. Furthermore, while it covers freshwater (Klamath, Yangtze) and coastal (mangroves, sea turtles) ecosystems, it is blind to **deep-sea ecosystems and ocean acidification**, as well as the **soil microbiome and regenerative agriculture** (precision agriculture is mentioned in 09, but soil carbon sequestration is absent). Finally, the physical land-use footprint of the renewable energy transition (mining for transition minerals) is ignored.

**2. MISSING BUILDERS**
The entries lack specific, verifiable actors doing ground-level ecosystem engineering outside of the Mangrove Action Project. Missing builders include:
*   **Tony Rinaudo & World Vision:** Pioneers of Farmer-Managed Natural Regeneration (FMNR), which has re-greened millions of hectares in the Sahel without planting new trees.
*   **Enric Sala & Pristine Seas:** Instrumental in creating 27 of the largest marine reserves worldwide, protecting over 6.5 million square km of ocean.
*   **Gabe Brown / Kiss the Ground:** Leading the transition of conventional US farmland to regenerative models that restore soil hydrology and carbon.
*   **Coral Vita (Gator Halpern & Sam Teicher):** Scaling micro-fragmentation technology to grow climate-resilient coral up to 50x faster than nature.

**3. SHADOW GAPS**
The collection fails to name **Green Colonialism**—the risk that decarbonizing the Global North drives catastrophic biodiversity loss in the Global South through lithium, cobalt, and copper mining. It also misses the **Jevons Paradox in agriculture**: as precision agriculture makes farming cheaper and more efficient (Entry 09), operators may expand their footprint rather than sparing land, negating the "dematerialization" benefit. Finally, it ignores **invasive species dynamics**; restoring river connectivity (Entry 04) or altering habitats can inadvertently accelerate the spread of invasive predators or weeds, destroying native recovery.

**4. ACTIONABILITY GAPS (The +0.03 Null Result)**
The entries read like a macro-historical dashboard. They tell a reader *that* the Yangtze recovered or *that* eDNA exists, but offer zero operational frameworks. To take action, a reader still lacks:
*   **Corporate Procurement Frameworks:** Entry 03 tears down Verra offsets, but doesn't tell a sustainability officer *what to buy instead* (e.g., how to vet high-permanence biochar or direct air capture credits).
*   **Local Playbooks:** Entry 04 highlights dam removal, but fails to outline the civic and legal steps a local coalition must take to petition for barrier removal, secure funding, or navigate FERC (Federal Energy Regulatory Commission) relicensing. 
*   **Citizen Science Bridges:** Entries 07 and 11 praise GBIF and eDNA, but don't tell a citizen how to collect a water sample for eDNA testing, or how a local NGO can use MethaneSAT data to legally compel a local fossil fuel operator to plug a leak.
*   **Transition Financing:** Entry 05 praises the Yangtze fishing ban but offers no economic blueprint for how a municipality actually funds the retraining and universal basic income required for displaced extractive workers. 

**5. INTER-ENTRY TENSIONS**
There is a massive structural tension between **Entry 03 (Carbon offset shadow)** and **Entry 12 (Mangrove builders)**. Entry 12 champions mangrove restoration, but coastal "blue carbon" projects are overwhelmingly financed by the exact voluntary carbon markets that Entry 03 declares structurally broken. If the voluntary market collapses, the mangrove builders lose their primary funding engine. Furthermore, **Entry 09** claims agricultural land use has peaked, but **Entry 01** relies on RethinkX’s highly speculative projection that 80% of land *could* be freed by alt-proteins—blurring the line between current reality and future theory.
