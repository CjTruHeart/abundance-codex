**1. BLIND SPOTS**
The collection heavily indexes on supply-side deep tech (desalination, nanomaterials, AWG) and macro-infrastructure (Singapore, NRW), but fundamentally ignores **agricultural demand reduction**. Agriculture consumes ~70% of global freshwater, yet there are zero entries dedicated to precision irrigation, drought-resistant crop genetics, or controlled-environment agriculture (greenhouses/vertical farms). Furthermore, **Nature-Based Solutions (NBS)**—like watershed restoration, riparian buffers, and "Sponge City" urban design—are entirely absent. The collection treats water as a fluid to be engineered in pipes and membranes, ignoring the ecological infrastructure that captures and filters it for free. 

**2. MISSING BUILDERS**
*   **Kongjian Yu / Turenscape:** Pioneered the "Sponge City" concept in China, redesigning urban landscapes to absorb and filter floodwater rather than funneling it into concrete channels.
*   **Netafim / Orbia:** The commercial pioneers of drip irrigation, fundamentally shifting the agricultural yield-per-drop equation globally.
*   **Evidence Action (Dispensers for Safe Water):** While Water.org is mentioned, Evidence Action’s hyper-scalable, low-tech point-of-use chlorination model currently provides safe water to over 50 million people daily, representing a massive omission in decentralized rural solutions.
*   **Imagine H2O:** The leading water innovation accelerator bridging the gap between deep-tech prototypes and municipal procurement.

**3. SHADOW GAPS**
*   **The Jevons Paradox in Agriculture:** Making irrigation more efficient often leads farmers to switch to thirstier, higher-value crops or expand their acreage, ultimately *increasing* total basin depletion.
*   **Cybersecurity Vulnerabilities:** As water systems digitize (smart grids, automated RO plants, OpenET), they become prime targets for state-sponsored cyberattacks or ransomware, a critical fragility of "smart" abundance.
*   **The Energy-Water Death Spiral:** Desalination and AWG are highly energy-intensive. If the local grid relies on hydropower (vulnerable to drought) or thermal plants (which require massive cooling water), a drought can simultaneously crash both the power grid and the new high-tech water supply.

**4. ACTIONABILITY GAPS**
The benchmark showed a null result (+0.03) for actionability because these entries are written for *spectators*, not *operators*. They describe billion-dollar macro-projects (Sorek B, PUB Singapore) and Nobel-winning chemistry, but offer zero procurement or financing playbooks. 
If a mid-sized utility manager or local policymaker reads this, they still do not know:
*   **How to finance the transition:** Where are the frameworks for municipal green bonds, public-private partnerships (PPPs), or performance-based contracts for NRW reduction?
*   **How to navigate procurement:** Municipalities are notoriously risk-averse. How does a city successfully pilot and scale a new biomimetic membrane without violating legacy procurement laws?
*   **How to structure tariffs:** How do you price water to recover the high CAPEX of a wastewater reuse plant while maintaining subsidized lifeline rates for low-income residents? 
*   *Concrete missing step:* A "Water Audit First-Move Framework" detailing how a municipality or corporation should baseline their non-revenue water and price their externalities before buying new tech.

**5. INTER-ENTRY TENSIONS**
*   **Centralization vs. Decentralization:** Entry 05 (PUB Singapore) and Entry 06 (Wastewater Reuse) champion hyper-centralized, heavily governed, closed-loop utility models. Conversely, Entry 02 (AWG) and Entry 09 (Molecular filtration) suggest a future of decentralized, off-grid water independence. The collection does not reconcile whether the future of water abundance is a massive public utility or a consumer appliance.
*   **Demand vs. Supply:** Entry 07 claims the US has successfully decoupled GDP from water use (demand reduction), while Entries 01 and 06 celebrate massive exponential growth in new production capacity. 

**6. CONFIDENCE CALIBRATION**
*   **Overconfident:** Entry 09 (Molecular filtration) at 0.85 is too high. Graphene and biomimetic membranes frequently fail at commercial scale due to biofouling and durability issues; treating atomic transport as an imminent commercial reality ignores the "valley of death" in membrane scale-up.
*   **Underconfident:** Entry 10 (Non-revenue water) at 0.84 could be higher. The physics and economics of leak detection (acoustic sensors, district metered areas) are deeply proven and highly predictable compared to experimental deep tech.
