# Water Domain Gap Assessment

## 1. BLIND SPOTS

**Agricultural water governance and allocation reform.** Agriculture consumes ~70% of freshwater withdrawals (acknowledged in entry 12) but no entry addresses the political economy of agricultural water rights, subsidized irrigation pricing, or the specific mechanisms by which water markets, tiered pricing, or rights reform could reallocate water. The entries treat demand-side agriculture as a footnote rather than the dominant variable.

**Flood-drought whiplash and stormwater capture.** Climate change is producing intensifying oscillation between extreme precipitation and drought. No entry addresses stormwater harvesting, green infrastructure, urban sponge city design, or the emerging practice of treating floods as supply opportunities rather than pure hazards. China's Sponge City program (~$12B invested across 30 pilot cities) is entirely absent.

**Transboundary water governance.** 153 countries share transboundary river basins. No entry addresses hydro-diplomacy, treaty frameworks (Nile Basin Initiative, Mekong River Commission, Indus Waters Treaty), or the escalating geopolitical risks of upstream dam construction. This is the domain where water scarcity becomes conflict.

**PFAS and emerging contaminant remediation at scale.** Entry 09 mentions PFAS filtration in passing, but the regulatory, health, and remediation challenge of forever chemicals, microplastics, pharmaceutical residues, and agricultural runoff contaminating existing freshwater supplies is unaddressed as a systemic threat.

**Indigenous and traditional water management.** Qanat systems, fog-catching (e.g., Morocco's Dar Si Hmad), community-managed watersheds (India's Tarun Bharat Sangh), and Indigenous water rights frameworks are absent. These represent millennia of proven practice.

**Water-energy nexus quantification.** Entries mention energy costs of desalination but never systematically address that water systems consume 4% of global electricity, or that energy production itself is water-intensive. The bidirectional dependency is unexamined.

## 2. MISSING BUILDERS

- **Wim van der Hoek / IWMI (International Water Management Institute):** Pioneering research on water productivity in agriculture, safe wastewater reuse in farming, and groundwater governance across South Asia and Africa.
- **Tarun Bharat Sangh / Rajendra Singh ("Water Man of India"):** Revived over 1,000 johads (traditional rainwater harvesting structures) across Rajasthan, restoring five rivers. A counter-narrative to high-tech solutions.
- **Xylem Inc.:** Major industrial water technology provider; their smart metering and leak detection (Sensus, Visenti) deployments across hundreds of cities are unmentioned despite direct relevance to entry 10.
- **WaterSHED (Cambodia/Laos):** Market-based sanitation and water access model reaching millions in Southeast Asia through demand creation rather than supply-push.
- **Dar Si Hmad (Morocco):** World's largest fog-collection project, producing ~6,300 L/day for rural Amazigh communities.
- **Murray-Darling Basin Authority (Australia):** The world's most ambitious (and contested) attempt at basin-scale water market reform—a critical case study in water rights reallocation.
- **GRACE-FO satellite mission (NASA/DLR):** Provides the gravity-based groundwater depletion data that makes invisible aquifer decline visible globally. Complements entry 12's OpenET focus.
- **iDE (International Development Enterprises):** Market-based approaches to smallholder irrigation, drip systems, and water access across Africa and Asia.
- **Imagine H2O:** Leading water innovation accelerator that has supported 200+ startups.

## 3. SHADOW GAPS

**Groundwater mining as a ticking clock.** The Ogallala Aquifer, North China Plain, and Indo-Gangetic Basin are being depleted at rates that will force agricultural collapse in specific regions within 20-40 years. No entry treats aquifer depletion with the urgency it demands—entry 11 discusses recharge but not the scale of the extraction crisis it must counteract.

**Brine disposal at scale.** Desalination and ZLD produce concentrated brine. At projected 2040 desalination volumes, brine discharge could exceed 200M m³/day. The ecological consequences for coastal marine ecosystems are mentioned superficially but never quantified as a systemic constraint.

**Water privatization backlash and governance failure.** Cochabamba (2000), Jakarta, Detroit shutoffs, and ongoing fights over water commodification represent a political failure mode the entries largely ignore. Water abundance narratives can be weaponized to justify privatization that excludes the poor.

**Climate-driven supply volatility.** Glacier retreat (Hindu Kush-Himalaya supplies 1.9B people), shifting monsoon patterns, and snowpack decline are reducing the reliability of the freshwater sources that most infrastructure depends on. This isn't a shadow note—it's the central threat multiplier.

**Sanitation-water coupling.** 3.6 billion people lack safely managed sanitation. Contamination of water sources by inadequate sanitation is a primary driver of the water quality crisis, yet sanitation appears nowhere as a linked challenge.

## 4. ACTIONABILITY GAPS (Critical)

The entries fail to improve actionability because they describe *what exists* without providing *decision frameworks for what to do next*. Specifically missing:

**For a city water manager:**
- No decision tree for choosing between supply augmentation strategies (desal vs. reuse vs. MAR vs. NRW reduction) based on local conditions (coastal/inland, climate, existing infrastructure, governance capacity, capital availability).
- No unit economics comparison framework: $/m³ by strategy, by context, with capital vs. operating cost breakdowns.
- No implementation sequencing guidance (e.g., "Start with NRW audit → then reuse feasibility → then desal only if needed").

**For a policymaker:**
- No model water pricing reform pathway. How does a government move from subsidized to cost-reflective pricing without political suicide? The entries never address tariff design, lifeline rates, or cross-subsidy structures.
- No regulatory template for potable reuse approval. What standards, monitoring protocols, and public engagement steps are required?
- No guidance on water rights reform mechanisms (cap-and-trade, proportional allocation, priority systems).

**For a community organizer or NGO:**
- No protocol for assessing which technology fits which community context. A village in rural Rajasthan needs different guidance than a peri-urban settlement in Lagos.
- No maintenance sustainability framework—the "broken pump" problem (entry 03) is identified but no solution pathway is offered.

**For an investor or entrepreneur:**
- No market-entry analysis: which water sub-sectors are investable at what stage? Which regulatory environments are favorable?
- No technology readiness level mapping across the portfolio (desal = TRL 9, MOF-AWG = TRL 4-5, graphene membranes = TRL 3-4).

**The fundamental gap:** The entries provide evidence that water abundance is *technically achievable* but never provide a conditional action map: "If you are [role] in [context] facing [constraint], your highest-leverage first move is [X], because [evidence]."

## 5. INTER-ENTRY TENSIONS

**AWG cost vs. desalination cost:** Entry 02 acknowledges SOURCE Hydropanels cost ~$150/m³ over their lifespan, while entry 01 celebrates desalination at $0.41-1.20/m³. Yet both are framed as "abundance breakthroughs." The 100x cost differential is never reconciled—the collection doesn't clearly articulate when AWG is the right tool vs. when it's an expensive distraction from infrastructure investment.

**Grand Challenge framing vs. community access reality:** Entry 07 declares water is "an abundant, universally available fluid temporarily locked behind correctable barriers" (confidence 0.88), while entry 03 documents 2.1 billion without safe water and 25% handpump failure rates (confidence 0.87). The grand challenge entry's confidence seems high given the on-the-ground evidence in entry 03 about how hard delivery actually is.

**Reuse cost claims vary:** Entry 04 cites NEWater at $0.08-0.15/m³, while entry 06 cites non-potable reuse at $0.30-0.55/m³ and potable reuse at $0.50-0.75/m³. The NEWater figure may reflect Singapore's specific subsidized accounting rather than replicable costs, but this discrepancy is never addressed.

## 6. CONFIDENCE CALIBRATION

**Overconfident:**
- Entry 07 (grand_challenge) at 0.88 makes the strongest philosophical claim ("water is abundant, barriers are correctable") but relies on the weakest causal chain. The barriers are political, institutional, and financial—not merely technical. Should be 0.70-0.75.
- Entry 08 (Gradiant) at 0.90 is the highest-confidence entry but covers a single company. Unicorn valuations are not evidence of planetary-scale impact. Should be 0.80-0.82.

**Underconfident:**
- Entry 10 (NRW) at 0.84 is arguably the most immediately actionable and well-evidenced lever. The World Bank data is robust and the intervention is proven across dozens of cities. Could be 0.88-0.90.
- Entry 03 (community access) at 0.87 is backed by the most comprehensive longitudinal dataset (JMP). The cascade effects are among the best-documented in development economics. Could be 0.90-0.92.
