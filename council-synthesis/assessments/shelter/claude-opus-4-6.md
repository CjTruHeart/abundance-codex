# Shelter Domain Coverage Assessment

## 1. BLIND SPOTS

**Land reform and land value capture:** The collection treats land as a "co-factor" mentioned in passing but never gives it a dedicated entry despite being the binding constraint in most housing markets. Land value taxation (Henry George), land readjustment (Japan's kukaku seiri, South Korea's urban development), and compulsory purchase reform are absent. Without land, every construction technology is irrelevant.

**Retrofit and adaptive reuse at scale:** 12 entries focus overwhelmingly on new construction. The existing global building stock is ~250 billion square meters. Energiesprong gets one sentence. Deep energy retrofits, office-to-residential conversions (post-COVID vacancy crisis), and adaptive reuse of commercial/industrial buildings are unaddressed. Most shelter abundance will come from transforming what exists, not building from scratch.

**Zoning and regulatory reform as a discrete domain:** YIMBY movements, Japan's national zoning system (which prevents local NIMBYism), Minneapolis's elimination of single-family zoning, New Zealand's Medium Density Residential Standards (2022), and California's ADU/SB 9/SB 35 reforms are entirely absent. This is arguably the single highest-leverage intervention in wealthy nations.

**Indigenous and vernacular building traditions:** Compressed earth blocks, rammed earth, bamboo construction (IBUKU, Sharma Springs), and adobe — technologies serving billions — get zero mention. These aren't historical curiosities; they're active, scalable, low-carbon building systems.

**Disaster-resilient and climate-adaptive shelter:** With 26.4 million people displaced annually by climate disasters (IDMC), the collection lacks entries on disaster-resilient design, post-disaster rapid shelter (UNHCR shelters, Shigeru Ban's work), floating/amphibious housing (Makoko, Netherlands), and climate migration shelter needs.

**Cooperative and social housing models beyond CLTs:** Vienna's Gemeindebauten (60% of residents in subsidized housing), Finland's Housing First (reducing chronic homelessness by 40%), and cooperative housing federations are absent.

## 2. MISSING BUILDERS

- **VAHA (formerly Broad Group, China):** Factory-built skyscrapers; built a 57-story tower in 19 days; demonstrates multi-story modular at extreme speed
- **Entekra (California):** Fully integrated off-site framing manufacturer; panelized wood-frame systems
- **Sidewalk Labs / Google's Quayside:** Attempted digitally-integrated urban development; instructive failure
- **Archiblox / Passivhaus Trust:** Net-zero prefab and Passive House standard scaling
- **SPARC/Mahila Milan/NSDF (India):** Slum Dwellers International alliance; community-managed savings and housing for millions across Mumbai and beyond
- **Shigeru Ban:** Pritzker Prize architect; cardboard tube emergency shelters deployed globally
- **Échale a tu Casa (Mexico):** Produced 3+ million bio-block homes using adobe-cement hybrid; community self-build model
- **Habitat for Humanity's Terwilliger Center:** Market systems approach to housing in 70+ countries
- **Opendoor/Zillow's iBuying experiments:** Instructive failures in algorithmic housing markets

## 3. SHADOW GAPS

**Insurance retreat:** Climate-driven insurance withdrawal (Florida, California, Australia) is creating a new form of housing precarity where physically adequate homes become financially uninhabitable. State Farm, Allstate withdrawals from California are canaries.

**Construction material supply chain fragility:** Sand scarcity (50 billion tons/year, UN reports illegal sand mining), timber supply constraints, and cement's geopolitical concentration are unaddressed supply-side risks.

**Demographic mismatch:** Aging populations in Japan, Korea, and Europe produce housing surplus (akiya — 9 million vacant homes in Japan) while Sub-Saharan Africa needs 4 million units/year. The collection doesn't address spatial mismatch between housing supply and demand.

**Short-term rental extraction:** Airbnb's removal of 1M+ units from long-term housing stock in major cities is unmentioned — a distinct mechanism from institutional investor purchases.

**Gender and shelter:** Women own less than 20% of land globally (UN Women). Domestic violence is a leading cause of women's homelessness. The gendered dimension of shelter insecurity is invisible in all 12 entries.

## 4. ACTIONABILITY GAPS (Critical)

This is why the +0.03 actionability result occurred. The entries describe *what exists* and *what's wrong* but never provide:

**Decision frameworks for specific actors:**
- A city council member reading all 12 entries cannot determine: Should we invest in CLTs, reform zoning, partner with modular builders, or create a public housing authority? In what order? At what scale? With what budget thresholds?
- A nonprofit leader cannot determine: What's the minimum viable path to delivering 100 affordable units in 24 months? What financing stack works (LIHTC + CLT + modular)?
- A homebuilder cannot determine: At what volume does modular become cheaper than stick-built for my market? What's the break-even analysis?

**Missing operational knowledge:**
- **Financing stacks:** No entry explains how affordable housing actually gets funded (LIHTC, HOME funds, CDBG, tax-exempt bonds, inclusionary zoning fees, cross-subsidy models). This is the #1 operational barrier.
- **Regulatory navigation:** No entry provides a framework for navigating building codes for novel construction (3D-printed, modular, mycelium). What certifications are needed? ICC-ES evaluation reports? Local amendments?
- **Sequencing guidance:** The entries never say "if you're in context X, start with action Y." There's no triage logic.
- **Scaling playbooks:** ICON printed 230 structures. How does a city go from "interested" to "100 units delivered"? What's the procurement process? RFP structure? Site selection criteria?
- **Policy templates:** No model ordinances, no example legislation, no regulatory sandbox frameworks. The entries identify zoning as a barrier but never describe what reformed zoning looks like operationally (form-based codes, by-right development, ministerial approval).

**Specific missing first moves:**
1. "Calculate your city's housing production deficit using [methodology]"
2. "Audit your zoning code for these 5 specific barriers to density"
3. "Use this cost-comparison framework to evaluate modular vs. site-built for your project"
4. "Apply to these 3 federal programs with these eligibility criteria and timelines"
5. "Structure a CLT using this legal template with these governance provisions"

## 5. INTER-ENTRY TENSIONS

**Housing deficit numbers conflict:** Entry 01 cites 2.8 billion lacking adequate housing and 318 million homeless. Entry 02 cites 1.6 billion inadequate and 300 million homeless. Entry 12 cites 3 billion needing adequate housing by 2030. These are not reconciled — the difference between 1.6B and 2.8B is a billion people. The sources and definitions differ but the collection never acknowledges this.

**Construction productivity data conflicts:** Entry 02 says productivity improved "only 10%" from 2000-2022. Entry 04 says US construction labor productivity declined "~0.6% per year since 1965." Entry 02 also says US productivity "declined ~40% from peak." These paint different pictures — slow improvement globally vs. absolute decline in the US — but aren't reconciled.

**Technology optimism vs. structural critique:** Entries 01, 04, 06, 07, 09, 11 treat construction technology as the primary lever. Entries 03, 10, 12 treat governance, finance, and community organization as primary. Entry 08 warns that technology itself creates new harms. The collection never explicitly adjudicates whether the binding constraint is technological or political — which matters enormously for action.

**Cost claims vary widely:** Entry 01 claims 15% savings (Alquist). Entry 04 claims 10-20% (up to 65% at scale). Entry 09 claims up to 70% of mortgage costs are interest and land value. These aren't contradictory but suggest radically different theories of change — is the problem construction cost or land/finance cost?

## 6. CONFIDENCE CALIBRATION

**Overconfident:**
- Entry 08 (digital surveillance shadow, 0.80): The "data-for-rent" claim cites a "Global Housing Data Privacy Index, 2026" that appears to be a speculative/non-existent source. A 0.70 confidence on that sub-claim is too high for what reads as a thought experiment rather than documented phenomenon.
- Entry 09 (modular habitat contrast, 0.78): Claims "up to 90% waste reduction" and "Category 5 hurricane resilience" with sources that are either company self-reports or unclear ("Nacimiento Project Engineering Data"). Should be 0.65-0.70.

**Underconfident:**
- Entry 10 (CLTs, 0.82): The evidence base here is actually among the strongest — Lincoln Institute, Grounded Solutions, and specific operating data from Proud Ground. Could be 0.85-0.87.
- Entry 05 (Japan, 0.82): Japan's industrialized housing record is one of the most thoroughly documented cases in housing literature. The 0.80 confidence on the post-war deficit claim undersells decades of academic documentation.

**Miscalibrated range:** The spread from 0.75 to 0.85 across entries is suspiciously narrow given that some entries cite peer-reviewed multi-decade data (Japan, CLTs) while others cite company press releases and speculative futures (mycelium, digital surveillance).
