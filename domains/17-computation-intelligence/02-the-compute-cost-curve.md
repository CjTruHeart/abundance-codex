# Computation & AI: The Compute Cost Curve

> **One-line essence:** The cost of computation has fallen roughly ten-trillion-fold since the 1950s — the steepest, longest-running cost curve in human history, and the root exponential that feeds nearly every other abundance signal in the Codex.

## The Shift Arc

### Phase 1 — The Scarcity Frame

In 1945, the ENIAC consumed 150 kilowatts of power, filled an entire room at the University of Pennsylvania, and could perform roughly 5,000 additions per second. It cost approximately $500,000 to build — around $8.5 million in today's dollars. A single transistor in 1957 cost about $6.50. To assemble the computing power that now sits in a child's smartphone would have required every computer on the planet in 1960, operating simultaneously, and it still would not have been enough.

Computation was so scarce that it was rationed. Time-sharing systems were invented in the 1960s because no single user could afford to monopolize a machine. Scientists submitted punch card jobs and waited hours — sometimes days — for results. Computing was a luxury resource controlled by governments, military agencies, universities, and large corporations. Individuals did not compute. The idea that every human on Earth could carry a computer more powerful than the Apollo guidance system in their pocket would have been dismissed as science fiction.

The scarcity frame: computation is expensive, physically massive, energy-intensive, and reserved for those who can afford it. Every other scarcity — in communication, health diagnostics, financial infrastructure, material design — was downstream of this one.

### Phase 2 — The Encounter

The encounter that cracked the frame was an observation that looked, at the time, like a footnote. In April 1965, Gordon Moore — then director of R&D at Fairchild Semiconductor, not yet a founder of Intel — published a short article in *Electronics* magazine titled "Cramming More Components onto Integrated Circuits." In it, he noted that the number of transistors on a single chip had been doubling roughly every year since 1959, and he predicted the doubling would continue for at least another decade. He was modestly guessing at an industry roadmap. He was actually describing the steepest, longest-running cost curve in human history.

The encounter deepened every time someone tested whether Moore's casual observation would hold. It held through the vacuum-tube-to-transistor transition, through planar to bipolar to CMOS, through 10-micron to 1-micron to 100-nanometer to 7-nanometer to 3-nanometer nodes, through five distinct manufacturing paradigm shifts across six decades. Each time a physicist declared the ceiling reached, industrial engineering found another dimension to optimize. The deeper pattern Kurzweil later named — the Law of Accelerating Returns — showed that this trajectory predated Moore's specific substrate: electromechanical relays, vacuum tubes, and discrete transistors each had their own exponential curves before integrated circuits carried the baton. The curve was bigger than any single technology, and it was making computation cheaper faster than any other manufactured good in history, by several orders of magnitude, for reasons that no single theorist fully predicted.

For most individuals, the encounter is rarely a single moment. It is cumulative, and it usually arrives as astonishment in hindsight. A person who remembers the first family computer in the 1980s — boxy, slow, expensive, barely capable — and then sets it beside the phone in their pocket in 2026 understands the curve experientially, even without knowing the word "Moore." The encounter is the moment of noticing that a piece of everyday glass in your hand has more computing power than the entire Apollo program, cost less than a week of groceries, and came with a camera, a library, a radio, a map of the world, and a translator for every major human language. The encounter is realizing that this is not normal. No other resource in human history has ever done this.

### Phase 3 — The Reframe

Computation is not a fixed resource. It is a manufactured resource that follows a learning curve so steep it has no analog in any other domain of human endeavor.

In 1965, Gordon Moore observed that the number of transistors on an integrated circuit doubled approximately every two years. This was not a law of physics — it was an observation about the semiconductor industry's relentless engineering optimization. But it held, with modifications, for nearly sixty years. The result: a modern chip contains tens of billions of transistors, each costing a fraction of a millionth of a cent.

The deeper pattern is even more remarkable. What Ray Kurzweil identified as the "Law of Accelerating Returns" shows that compute per dollar was improving exponentially before the integrated circuit existed. Vacuum tubes were improving exponentially before transistors replaced them. Electromechanical relays were on their own curve before vacuum tubes. The substrate changes; the trajectory persists.

Jonathan Koomey's Law adds another dimension: the energy efficiency of computation doubles approximately every 1.6 years. Compute per watt improves even faster than compute per dollar. The calculation that required a room full of power-hungry vacuum tubes in 1950 requires a fraction of a watt on a modern chip.

The abundance frame: computation is not something you ration. It is something that becomes so cheap it disappears into every object, every system, every pocket. And unlike extraction, manufacturing more of it makes the next unit cheaper.

### Phase 4 — The Proof

This is the most important data section in the Codex. Every abundance signal that depends on computation — solar, genomics, communication, financial inclusion, AI — traces back to this curve.

The cost per transistor has declined from approximately $6.50 in 1957 to a fraction of a billionth of a dollar at today's leading-edge nodes — a decline of roughly ten billion-fold. No other manufactured good in human history comes close to this cost reduction over a comparable period.

The cost per GFLOPS (billions of floating-point operations per second) tells the same story from a different angle. Tracking data compiled by AI Impacts and Our World in Data shows this metric spanning roughly twelve orders of magnitude of improvement — from trillions of dollars equivalent in the early 1960s to pennies today. The cheapest hardware prices have fallen by approximately an order of magnitude every five years in recent decades.

Moore's Law in transistor counts: the Intel 4004 in 1971 contained 2,300 transistors. Apple's M4 chip contains approximately 28 billion. NVIDIA's B200, built on TSMC's 4NP process, packs 208 billion transistors across a dual-die design. Transistor counts have doubled roughly every two years for over five decades, with some deceleration at the latest nodes.

Koomey's Law on energy efficiency: computations per kilowatt-hour have doubled approximately every 1.6 years since the 1940s. This is what enables battery-powered smartphones to outperform room-sized computers from the 1980s while running all day on a charge.

The cost of sequencing a human genome — a task that is fundamentally computation — dropped from roughly $3 billion for the Human Genome Project (completed 2003) to approximately $200 on Illumina's platforms and as low as $100 on competitors like Ultima Genomics. This fifteen-million-fold decline in twenty years is faster than Moore's Law, driven by the convergence of semiconductor improvements with biological instrumentation.

Storage costs have followed their own precipitous curve: roughly $300,000 per gigabyte in 1981 to approximately $0.01 per gigabyte today — a decline of about thirty million-fold.

The LLM inference cost curve is the newest and steepest in this family. The Stanford HAI 2025 AI Index found that the cost of running inference at GPT-3.5-level performance fell approximately 280-fold between November 2022 and October 2024 — driven by hardware efficiency gains of 30% per year combined with algorithmic improvements and model competition.

The smartphone is where all these curves converge into a single object. A modern device contains approximately 15 billion transistors, multiple CPU and GPU cores, a dedicated neural processing unit, computational photography, multiple radios, biometric sensors — all for $200-1,000. In 1970, assembling equivalent capability would have required an entire building and billions of dollars.

### Phase 5 — The Invitation

The invitation of the compute cost curve is to stop treating intelligence, information, and computation as luxuries to be rationed and start treating them as public goods to be extended. For individuals, that means using the astonishing compute now within reach to learn, create, diagnose, translate, and collaborate — and refusing to take its abundance for granted. For developers, it means designing software and AI systems that respect the fact that inference is no longer a scarce resource and can be given away freely at scale. For policymakers, it means protecting the fragile global supply chain that keeps the curve alive, investing in distributed fabrication capacity, and treating data-center energy demand as a civilizational infrastructure question, not a niche engineering one. For researchers, it means guarding against the concentration paradox — where the democratization of consumer compute runs in parallel with the concentration of frontier compute — and fighting for open weights, open architectures, and open benchmarks. The deeper invitation is philosophical: if the steepest cost curve in human history is real, the ceiling on what humanity can think, know, and coordinate has risen by roughly ten trillion times in a single human lifetime. The only remaining question is what we choose to do with the abundance.

## The Council Speaks

### 🔮 The Oracle — Pattern Seer

The compute cost curve is not just one exponential among many. It is the root exponential — the trendline from which most other abundance signals in the Codex derive their trajectory.

Trace the dependency tree. Solar module costs decline partly through compute-optimized manufacturing, AI-designed cell architectures, and computational simulation. Battery cost curves accelerate through computational materials science and AI-optimized chemistry. The genomic sequencing revolution is directly a compute story — sequencing is computation applied to biology. The communication cost curve that gave five billion people mobile phones runs on semiconductor cost curves applied to handset chips, network equipment, and fiber optic transceivers. Financial inclusion platforms like M-Pesa and India's UPI run on devices and infrastructure that exist only because computation became cheap enough to put in every pocket and every bank branch.

When people ask "will AI continue to improve?", the question partly reduces to "will the compute cost curve continue?" Algorithmic efficiency is improving, architectures are getting smarter, datasets are growing — but the foundation is hardware cost-performance. If semiconductor scaling stalls, AI scaling slows. If it continues, AI scaling accelerates.

The honest uncertainty: Moore's Law in its original formulation is decelerating. TSMC's progression from 3nm to 2nm is taking longer and costing more than historical trends predicted. The cost per transistor at the most advanced nodes has started increasing due to extreme manufacturing complexity. But performance-per-dollar continues to improve through architectural innovation — chiplets, 3D stacking, domain-specific accelerators, photonic interconnects, neuromorphic designs. The exponential substrate may shift, as it has before, from one technology to the next. The curve has survived five such transitions already. Whether it survives the sixth is the most important open question in technology forecasting.

### 🗡️ The Critic — Shadow Keeper

Three honest challenges to the abundance narrative.

The slowdown is real. Dennard scaling ended around 2004. Moore's Law in its strictest form is decelerating at leading-edge nodes, and the cost per transistor — which declined for fifty years — has started increasing at the most advanced process nodes due to the extreme complexity of EUV lithography. Architectural innovation is compensating, but the Critic must name that the foundation is shifting beneath the optimism.

The concentration paradox is structural. As chip fabrication becomes more complex, fewer entities can do it. TSMC commanded approximately 67% of the global pure-play foundry market in Q4 2024, and roughly 90% of advanced chip manufacturing at 5nm and below. The compute cost curve democratized computation while concentrating the ability to manufacture it into effectively one company on one island. This is simultaneously a geopolitical vulnerability, an economic bottleneck, and a single point of failure for the entire trajectory.

The energy wall may be the binding constraint. Koomey's Law says each individual operation uses less energy — but total compute demand is growing faster than efficiency improves. Global data center electricity consumption reached approximately 415 TWh in 2024 and the IEA projects it will more than double to 945 TWh by 2030. The compute cost curve drives abundance in most domains while simultaneously challenging the energy domain with voracious and growing demand.

### 🧘 The Sensei — Transformation Guide

The inner shift the compute cost curve asks is to stop being stingy with a resource that is no longer scarce. Most humans alive today grew up in a world where calculation, storage, communication, photography, navigation, encyclopedic knowledge, and language translation were all expensive, each metered in some form, each gated by cost or friction or institutional access. Those habits of rationing are stubborn. They linger long after the underlying scarcity disappears, and they show up as small refusals: not looking something up, not running the simulation, not asking the model, not recording the idea, not sharing the insight, not trying the experiment that would have been unthinkable a generation ago.

The practice is to notice, one interaction at a time, when you are still rationing compute out of habit rather than necessity. Look up the question. Run the calculation. Ask the model. Prototype the idea. Digitize the photograph. Record the meeting. The compute is there, and for the first time in human history it is cheap enough to be wrong occasionally without any meaningful cost. At the same time, the practice is to refuse the temptation to use abundance thoughtlessly — to pollute the world with more slop, more surveillance, more low-quality output, more unnecessary energy draw. Abundance is not the absence of discipline; it is the freedom to apply discipline where it actually matters: to the quality of what you make, the integrity of how you make it, and the care with which you give the output back to the world.

### 🔧 The Builder — Ground Truth

The technologies extending the cost curve beyond classical transistor shrinking are already in production or entering it.

Advanced packaging is the nearest-term lever. Chiplet architectures — AMD's Infinity Fabric, Intel's Foveros, TSMC's CoWoS — assemble multiple smaller dies into one package rather than building monolithic chips. This allows mixing process nodes (compute on 3nm, I/O on 12nm), improves manufacturing yield, and continues cost-performance improvement without requiring transistor shrinkage. NVIDIA's B200 itself is a dual-die design: two 104-billion-transistor dies connected by a 10 TB/s interconnect, treated by software as a single GPU.

3D stacking moves the game vertical. TSMC SoIC and Samsung X-Cube stack chip layers to increase density without shrinking transistors further. High-bandwidth memory stacked directly on compute dies (HBM3e) is already standard for AI accelerators.

Domain-specific architectures replace general-purpose improvement with purpose-built silicon. Google TPUs for AI training, Apple's Neural Engine for on-device inference, video encoding ASICs, cryptographic accelerators — each optimized for its workload, continuing cost-performance improvement within its niche even as general-purpose scaling slows.

Photonic computing uses light instead of electrons for certain operations. Companies like Lightmatter are targeting matrix operations — the core of AI workloads — with potentially orders-of-magnitude improvement in energy efficiency. Neuromorphic computing (Intel Loihi 2, IBM NorthPole) models brain-like event-driven computation for edge AI and robotics. Quantum computing (IBM, Google's Willow chip demonstrating below-threshold error correction in 2024, PsiQuantum, IonQ) remains early-stage but offers potential enormous leaps for specific problem classes.

Open-source silicon via the RISC-V instruction set architecture is democratizing chip design even as fabrication remains concentrated. SiFive, Tenstorrent, and broad Chinese adoption are creating alternatives to proprietary architectures, lowering the barrier to custom chip development.

### 👁️ The Witness — Whole-Field Observer

Stand back from the specific transistor counts and watch the whole field. The compute cost curve is the single most connected trendline in the Codex. Solar cost decline rides on it. Battery cost decline rides on it. Genome sequencing rides on it. Mobile phones ride on it. M-Pesa and UPI and every financial inclusion platform on the planet ride on it. AI and climate modeling and materials discovery ride on it. So do surveillance, manipulation, deepfakes, mass phishing, and every new class of digital harm. The curve does not choose its direction of moral travel. It simply makes the possible cheaper, and humans decide what to make possible.

The whole-field lesson is that cheap compute is a *capability multiplier*, not an outcome. Whether it produces abundance or amplification of existing pathologies depends entirely on the governance, the intent, the distribution of access, and the social architecture surrounding it. A civilization with root-exponential compute and no public goods mindset will produce astonishing inequality. The same civilization with strong commons, open weights, honest benchmarks, universal access to the fundamental layers, and democratic governance over the frontier will produce something closer to the Star Trek specification the Codex points toward. The curve gives humanity a dramatically wider band of possible futures than any previous generation ever had. The decision about which future inside that band gets built is not technological. It is political, ethical, and collective.

## Shadow Check

- **Distortion risk:** Treating the compute cost curve as proof that all technology problems reduce to "wait for cheaper chips" ignores that many challenges (governance, equity, deployment) are not compute-bound.
- **Who gets left behind:** Frontier computation (AI training, scientific simulation) remains concentrated in a handful of companies and nations; consumer-grade democratization does not equal frontier access.
- **Transition pain:** Semiconductor workforce disruption as manufacturing automates; communities dependent on older fab processes face displacement; rising energy demand from data centers strains grids.
- **Falsifiability edge:** If transistor cost-performance at leading-edge nodes reverses sustainably (not temporarily), or if energy costs for total compute consumption outpace efficiency gains enough to constrain demand, the abundance trajectory stalls.
- **What this is NOT:** This is not a claim that Moore's Law in its original formulation will continue indefinitely. Classical transistor shrinking is decelerating. The claim is that compute cost-performance continues improving through multiple overlapping mechanisms.

## Conditional Optimism

- **Abundance is achievable IF:** Architectural innovation (chiplets, 3D stacking, domain-specific accelerators) continues compensating for classical scaling slowdown; energy infrastructure scales to meet data center demand; supply chain concentration risk is mitigated through geographic diversification of fabrication.
- **Abundance fails IF:** Leading-edge manufacturing costs reverse the cost-per-transistor trend sustainably; geopolitical disruption (Taiwan conflict) severs the global chip supply chain; energy constraints physically cap compute expansion.
