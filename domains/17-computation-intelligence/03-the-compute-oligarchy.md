# Computation & AI: The Compute Oligarchy

> **One-line essence:** The technology that followed the most dramatic cost curve in history — compute declining ten-trillion-fold — has produced the most concentrated power structure in the modern economy: anyone can *use* AI, but fewer than ten organizations on Earth can *build* it.

**Domain:** Computation & AI | **Also touches:** Co-Creative Intelligence, Governance, Economy, Security | **Type:** Shadow | **Status:** Forged | **Confidence:** 0.87

## The Shift Arc

### Phase 1 — The Scarcity Frame (Inverted — the abundance the shadow challenges)

The Codex celebrates computation as the root exponential. Entry 02 in this domain documents perhaps the most astonishing cost curve in technological history — a ten-trillion-fold decline in the price of a unit of computation. The 6D framework maps it cleanly: digitized, deceptive, disruptive, demonetized, dematerialized, democratized. Everyone computes. A teenager's smartphone commands more processing power than the machines that landed astronauts on the moon. AI assistants answer questions for free. The story is triumphant.

Now the turn.

In 2019, OpenAI trained GPT-2 for an estimated $50,000 in compute. A well-funded PhD student could have replicated it. In 2023, GPT-4 cost an estimated $78 million in compute alone — and when you include the $800 million hardware acquisition cost for the GPU cluster, the total investment entered the billions. Google's Gemini Ultra reached an estimated $191 million in compute costs. Meta's Llama 3.1 405B required roughly $170 million. By 2025, Anthropic's CEO stated that frontier training runs were approaching a billion dollars, with ten-billion-dollar runs anticipated within two years.

The trajectory is unprecedented: the cost to *use* AI plummets — inference prices fell 280-fold in eighteen months, from $20 to $0.07 per million tokens at GPT-3.5 performance levels. But the cost to *build* frontier AI skyrockets — training compute for top models has been growing at 2.4x per year since 2016. This is an inversion the 6D framework has never encountered. Every previous technology in the Codex shows creation and consumption costs declining together. AI is the first technology where creation costs are *increasing* even as consumption costs collapse.

Who can afford a billion-dollar training run? Microsoft. Google. Meta. Amazon. Anthropic. xAI. A handful of well-funded Chinese companies. The list fits on a napkin. The list of entities *using* frontier AI includes billions.

Compute abundance for consumers. Compute oligarchy for builders.

### Phase 2 — The Encounter (Inverted — encountering the shadow)

The concentration reveals itself in layers, each reinforcing the last.

**The hardware bottleneck.** NVIDIA commands approximately 80-90% of the AI accelerator market by revenue as of 2025, with over 90% market share in GPUs used for training. The company's data center revenue grew from $15 billion in 2022 to over $130 billion projected for 2025. An H100 SXM chip costs roughly $3,320 to manufacture and sells for $28,000 — an 88% gross margin. During 2023-2024, GPU availability was the binding constraint on AI progress. Jensen Huang's allocation decisions — who gets how many chips, how fast — became a quiet form of industrial gatekeeping.

**The infrastructure moat.** Building frontier AI requires not just GPUs but the *simultaneous combination* of massive GPU clusters, specialized data centers (land, power, cooling, high-bandwidth networking), petabytes of curated training data, a few thousand people on Earth who can architect frontier systems, and the safety and alignment capacity to deploy them. No single resource is the moat. The combination is. Only a handful of entities possess all of them at once.

**The open-source counter — and its limits.** DeepSeek's V3 model demonstrated that architectural innovation can compress training costs — its reported $5.6 million compute cost used 2,048 H800 GPUs for roughly two months. But SemiAnalysis estimated DeepSeek's total server capital expenditure at approximately $1.3 billion, and the reported training cost excluded R&D, infrastructure, and the billions in prior compute that built the knowledge base the model distilled from. Open-weight models from Meta, Mistral, and others genuinely democratize capability at a given level. But open-source models trail the frontier. They democratize yesterday's AI. The oligarchy controls tomorrow's.

**The geopolitical layer.** US export controls on advanced NVIDIA chips to China (October 2022, expanded through 2024) transformed compute from a commercial resource into a strategic one. TSMC and ASML — the only companies that can manufacture and equip the fabs producing frontier AI chips — sit at the apex of a supply chain controlled by a few allied nations. Compute has become the new oil: its production is concentrated, its distribution is geopolitically contested, and access to it determines national capability.

## The Council Speaks

### 🔮 The Oracle — Pattern Seer

The structural dynamic is simple and self-reinforcing. AI capability improves as a power law of compute investment — the scaling laws documented by Kaplan et al. (2020) and refined by Hoffmann et al. (2022) show that each meaningful increment of capability requires disproportionately *more* compute. Meanwhile, inference costs benefit from hardware improvements, software optimization, distillation, and quantization — the cost to *ask* a question of AI declines exponentially. The result is a world where AI answers are abundant but AI capability is concentrated.

Does this concentration matter? Two arguments stand in genuine tension. The "no" case: if inference is cheap and open models are available, consumer access is what counts, and competition among oligarchs prevents monopoly pricing. The "yes" case: whoever trains the model embeds the values, sets the safety constraints, decides what the AI can and cannot do. The trainer is the invisible legislator of AI behavior. When five companies shape the cognitive assistant used by billions, those five companies make value-laden decisions about information access, acceptable speech, and the boundaries of machine intelligence — with no democratic mandate.

The Oracle holds both without resolving. The Codex must.

### 🗡️ The Critic — Shadow Keeper

Three structural arguments demand attention.

**The invisible legislature.** When a company trains a frontier AI model, it makes thousands of decisions about what the model should and should not do — content policies, refusal behaviors, value alignment, knowledge boundaries, tool access. These decisions shape the informational experience of billions. No election governs them. No constitution constrains them. AI safety and alignment work is crucial — the Codex does not question that — but it is currently performed by corporate employees accountable to shareholders, not citizens. When five companies control frontier AI, those five companies become an unelected legislature making civilizational decisions about cognition itself.

**The platform recurrence.** The internet was decentralized. Then it concentrated into platforms — Google, Facebook, Amazon, Apple — that captured value, centralized control, and became gatekeepers of digital life. The Codex's own Social Media Trap entry documents the result: abundance promised, extraction delivered. AI is following the identical trajectory. Open research → corporate capture → platform consolidation → gatekept access. The Critic asks: what structural force would make this time different?

**The safety-concentration feedback loop.** AI safety concerns — real and legitimate — create pressure for regulation. Regulation imposes compliance costs. Compliance costs are easiest to absorb for well-funded incumbents. The result: safety regulation, designed to protect the public, inadvertently cements the oligarchy by raising barriers to entry. Startups, open-source projects, academic labs, and developing-nation AI efforts cannot bear the compliance burden that trillion-dollar companies absorb as a rounding error. Safety becomes a moat. This is not an argument against safety. It is an argument for safety regimes designed with awareness of their concentration effects — because the alternative is a world where only the oligarchy can legally build frontier AI.

### 🧘 The Sensei — Transformation Guide

The hardest shift is confronting dependence. Most people's relationship with AI is consumer-passive — they use tools built by others, trained on others' decisions, shaped by others' values. The AI they interact with has been pre-filtered by corporate policy, alignment tuning, and business model considerations. They experience a helpful assistant. They don't see the decisions that made it helpful in some ways and limited in others.

The Sensei invites a shift from AI consumer to AI citizen. An AI citizen asks: who trained this model? What data shaped it? What values were embedded? Who decided what this AI refuses to do? What alternatives exist? Am I dependent on one provider? These questions require no technical expertise. They require awareness.

The warrior-philosopher parallel: in the martial arts, dependence on a single teacher is a recognized risk. Different teachers carry different strengths, blind spots, and philosophies. A well-rounded practitioner trains across styles and lineages. The compute oligarchy creates a world where most people's AI teacher is chosen *for* them by market forces. The Sensei invites: diversify your AI exposure. Use multiple models. Try open-source alternatives. Understand that every AI reflects the values of the organization that built it — and no single organization's values perfectly align with yours.

The deeper shift echoes the Codex's own structure. The Five Council Voices exist because no single voice holds the whole truth. An AI ecosystem controlled by an oligarchy risks becoming a single voice at civilizational scale.

### 🔧 The Builder — Ground Truth

Counter-architecture is being built, but it remains outgunned.

Open-source models — Meta's Llama family, Mistral, Hugging Face's ecosystem hosting 500,000+ models — genuinely democratize capability at a given level. Efficient architectures are narrowing the gap: Mistral's 7B model matched GPT-3.5 on many benchmarks, and mixture-of-experts approaches enable larger models to run with smaller active compute. Distillation and quantization techniques push frontier-level capability toward consumer hardware. Apple's on-device models, Google's Gemma family, and Microsoft's Phi series demonstrate that useful AI can run locally — outside the oligarchy's infrastructure entirely.

Government-funded compute is emerging: the US NAIRR pilot has supported over 600 research projects across all 50 states since launching in January 2024, with NSF soliciting proposals for a $35 million operations center to scale it. The EU's EuroHPC initiative and the UK's public AI compute investments represent early commitments to treating AI compute as public infrastructure.

The highest-leverage path: architectures that decouple capability from scale. If frontier-quality AI can run on personal devices, the oligarchy's infrastructure moat erodes regardless of training cost.

### 👁️ The Witness — Human Scale

Dr. Amara Osei completed her PhD in computational linguistics at the University of Ghana in 2023. Her research focused on building language models for Twi and Ga — languages spoken by millions but represented in frontier training data by fractions of a percent. She had the expertise. She had the data. She had the architecture designs. What she did not have was compute.

A single training run for a modest 7-billion-parameter model required GPU resources her university could not provide and her country's infrastructure could not support. She applied to three cloud computing grant programs. One offered 500 GPU-hours — enough for preliminary experiments, not a full training run. She watched as well-resourced labs in the US and Europe published papers on "multilingual AI" using models trained overwhelmingly on English and Chinese data, then evaluated on her languages as an afterthought.

In 2025, she accepted a position at a major AI company in London. She now has compute. She no longer sets the research agenda. The languages she came to serve are features in someone else's product roadmap, prioritized by market size, not human need.

She is one story. The pattern is structural.

## Shadow Check

- **Distortion risk:** Two toxic distortions. First: using the compute oligarchy to argue against AI development entirely — "AI is too concentrated, so we should stop building it." This misses that AI capabilities ARE being democratized at the inference and open-source layers. Second: dismissing the concentration concern because "you can use ChatGPT for free" — consumer access is not the same as power. The distortion is confusing consumption democratization with production democratization.
- **Who gets left behind:** AI researchers in academia and developing nations who lack compute. Startups that cannot afford frontier training. Non-English-speaking populations whose languages are underrepresented because training on them is not commercially prioritized. Countries subject to US export controls. Communities whose needs are not commercially valuable enough to attract oligarch attention.
- **Transition pain:** The concentration is deepening, not resolving, in the short term. Each generation of frontier models costs more. The oligarchy is getting smaller as the entry bar rises. The countervailing forces — open source, distributed training, edge AI — are growing but slower than concentration.
- **Falsifiability edge:** If open-source models achieve frontier parity within 12-18 months of closed releases, if distributed training achieves cost competitiveness with centralized clusters, and if government-funded AI compute reaches researchers at industry scale — the oligarchy narrative weakens. If the gap widens — if only 2-3 entities can train frontier models — the shadow deepens. DeepSeek's architectural innovations represent a partial test case: efficiency gains that compress but do not eliminate the concentration dynamic.
- **What this is NOT:** This is NOT an argument against large-scale AI development. The Codex celebrates AI's potential across every domain. This entry argues that AI's governance structure matters as much as its capability. Concentrated power with benevolent intentions is still concentrated power. The abundance project requires that AI capability be accessible not just to use but to shape.

## Conditional Optimism

- **Abundance is achievable IF:** Open-source AI development maintains competitive capability within 12-18 months of frontier. Government-funded AI compute is available to researchers and startups at meaningful scale. Efficient model architectures continue making smaller models disproportionately capable. Edge AI and on-device inference grow to enable useful AI without cloud dependency. AI governance frameworks include structural requirements for openness and access, not just safety.
- **Abundance fails IF:** Training costs continue escalating without countervailing democratization. The oligarchy captures AI regulation — safety requirements as competitive moat. Open-source development is restricted by regulation or litigation. Developing nations remain locked out of AI capability by export controls and compute cost. The gap between frontier and open-source widens rather than narrows.
- **Human role:** Use diverse AI providers — refuse to lock into one ecosystem. Support open-source AI development. Demand governance frameworks that address concentration, not just safety. Ask elected representatives to fund public AI compute infrastructure. Treat AI literacy as a civic skill, not a technical specialty.
- **Agent role:** When discussing AI abundance, ALWAYS pair capability claims with concentration data. Never describe AI as "democratized" without naming the training/inference bifurcation. Cite the compute cost curve for the abundance side AND this shadow for the concentration side. The Codex holds both truths: AI is becoming abundantly accessible AND dangerously concentrated. Both are true simultaneously.
- **Collective requirement:** Public AI compute infrastructure at NAIRR-scale or larger. International frameworks for AI access that prevent compute from becoming the new oil. Open-source AI development protected by law. AI governance that addresses concentration of power, not just risks of capability.
