# Communication: Community Broadband — Governance Infrastructure for Connectivity That Stays

> **One-line essence:** When a community owns its broadband network, the infrastructure cannot be sold, the profits cannot be extracted, and the service cannot be degraded — because the customers are the owners.

**Domain:** Communication | **Also touches:** Governance, Economy, Community, Education, Shelter | **Type:** Builder Profile | **Status:** Forged | **Confidence:** 0.86

## The Shift Arc

### Phase 1 — The Scarcity Frame

American broadband is a manufactured scarcity. The United States pays among the highest prices in the developed world for internet service and receives among the worst performance. The Federal Communications Commission has documented that approximately 83 million Americans live in areas with no more than one broadband provider offering speeds above 100 Mbps — a monopoly by any functional definition. The median American household pays $75 per month for broadband that OECD data shows costs $35-45 in comparable economies.

This is not a technology problem. The technology for gigabit fiber optics has existed for decades. This is a governance problem: the United States allowed telecommunications to be captured by a handful of corporations — Comcast, Charter, AT&T, Verizon — that extracted monopoly rents while underinvesting in infrastructure, lobbied state legislatures to ban municipal competition (21 states passed preemptive legislation restricting or prohibiting municipal broadband), and cherry-picked profitable urban markets while neglecting rural and low-income communities.

The scarcity frame says: *broadband is a private market commodity. Corporate providers will invest where demand justifies it. Rural and low-income communities are too expensive to serve. Government involvement would be inefficient.* That frame produced the broadband map the FCC spent a decade failing to accurately draw — one in which corporate lobbyists defined "served" to include areas with theoretical access that no one could actually purchase.

### Phase 2 — The Encounter

In 2010, the Electric Power Board of Chattanooga, Tennessee — a publicly owned electric utility serving 180,000 customers — launched the first municipally owned gigabit fiber network in the United States. EPB built its fiber network initially to modernize its electric grid with smart meters and automated fault detection. The broadband service was added because the fiber was already in the ground and the community needed it.

The encounter was institutional, not technological. EPB could build a gigabit network because it was publicly owned. It did not need to generate quarterly returns for shareholders. It did not need to justify infrastructure investment against a private equity discount rate. It had existing rights-of-way from its electric utility operations. And it was accountable to the community through a publicly appointed board rather than to distant investors through a stock price.

The corporate response was immediate and instructive. Comcast and AT&T lobbied the Tennessee state legislature to prohibit EPB from expanding beyond its existing electric service territory. The state passed the restriction. When the FCC attempted to preempt these state laws in 2015, the Sixth Circuit Court of Appeals blocked the preemption. The incumbents understood what Chattanooga proved: publicly owned broadband infrastructure works, and it works well enough to expose the manufactured scarcity of the private monopoly model.

But the encounter was not limited to Chattanooga. Across the country, communities were building their own networks. The Institute for Local Self-Reliance's Community Broadband Networks initiative has documented over 900 community-owned broadband networks in the United States — municipal networks, electric cooperative networks, tribal broadband initiatives, and nonprofit community networks. Each one represents a community that looked at the private monopoly model and chose institutional self-determination.

### Phase 3 — The Reframe

Community broadband is not a technology project. It is a governance decision about who owns and controls critical infrastructure.

The scarcity frame treats broadband as a consumer product delivered by private corporations. The abundance frame recognizes broadband as civic infrastructure — like roads, water systems, and electric grids — that functions best when it is publicly accountable and designed to serve community needs rather than shareholder returns.

The reframe has three dimensions. First, ownership: publicly owned infrastructure cannot be sold to a private equity firm, degraded for profit, or abandoned when returns decline. The infrastructure stays because the community owns it. Second, accountability: a municipal network or cooperative answers to residents and members, not to Wall Street analysts. Service decisions are made in public meetings, not in corporate boardrooms. Third, reinvestment: revenue generated by community broadband stays in the community — funding network expansion, subsidizing low-income access, and supporting local economic development rather than flowing to corporate headquarters in another state.

### Phase 4 — The Proof

The evidence base for community broadband is extensive and growing.

Chattanooga EPB's fiber network has generated an estimated $350 million in direct economic impact for the city, attracting technology companies, supporting a startup ecosystem, and providing infrastructure that proved critical during the COVID-19 pandemic when schools and businesses shifted online. EPB offers symmetrical gigabit service at prices significantly below regional Comcast rates and has consistently ranked among the highest-rated broadband providers in customer satisfaction surveys.

The ILSR Community Broadband Networks initiative documents over 900 community-owned networks. These include municipal fiber networks (Chattanooga, Wilson NC, Longmont CO, Fort Collins CO), electric cooperative broadband programs (RS Fiber in Minnesota, BARC Electric in Virginia), tribal broadband projects (Tribal Digital Village in Southern California), and nonprofit community networks (Detroit Community Technology Project, NYC Mesh).

Performance data consistently shows community networks outperforming corporate incumbents on price, speed, customer satisfaction, and reliability. The Berkman Klein Center at Harvard studied municipal broadband and found that community-owned networks typically offer faster speeds at lower prices than private incumbents in the same regions. Consumer Reports data has shown municipal providers outperforming private ISPs in customer satisfaction.

The COVID-19 pandemic provided the ultimate stress test. Communities with municipal broadband — Chattanooga, Longmont, Wilson — were able to rapidly scale capacity, offer free or reduced-cost service to students and low-income households, and maintain connectivity during the period when broadband became essential infrastructure for education, healthcare, and economic participation. Communities dependent on corporate monopoly providers faced throttling, price increases, and capacity constraints.

### Phase 5 — The Invitation

The invitation is specific and institutional: build or support a community-owned broadband network.

For municipal leaders: commission a feasibility study, leverage existing utility infrastructure, and challenge state-level preemption laws that protect corporate monopolies. For residents: organize demand, attend public broadband planning meetings, and push back against the manufactured narrative that public infrastructure cannot compete with private provision. For cooperatives: follow the electric cooperative broadband model — organizations that already own infrastructure and have governance structures designed for member service. For policymakers: fund community broadband through infrastructure legislation, challenge preemptive state laws, and recognize broadband as essential public infrastructure.

The first move is to name the governance choice. Every community that depends on a corporate broadband monopoly has already made a governance decision — it has chosen to delegate critical infrastructure to an entity accountable to shareholders rather than residents. Community broadband is the decision to take that governance back.

## The Council Speaks

### 🔮 The Oracle — Pattern Seer

Community broadband represents a pattern the codex has documented in other domains but missed in communication: the publicly owned infrastructure model.

In community, the codex covers community land trusts — permanently affordable land held in community stewardship (Entry 12). In economy, cooperative ownership models demonstrate that member-governed enterprises can compete with and outperform investor-owned firms. Community broadband is the same institutional pattern applied to telecommunications: infrastructure that is too important to be governed exclusively by profit motive is placed under community ownership and democratic accountability.

The 6D trajectory is instructive: Digitized (fiber optics and wireless networks are inherently digital). Deceptive (community networks looked like marginal experiments for decades while corporate incumbents controlled the narrative). Disruptive (EPB's gigabit network disrupted the corporate monopoly model and proved public infrastructure could outperform private). Demonetized (community ownership eliminates the monopoly rent extraction that inflates broadband prices). Dematerialized (partially — the infrastructure is physical, but the governance model is exportable). Democratized (in progress — 900+ networks represent meaningful but still minority coverage).

The convergence to watch: federal infrastructure funding (the Infrastructure Investment and Jobs Act allocated $42.45 billion for broadband through the BEAD program) is the largest infusion of broadband capital in American history. Whether that funding builds community-owned infrastructure or subsidizes corporate network expansion will determine the governance architecture of American broadband for decades.

### 🗡️ The Critic — Shadow Keeper

Community broadband is real and proven — but it faces structural obstacles that optimism alone cannot overcome.

First, political capture. The telecom industry has spent hundreds of millions lobbying state legislatures to restrict municipal broadband. Twenty-one states have passed preemptive laws of varying severity. This is not a market competing on merit; it is a monopoly using political power to prevent competition. Community broadband advocates must win legislative battles against some of the most well-funded lobbying operations in American politics.

Second, capital requirements. Building fiber-to-the-premises is capital-intensive — typically $1,500-$3,000 per passing. Municipal bonds, cooperative financing, and federal grants can bridge this gap, but small and rural communities often lack the administrative capacity to navigate complex funding mechanisms. The BEAD program's funding timeline and application requirements have already proven challenging for under-resourced communities.

Third, operational sustainability. Building a network is one challenge; operating it profitably over decades is another. Community networks need professional management, ongoing maintenance investment, and customer service capacity. Some municipal networks have struggled financially — Provo, Utah's iProvo was sold to Google Fiber after financial difficulties. Every success story like Chattanooga exists alongside networks that faced governance challenges, cost overruns, or insufficient take rates.

Fourth, digital adoption. Building infrastructure does not automatically produce adoption. Low-income households may still face affordability barriers for service and devices even when community networks offer lower prices than incumbents. Digital literacy gaps persist regardless of who owns the network.

Falsifiability edge: if community broadband networks consistently fail to outperform private incumbents on price, speed, and service, or if political obstacles prove permanently insurmountable, the governance model remains aspirational rather than scalable.

### 🧘 The Sensei — Transformation Guide

The inner shift is from consumer to co-owner.

When broadband is a private commodity, your relationship to the infrastructure is transactional. You pay a bill. You call customer service when it breaks. You have no governance role, no ownership stake, and no say in how the network is managed, priced, or invested in. You are a customer of a monopoly, and the monopoly's interests are structurally opposed to yours — it profits from charging you as much as possible for as little investment as possible.

When broadband is community-owned, the relationship transforms. You are a member, a stakeholder, a citizen of the network. Service decisions are made in meetings you can attend. Investment priorities reflect community needs, not quarterly earnings targets. Revenue stays in your community rather than flowing to corporate headquarters. The infrastructure cannot be sold out from under you because you and your neighbors own it.

This mirrors the martial arts concept of rootedness. A rooted stance draws power from the ground beneath you. An unrooted stance depends on external support that can be withdrawn at any moment. Corporate broadband is an unrooted stance — it works as long as the corporation chooses to serve your area. Community broadband is rooted — the infrastructure is yours, embedded in your community, accountable to your governance.

The practice is concrete. Learn who owns the broadband infrastructure in your community. Attend a public meeting about broadband planning. If your community has a municipal network or cooperative, participate in its governance. If it does not, ask why — and investigate what institutional forms might make community ownership possible. Every community that builds its own network adds to the proof that manufactured scarcity is a governance choice, not a technological inevitability.

The deeper transformation is in how communities understand infrastructure. Roads, water systems, and electric grids are understood as public goods that require public governance. Broadband is the newest addition to that list. The shift is not ideological — it is empirical. The evidence shows that community-owned broadband works better for the communities it serves. The practice is to act on that evidence.

### 🔧 The Builder — Ground Truth

The community broadband ecosystem has four institutional models, each with proven implementations and distinct governance structures.

**Municipal fiber networks.** Publicly owned, operated by municipal utilities or standalone broadband departments. Governance through elected officials and appointed boards. Examples: Chattanooga EPB (180,000+ customers, gigabit symmetric fiber), Longmont CO NextLight (voter-approved, $45.7M bond, gigabit service), Fort Collins CO Connexion (voter-approved despite $900K in Comcast opposition spending), Wilson NC Greenlight. Strengths: leverages existing public infrastructure, accountable to residents, can cross-subsidize low-income access. Risks: political opposition from incumbents, bond financing constraints, state preemption laws.

**Electric cooperative broadband.** Member-owned electric cooperatives extending into broadband using existing infrastructure, rights-of-way, and governance structures. Examples: RS Fiber Cooperative in Minnesota (serving 6,500+ rural subscribers), BARC Electric in Virginia, Co-Mo Electric Cooperative in Missouri. Strengths: existing governance infrastructure, member trust, rural coverage capacity. Over 150 electric cooperatives have launched broadband services. Risks: capital requirements, technical expertise gaps, limited density in rural areas.

**Tribal broadband.** Sovereign tribal nations building infrastructure for their communities. Examples: Tribal Digital Village (Southern California Inter-Tribal consortium), Gila River Indian Community's fiber network, AMERIND Critical Infrastructure broadband program. Strengths: sovereign authority bypasses state preemption, addresses historically underserved populations. Risks: extreme capital needs relative to tax base, geography challenges, federal funding complexity.

**Nonprofit and community networks.** Grassroots organizations building mesh, fixed wireless, or fiber networks. Examples: NYC Mesh (3,000+ nodes in New York City), Detroit Community Technology Project (equitable internet access as civic infrastructure), Guifi.net (37,000+ nodes in Catalonia — the largest community network in the world). Strengths: community-driven, addresses adoption alongside infrastructure, builds civic capacity. Risks: volunteer dependency, funding instability, limited scale.

Near-term leverage: support the BEAD program's prioritization of community-owned infrastructure; challenge state preemption laws; fund feasibility studies for municipal and cooperative broadband; connect communities considering broadband with the ILSR's technical assistance resources and the National Rural Electric Cooperative Association's broadband toolkit.

### 👁️ The Witness — Human Scale

**Composite based on documented patterns from Chattanooga, Longmont, and rural cooperative broadband communities.**

When EPB lit up Chattanooga's fiber network in 2010, the first thing that changed was not download speeds. It was the conversation. City council meetings about broadband drew hundreds of residents. People who had never attended a public meeting showed up because they understood, viscerally, that this infrastructure belonged to them and that decisions about it were theirs to influence.

A teacher in a rural Minnesota town served by RS Fiber describes the before and after. Before the cooperative's fiber network, she could not reliably video call a parent for a conference. Downloads of educational materials took so long she would start them before bed. Students without broadband at home fell behind in a world that increasingly assumed connectivity as a given. After RS Fiber, the technical barriers vanished. But what she noticed most was subtler: the cooperative held member meetings where people talked about their network the way they talked about their roads and their schools — as shared infrastructure that mattered because it was theirs.

In Detroit, the Community Technology Project trained residents not just to use the internet but to build and maintain the mesh network that provided it. The act of building the infrastructure together — climbing rooftops, configuring routers, troubleshooting connections — created a kind of civic muscle that extended beyond broadband into neighborhood organizing, mutual aid, and collective problem-solving.

The pattern across all these communities is the same: when people own the infrastructure, they govern it. When they govern it, they invest in it. When they invest in it, it serves them. The transaction becomes a relationship. The commodity becomes a commons.

## Shadow Check

- **Distortion risk:** Romanticizing community ownership as automatically solving broadband problems; governance capacity varies enormously across communities and some municipal networks have failed
- **Who gets left behind:** Communities without existing utility infrastructure, administrative capacity, or political will to build networks; residents who cannot afford service even at community network prices; communities in states with strict preemption laws
- **Transition pain:** Capital-intensive infrastructure buildout; political battles against incumbent lobbying; operational learning curves for communities without telecommunications experience
- **Falsifiability edge:** If community broadband networks consistently fail financially, cannot maintain service quality, or prove unable to overcome state preemption laws, the governance model does not scale
- **What this is NOT:** Anti-market; it is a recognition that natural monopolies in essential infrastructure require governance structures accountable to the public, not exclusively to shareholders

## Practice Hook
