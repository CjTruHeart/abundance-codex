# Communication: The DIY Telco — Community-Owned Networks

> **One-line essence:** When the network won't reach you, you don't wait — you become the network.

**Domain:** Communication | **Also touches:** Governance, Community, Economy, Education | **Type:** Builder Profile | **Status:** Forged | **Confidence:** 0.78

---

## The Shift Arc

### Phase 1 — The Scarcity Frame

Connectivity is something delivered to you — by telecom giants, by governments, by distant infrastructure you do not control. If you live outside profitable zones — mountains, jungles, rural margins — you are invisible to the network.

In villages like San Juan Yaee, Oaxaca, the signal simply stopped. No bars. No data. No participation in the digital world. Communication scarcity wasn't theoretical — it meant no emergency calls, no access to markets, no voice in the broader system.

The implicit rule: *if the market doesn't serve you, you remain unconnected.*

### Phase 2 — The Encounter

In 2013–2016, a small group of technologists and indigenous communities did something radical: they built their own cellular network.

On a rooftop in San Juan Yaee, villagers installed GSM equipment — open-source software, low-cost radios, improvised towers. The cost was a fraction of traditional telecom deployment. The labor was local.

Then came the breakthrough: in 2016, Mexico granted an **Indigenous social telecom concession** to Telecomunicaciones Indígenas Comunitarias (TIC A.C.).

This wasn't just a technical hack. It was a governance unlock. For the first time, a community was legally recognized as a telecom operator.

### Phase 3 — The Reframe

Connectivity is not a service you receive. It is infrastructure you can own, govern, and maintain.

The scarcity frame says: *coverage expands from the center outward.*
The abundance frame says: *networks can emerge from the edge inward.*

Community networks transform users into operators. The question shifts from "who will connect us?" to "what do we need to connect ourselves?"

### Phase 4 — The Proof

The model spread. Dozens of indigenous communities across Mexico deployed community-owned cellular networks. Similar efforts emerged globally — Argentina, South Africa, India, the Philippines.

Costs remained radically lower than traditional telecom builds. Governance remained local. Usage aligned with actual community needs, not extractive business models.

This is not theoretical. It is deployed infrastructure.

### Phase 5 — The Invitation

If communication infrastructure can be locally owned, what else can? Energy. Water. Data. Governance itself.

The next move is not waiting for universal coverage — it is enabling universal capability to build coverage. The practice: when encountering infrastructure gaps, ask not "who should fix this?" but "what would it take for us to build it?"

## The Council Speaks

### 🔮 The Oracle — Pattern Seer

Community networks are the communication domain's version of the solar rooftop revolution.

For decades, telecom followed a centralized model: high capital expenditure, licensed spectrum, vertically integrated operators. This created efficiency at scale — but exclusion at the edges. Rural and indigenous regions were structurally unprofitable.

What changed is not just cost — it's architecture. Open-source telecom stacks (like OpenBTS), falling hardware costs, and unlicensed or socially allocated spectrum created a new possibility: **telecom as a modular, local system** rather than a centralized monopoly.

On the 6D curve: telecom infrastructure increasingly software-defined (digitized); early community networks dismissed as fringe experiments through the early 2010s (deceptive); legal recognition in Mexico 2016 proves viability (disruptive); infrastructure costs collapse relative to traditional carriers (demonetized); core network functions shift to software (dematerialized); communities become operators (democratized).

The deeper pattern is infrastructure decentralization. We've seen it before — rooftop solar in energy, user-generated content in media, mobile money in finance. Now communication joins the pattern.

The convergence to watch: **community networks + LEO satellites + open spectrum policy**. Local last-mile ownership paired with global backhaul could create a hybrid system — globally connected, locally governed.

### 🗡️ The Critic — Shadow Keeper

Community networks are not a silver bullet. They are fragile systems operating in hostile terrain.

First, **skills scarcity**. Building and maintaining telecom infrastructure requires technical knowledge that many communities must bootstrap from scratch. When key individuals leave, networks can degrade or collapse.

Second, **burnout and governance strain**. Local operators are not just engineers — they become administrators, conflict mediators, and regulators. This is unpaid or underpaid labor in many cases.

Third, **backhaul dependence**. Most community networks still rely on upstream internet providers. If that link fails — or becomes too expensive — the network becomes isolated.

Fourth, **regulatory risk**. Mexico's concession is a breakthrough — but not the global norm. In many countries, spectrum laws criminalize exactly this kind of local innovation.

Fifth, **romanticization risk**. External observers often celebrate "community networks" without funding their long-term sustainability. Pilot projects succeed; maintenance fails.

Falsifiability edge: if community networks cannot sustain multi-year operations without external NGO support, the model remains symbolic, not structural.

### 🧘 The Sensei — Transformation Guide

The identity shift here is profound.

From: *we are unserved.*
To: *we are builders of the system we need.*

Scarcity thinking trains dependence. It teaches that infrastructure comes from above — from governments, corporations, experts. When that system fails you, the learned response is waiting, lobbying, or leaving.

Community networks demand a different posture: stewardship. This is uncomfortable. It requires releasing the belief that only certified institutions can build critical systems. It asks communities to trust their own capacity — not blindly, but through practice, iteration, and shared responsibility.

But there is a second shift, more subtle: from *consumption* to *participation*. When you own the network, you feel its fragility. Bandwidth is no longer abstract — it's something you allocate, maintain, protect. Governance becomes real.

This is the deeper training: abundance is not just access. It is responsibility scaled across many hands. The practice: when you encounter a system failure, pause before outsourcing the solution. Ask, *what part of this system could we own?*

### 🔧 The Builder — Ground Truth

The core builder here is **Telecomunicaciones Indígenas Comunitarias (TIC A.C.)** in Oaxaca, Mexico.

What they built:
- GSM cellular networks using open-source software (OpenBTS lineage and successors)
- Low-cost base stations (~$5,000–$10,000 vs. hundreds of thousands for traditional towers)
- Community-run billing systems (often ~$1–2/month per user)

Key milestone: **2016** — the Mexican regulator (IFT) grants the first Indigenous social telecom concession.

Scale: roughly 20+ communities initially, expanding to dozens (varies by source).

Technical stack: local base station → microwave or fiber backhaul → internet gateway, with increasing experimentation with mesh networks and hybrid systems.

Bottlenecks: backhaul cost (often the largest recurring expense), technical training and retention, regulatory replication outside Mexico.

Adjacent builders: Rhizomatica (technical support and advocacy), the Association for Progressive Communications (policy and research), Zenzeleni Networks in South Africa, and a growing global community network movement.

Leverage point: the highest leverage is not hardware — it's **policy templates plus training systems**. If governments create licensing pathways and communities gain technical literacy, this model scales.

### 👁️ The Witness — Human Scale

**Composite based on documented patterns from WIRED reporting on Oaxaca community networks and TIC A.C. project documentation.**

In San Juan Yaee, the first call on the community network wasn't dramatic. It was ordinary. A man called his brother in the next village.

Before the network, that call required a two-hour walk over mountain roads. Messages traveled physically — by foot, by chance. Now it took seconds.

The rooftop installation had been improvised — equipment hauled up by hand, cables run across uneven walls. No telecom trucks. No corporate rollout. Just neighbors building something together.

When the phone rang, it wasn't just a signal. It was proof: *we are no longer waiting.*

The network didn't just connect calls. It changed posture. People stopped asking when coverage would arrive. They started asking what else they could build.

## Shadow Check

- **Distortion risk:** Romanticizing grassroots networks without funding long-term operations
- **Who gets left behind:** Communities without technical capacity or regulatory support
- **Transition pain:** Burden shifts from corporations to local actors without guaranteed sustainability
- **Falsifiability edge:** If networks fail after initial deployment due to maintenance or governance breakdown
- **What this is NOT:** A replacement for all telecom infrastructure — this complements, not replaces, large-scale networks
