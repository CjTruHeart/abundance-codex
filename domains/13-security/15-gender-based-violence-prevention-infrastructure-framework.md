# Security: Gender-Based Violence Prevention — The Infrastructure That Was Never Built

> **One-line essence:** One in three women on Earth experiences physical or sexual violence in her lifetime, and the security domain has spent decades building response systems while barely beginning to build the prevention infrastructure that could reduce the violence before it happens.

**Domain:** Security | **Also touches:** Health, Community, Governance, Education, Economy | **Type:** Framework | **Status:** Forged | **Confidence:** 0.85

---

## The Shift Arc

### Phase 1 — The Scarcity Frame

For most of modern history, gender-based violence was not treated as a security issue at all. It was treated as a private matter — something that happened behind closed doors, between individuals, within families, and was no concern of the state, the security apparatus, or the public. When legal systems addressed it, they addressed it as a crime of individual deviance: one perpetrator, one victim, one prosecution. The structural, institutional, and cultural conditions that produced gender-based violence at pandemic scale were invisible to the security frame.

The scarcity frame around GBV takes a specific form: inevitability. Violence against women is treated as a permanent background condition — tragic but ultimately ineradicable, like weather. Prevention is not taken seriously because the implicit assumption is that human nature, or male nature, or the nature of power, makes this violence a fixed feature of every society. The best that can be done, in this frame, is to respond after the harm: shelters, hotlines, restraining orders, prosecutions, support services. All necessary. None sufficient.

The scale of the problem makes the inadequacy of response-only approaches unmistakable. The World Health Organization estimates that globally, 1 in 3 women — approximately 736 million — have experienced physical and/or sexual intimate partner violence or non-partner sexual violence in their lifetime. Violence against women is a leading cause of death and disability for women of reproductive age worldwide. The economic cost has been estimated at approximately 2% of global GDP. The health consequences include physical injury, chronic pain, depression, PTSD, reproductive health complications, and elevated suicide risk. And these are lifetime prevalence figures — they describe a steady-state condition, not an acute crisis.

The scarcity frame says: this is how it is. Build better response. The prevention frame says: this is how it has been, and it can be different — but only if we build the institutional infrastructure to change it.

### Phase 2 — The Encounter

The encounter that cracked the response-only frame was the accumulation of evidence, over three decades, that gender-based violence is preventable — not just in theory, but in practice, with measurable results from rigorously evaluated programs.

The most significant breakthrough came from the SASA! program, developed by Raising Voices in Uganda and evaluated through a community randomized controlled trial published in The Lancet in 2014. SASA! (which stands for Start, Awareness, Support, Action in Luganda) is a community mobilization intervention that works through four phases of engagement with community members, community activists, institutional leaders, and local officials. It does not focus on individual perpetrators. It focuses on shifting the community norms, power dynamics, and institutional responses that enable violence.

The results were extraordinary. The cluster-randomized trial, conducted in eight communities in Kampala, found that SASA! communities experienced a 52% reduction in past-year physical intimate partner violence compared to control communities. Women in SASA! communities were significantly more likely to report that community members would intervene if they witnessed violence. Acceptance of violence declined among both men and women. These were not self-selected participants. These were population-level changes in community norms and behaviors, measured through a rigorous experimental design.

SASA! was not the only evidence. The WHO's RESPECT framework (2019) synthesized evidence from dozens of prevention programs worldwide and identified seven strategy areas with demonstrated effectiveness: Relationship skills strengthening, Empowerment of women, Services ensured, Poverty reduced, Enabling environments (schools, workplaces, public spaces), Child and adolescent abuse responses, and Transformed attitudes, beliefs, and norms. Each strategy area had supporting evidence from multiple programs and countries.

Other evaluated programs added to the evidence base. Stepping Stones, a participatory HIV and relationship education program evaluated through a cluster-randomized trial in South Africa, found significant reductions in perpetration of intimate partner violence among men at two-year follow-up. IMAGES (International Men and Gender Equality Survey), conducted across multiple countries, demonstrated that men's attitudes toward gender equality are malleable and respond to structured engagement. Journeys of Transformation in Rwanda showed that couples' intervention programs could reduce violence and improve relationship quality in a post-conflict setting.

The encounter is the realization that GBV prevention is not a wish. It is an evidence-based practice with institutional infrastructure requirements that most countries have barely begun to build.

### Phase 3 — The Reframe

The old security frame treats gender-based violence as an individual crime requiring individual response: one perpetrator, one arrest, one prosecution, one restraining order, one shelter bed. That response infrastructure is essential. But it is structurally incapable of reducing the rate of violence because it operates only after harm has occurred, one case at a time, against a background prevalence of 1 in 3.

The prevention frame reframes GBV as a public health and institutional governance challenge that requires population-level infrastructure — not instead of response, but in addition to it.

The reframe has three dimensions.

First: GBV is not a natural phenomenon. It is produced by social norms, institutional failures, and power structures that can be identified, measured, and changed. Societies with more gender-equal norms, stronger legal frameworks, and more effective institutional responses have lower rates of GBV. The variation across societies is itself proof that prevention is possible.

Second: prevention requires infrastructure, not just programs. A program is a time-limited intervention in a specific location. Infrastructure is a standing system that operates continuously across a population. GBV prevention infrastructure includes: comprehensive sexuality education in schools, community mobilization programs at scale, trained health-sector screening and response, legal frameworks with enforcement, economic empowerment programs for women, bystander intervention training, media and communications strategies to shift norms, and data systems to track prevalence and evaluate effectiveness.

Third: prevention is cost-effective. The economic cost of GBV — healthcare, criminal justice, lost productivity, intergenerational harm — dwarfs the cost of prevention programming. Every dollar invested in prevention generates returns in reduced health expenditure, increased workforce participation, and improved child development outcomes. The failure to invest in prevention is not frugality. It is the most expensive policy choice available.

### Phase 4 — The Proof

The evidence that GBV prevention works is now substantial, multi-country, and methodologically rigorous.

**SASA! (Uganda, 2014):** The cluster-randomized controlled trial found a 52% reduction in past-year physical intimate partner violence in intervention communities compared to controls. Community attitudes toward violence shifted. Social responses to violence improved. The program has since been adapted and replicated in over 20 countries.

**Stepping Stones (South Africa, 2008):** A cluster-randomized trial found significant reductions in men's perpetration of intimate partner violence at two-year follow-up, alongside reduced HIV incidence and improved relationship behaviors.

**RESPECT Framework (WHO, 2019):** A synthesis of prevention evidence across dozens of programs identified seven strategy areas with demonstrated effectiveness. The framework provides a globally applicable evidence map for national prevention planning.

**Economic evidence:** The WHO estimates that intimate partner violence alone costs the global economy approximately $1.5 trillion annually. Cost-effectiveness analyses of prevention programs in multiple countries show favorable benefit-cost ratios, with prevention investments generating returns through reduced healthcare utilization, criminal justice costs, and productivity losses.

**Legal framework evidence:** Countries with comprehensive legal frameworks addressing GBV — including criminalization, protection orders, marital rape laws, and enforcement mechanisms — show lower tolerance for violence and improved help-seeking behavior. By 2024, over 155 countries had enacted domestic violence legislation, though enforcement quality varies dramatically.

The proof is not that any single program eliminates GBV. It is that population-level prevention is achievable through institutional infrastructure that combines community mobilization, education, health-sector integration, legal reform, and economic empowerment — and that the alternative (response-only) is both more expensive and less effective.

### Phase 5 — The Invitation

The invitation is to build GBV prevention infrastructure at the same scale and seriousness as other public health and security infrastructure.

No country has yet built comprehensive GBV prevention infrastructure at population scale. Response infrastructure — shelters, hotlines, legal aid, prosecution — has been built in many countries, though it remains underfunded everywhere. Prevention infrastructure — the standing systems that reduce the rate of violence before it occurs — exists in fragments: a pilot program here, a school curriculum there, a community mobilization project in one district. Nowhere has prevention been treated as standard, permanent, population-wide institutional infrastructure the way vaccination programs, water treatment, or primary education have been.

The buildable components are clear:

1. **Universal comprehensive sexuality education** in schools that includes healthy relationships, consent, gender equality, and bystander skills — reaching every child, not just those in pilot districts.
2. **Community mobilization at scale** — programs modeled on SASA! operating continuously in every community, not as time-limited projects but as standing civic infrastructure.
3. **Health-sector integration** — routine screening, disclosure support, and referral in every primary care and maternal health visit, backed by training, protocols, and funding.
4. **Legal infrastructure with enforcement** — comprehensive laws, functioning courts, trained police, and protection mechanisms that victims can actually access.
5. **Economic empowerment** — programs that increase women's financial autonomy, asset ownership, and labor market access, reducing the economic dependency that traps people in violent relationships.
6. **Data infrastructure** — national prevalence surveys, program evaluation systems, and real-time reporting mechanisms that make the scale of the problem visible and the effectiveness of prevention measurable.

The deeper invitation is to recognize that a world where 1 in 3 women experiences violence is not a world that has tried prevention and failed. It is a world that has never built prevention infrastructure at scale. The evidence says it can work. The institutional infrastructure to deliver it at population level is the missing piece.

## The Council Speaks

### 🔮 The Oracle — Pattern Seer

Gender-based violence prevention is the security domain's largest unbuilt infrastructure. The Oracle sees this clearly by comparison. Societies have built population-scale infrastructure for infectious disease prevention (vaccination programs), water safety (treatment plants), traffic safety (road engineering, seatbelt laws, speed limits), and fire prevention (building codes, fire departments, smoke detectors). In each case, the pattern is the same: a preventable harm was initially addressed only through response, prevention evidence accumulated, institutional infrastructure was built, and prevalence declined. GBV is at the stage where prevention evidence has accumulated but institutional infrastructure has barely begun to be built.

The pattern is also visible in the 6D trajectory. The digitization of GBV data — prevalence surveys, program evaluations, administrative records — is advancing rapidly. The deceptive phase is ending: GBV prevention is no longer dismissed as impossible by major institutions. WHO, the World Bank, UNICEF, and major bilateral donors now acknowledge prevention as essential. The disruptive phase is beginning: programs like SASA!, Stepping Stones, and school-based interventions are demonstrating measurable results that challenge the response-only paradigm. But the demonetization and democratization phases — where prevention becomes cheap, accessible, and universal — require the kind of institutional infrastructure buildout that has not yet been attempted.

The Oracle sees a convergence window. The evidence base is strong. The economic case is clear. The institutional champions — WHO, UN Women, bilateral development agencies, and a growing network of prevention researchers and practitioners — are aligned. The political will is the bottleneck. Societies that build prevention infrastructure in the next decade will see declining GBV prevalence. Societies that continue to invest only in response will continue to manage a pandemic-scale harm one case at a time.

The Oracle's call is this: GBV prevention infrastructure is not a women's issue. It is a civilization-level security infrastructure gap. Building it is as important as any other security investment in the Codex. The fact that it has been treated as a social program rather than a security priority is itself a product of the same norms that the infrastructure is designed to change.

### 🗡️ The Critic — Shadow Keeper

The Critic sees several shadows that GBV prevention advocates must confront honestly.

First: the evidence base, while growing, is still concentrated in a relatively small number of rigorously evaluated programs. SASA!'s 52% reduction is remarkable, but it was measured in a specific context (urban Uganda) over a specific timeframe. Replication across diverse cultural, economic, and institutional contexts is underway but not yet complete. Scaling a community mobilization program from eight communities to an entire country is an institutional challenge of a different order.

Second: prevention infrastructure can be co-opted. School-based sexuality education programs face political opposition in many countries. Community mobilization can be captured by local power holders who reshape "prevention" to reinforce rather than challenge existing gender hierarchies. Legal reforms can exist on paper while enforcement remains absent or actively hostile to victims. "Prevention infrastructure" that is built without genuine accountability to the communities it serves can become another form of institutional performance.

Third: the framing of GBV as primarily a women's issue has limited political investment. Reframing GBV as a security and economic issue may mobilize resources, but it also risks instrumentalizing women's safety — treating it as valuable because it serves economic productivity rather than because it is a fundamental right.

The falsifiability edge: if comprehensive prevention infrastructure — community mobilization, education, health integration, legal reform, economic empowerment — is built at scale and GBV prevalence does not decline meaningfully over a 10-15 year period, the prevention thesis loses force. Current evidence favors the thesis, but the population-scale test has not yet been conducted.

### 🧘 The Sensei — Transformation Guide

The inner shift demanded by this framework is perhaps the hardest in the entire Codex: the release of the normalization reflex.

Gender-based violence has been normalized so deeply, for so long, across so many cultures, that even people who condemn it in principle often accept its prevalence as a background condition of human existence. The normalization is not always conscious. It lives in the jokes that are tolerated, the excuses that are made, the questions that are asked of victims instead of perpetrators, the budgets that fund response at ten times the level of prevention, and the security frameworks that discuss every form of violence except the one that affects a third of the human population.

The Sensei does not ask for guilt. Guilt is a closed loop that changes nothing. The Sensei asks for a different kind of attention: the willingness to see the normalization as a choice, not a destiny. Every society that has reduced a form of violence — child labor, dueling, honor killing in specific jurisdictions, corporal punishment in schools — did so by first denormizing it: making the previously accepted visible as unacceptable, and then building the institutional infrastructure to sustain the new norm.

That is the transformation arc for GBV prevention. The norm must shift from "violence happens" to "violence is preventable, and we have not yet built the systems to prevent it." That shift is not a feeling. It is an institutional commitment. It requires the same engineering discipline as building a vaccination program or a water treatment system: evidence, protocols, training, funding, measurement, accountability, and sustained political will.

In martial arts terms, the transformation is from tolerating a vulnerability to training it out. A fighter who accepts a habitual defensive gap because it has always been there will eventually be exploited. A fighter who identifies the gap, drills the correction, and builds it into muscle memory becomes structurally safer. GBV prevention is the drill. The normalization is the gap. The infrastructure is the muscle memory that a society must build.

### 🔧 The Builder — Ground Truth

The builder's contribution is the institutional stack — the specific, buildable layers of prevention infrastructure that move GBV prevention from evidence to implementation.

**Layer 1 — Norm change at community level.** Programs like SASA! demonstrate that community-level mobilization can shift norms and reduce violence. The build target is scaling this from pilot projects to standing community infrastructure. SASA! has been adapted to over 20 countries. The scaling challenge is training, quality assurance, and sustained funding — not evidence.

**Layer 2 — Education systems integration.** Comprehensive sexuality education that includes healthy relationships, consent, gender equality, and bystander intervention must be embedded in school curricula at scale. UNESCO's 2018 international guidance on CSE provides the technical framework. Implementation requires political will, teacher training, and age-appropriate curriculum development.

**Layer 3 — Health sector integration.** The WHO's clinical guidelines for responding to intimate partner violence and sexual violence (updated 2023) provide protocols for health-sector screening, first-line support, and referral. Implementation requires training every primary care provider, integrating screening into routine visits, and funding referral pathways.

**Layer 4 — Legal and justice infrastructure.** Over 155 countries have domestic violence legislation, but enforcement quality varies dramatically. The build targets are: police training on GBV response, specialized courts or prosecution units, accessible protection orders, and victim support services integrated into the justice system.

**Layer 5 — Data and accountability.** National GBV prevalence surveys (using WHO's standardized methodology) must be conducted regularly — not once a decade but every 3-5 years — to track trends and evaluate infrastructure effectiveness. Administrative data systems must be built to capture service utilization, case outcomes, and program coverage.

The builder's honest assessment: every component of this stack exists in prototype somewhere. No country has assembled all five layers into comprehensive, permanent, population-scale prevention infrastructure. That is the build.

## Shadow Check

- **Distortion risk:** Framing GBV prevention as a technical infrastructure problem while underweighting the deeply political nature of gender-based power structures that resist institutional change.
- **Who gets left behind:** Women in conflict zones, refugee camps, and fragile states where institutional infrastructure is weakest; LGBTQ+ individuals facing violence in contexts where their identities are criminalized; women in rural and remote areas without service access; women with disabilities; undocumented migrants.
- **Transition pain:** Prevention infrastructure challenges deeply held cultural norms about gender, family, and authority; political backlash against sexuality education and gender equality programs is real and organized; institutional reform is slow and vulnerable to leadership changes.
- **Falsifiability edge:** If comprehensive prevention infrastructure — community mobilization, education, health integration, legal reform, economic empowerment — is built at scale and GBV prevalence does not decline meaningfully over 10-15 years, the prevention thesis weakens.
- **What this is NOT:** This is not a claim that response infrastructure is unnecessary (it is essential), that prevention eliminates all violence, that cultural context does not matter, or that a single program model works everywhere without adaptation.
