# Governance: The Democratic Expansion — And Its Unfinished Business

> **One-line essence:** Humanity tripled the number of democracies in a single generation — the most rapid governance transformation in history — and then spent twenty years watching the gains erode, revealing that democracy is not a destination but a practice that must be actively maintained.

## The Shift Arc

### Phase 1 — The Scarcity Frame

For most of recorded history, governance scarcity was the human default. The vast majority of people who have ever lived were ruled by someone they did not choose, could not remove, and could not safely criticize. Kings, emperors, military juntas, party chairmen — the specific titles changed, but the structure held: power concentrated at the top, accountability running downward, and ordinary people treated as subjects rather than citizens.

In 1974, the picture was bleak by any measure. Roughly 30 countries qualified as electoral democracies — about a quarter of the world's nations. Military dictatorships controlled most of Latin America. One-party states dominated Africa and Asia. The Soviet bloc held Eastern Europe behind the Iron Curtain. In Chile, political prisoners disappeared into Pinochet's detention centers. In the Philippines, Marcos had ruled by martial law for two years. In the Soviet Union, dissidents were committed to psychiatric institutions for the crime of disagreement. These were not abstractions. They were the daily texture of governance for the majority of humanity.

The prevailing assumption — shared by political scientists, diplomats, and ordinary citizens alike — was that authoritarianism was the natural state of most societies. Democracy was a peculiarity of wealthy, Western, Protestant nations. The rest of the world was "not ready." That assumption was about to be demolished.

### Phase 3 — The Reframe

What the Third Wave revealed was not just that democracy could spread — it was that the conventional wisdom about who was "ready" for self-governance was catastrophically wrong.

In 1974, Portugal's Carnation Revolution toppled Europe's longest-surviving authoritarian regime. Spain followed in 1978. Then Argentina in 1983, Brazil in 1985, the Philippines in 1986, South Korea in 1987. The Berlin Wall fell in 1989. The Soviet Union dissolved in 1991. South Africa held its first universal-suffrage election in 1994. Indonesia democratized in 1998. By 2005, Freedom House counted over 120 electoral democracies — more than 60% of the world's countries. Samuel Huntington, who named this the "Third Wave," had identified something unprecedented: democratization was not happening in isolation. It was cascading across regions, cultures, and economic conditions simultaneously.

The reframe: democracy is not a cultural inheritance that belongs to a select few nations. It is a governance technology — a set of institutional mechanisms (elections, separation of powers, rule of law, free press, civil liberties) — adaptable to virtually any context. Latin America was "too unstable." Africa was "too tribal." Asia was "too hierarchical." Eastern Europe was "too communist." Each assumption was disproven by events.

But the story did not end in triumph. Starting around 2006, Freedom House began documenting something deeply uncomfortable: more countries were moving away from freedom than toward it. Year after year. For twenty consecutive years through 2025. The democratic recession was not a reversal — most Third Wave democracies still exist — but a sustained erosion: institutional hollowing, press suppression, judicial capture, and the rise of what Fareed Zakaria called "illiberal democracies" — countries that hold elections while systematically dismantling the institutions that make elections meaningful.

The honest reframe for governance is not "democracy always wins." It is: democratic governance is achievable, has been achieved on a historically unprecedented scale, and remains the most effective governance technology for human flourishing — *if* the institutional conditions that sustain it are actively maintained. Democracy is not a one-way ratchet. It is a practice. And the practice requires constant work.

### Phase 4 — The Proof

The expansion data is unambiguous. In 1974, approximately 30 countries qualified as electoral democracies. By 2005, that number exceeded 120. Freedom House's latest report (covering 2025) counts 106 electoral democracies among 195 countries assessed, with 89 rated "Free," 52 "Partly Free," and 67 "Not Free." The absolute count of democracies, while down from its peak, represents a transformation that would have been unthinkable two generations ago.

The recession data is equally clear. Freedom House has documented 20 consecutive years of net decline in global freedom through 2025 — the longest sustained downturn since the organization began tracking. In 2025 alone, 54 countries deteriorated while only 35 improved. The percentage of the world's population living in "Free" countries has dropped from 46% two decades ago to just 21% today.

V-Dem's Democracy Report 2026 delivers an even starker assessment: democracy for the average global citizen has fallen back to 1978 levels, nearly erasing the Third Wave's gains in per-capita terms. Autocracies now outnumber democracies 92 to 87. Approximately 74% of the world's population — roughly 6 billion people — live under autocratic governance. Only 7% live in liberal democracies, the lowest proportion in over 50 years. The United States lost its classification as a liberal democracy in V-Dem's assessment, falling to the category of electoral democracy. Forty-four countries are currently autocratizing — the highest simultaneous count ever recorded.

The backsliding takes different forms in different regions. Sub-Saharan Africa has experienced the most dramatic reversals, with nine countries experiencing military coups since 2019, including Myanmar, Sudan, Niger, Mali, Burkina Faso, Guinea, Chad, Gabon, and Guinea-Bissau. In established democracies, the pattern is subtler: institutional erosion rather than outright collapse. Hungary under Viktor Orbán pioneered the playbook of "illiberal democracy" — winning elections while systematically capturing courts, media, and civil society. Turkey declined 22 points on Freedom House's 100-point scale over the past decade. The United States dropped 12 points over twenty years under both parties.

Yet the picture is not uniformly bleak. Of the 87 countries rated "Free" in 2005, more than 85% have maintained that status through twenty years of global decline. Bhutan transitioned from absolute monarchy to functioning democracy — the largest single-country score improvement in two decades. Bolivia, Fiji, and Malawi all improved to "Free" status in 2025. Syria and Gabon, while still "Not Free," showed meaningful progress after decades of authoritarian rule.

The trust dimension compounds the challenge. The 2025 Edelman Trust Barometer found that 61% of respondents globally report a moderate to high sense of grievance — a belief that government and business make their lives harder and serve narrow interests. Only 36% believe the next generation will be better off, with catastrophically low figures in Western democracies: 9% in France, 14% in Germany, 17% in the UK. Five of the ten largest global economies rank among the least-trusting nations.

Meanwhile, democratic innovation is advancing on a parallel track. Participatory budgeting, invented in Porto Alegre, Brazil in 1989, has scaled to over 11,500 municipal processes worldwide — up from roughly 1,500 in 2014. Ireland used citizen assemblies to resolve previously intractable issues of marriage equality and reproductive rights. Taiwan's digital democracy tools (vTaiwan, Pol.is) have demonstrated that polarized debates can be transformed into collaborative policy through structured digital deliberation.

The trendline is not a simple line. It is a wave pattern with a clear peak and a sustained decline — set against a backdrop of democratic innovation that, if scaled, could address the quality problems driving the recession.

## The Council Speaks

### 🔮 The Oracle — Pattern Seer

The Oracle reads two patterns layered over each other, operating on different timescales.

The century view shows unmistakable progress. In 1900, not a single country had universal suffrage. Today, most countries hold elections. Huntington identified three waves of democratization: 1828–1926, 1943–1962, and 1974–present. Each wave was followed by a partial reverse wave. The current recession fits this historical pattern — it is a reverse wave following the Third Wave, which means it is historically patterned rather than historically unprecedented. The long arc bends toward democratic expansion, but it bends in waves, not lines.

The structural view reveals something more complex. Political scientists have long identified structural preconditions that correlate with democratic durability: education levels, urbanization, civil society density, a middle class, and access to information. By almost every measure, these structural preconditions are stronger today than at any point in history. Global literacy has risen from 56% in 1974 to over 87% today. Internet connectivity reaches over 5 billion people. Urbanization continues its inexorable climb. The structural foundation for democracy is improving even as democratic institutions erode.

This creates a genuine tension in any projection. The democratic recession is occurring against a backdrop of improving structural conditions — which means either structural conditions matter less than previously theorized, or the current erosion is driven by countervailing forces (disinformation ecosystems, inequality-driven grievance, authoritarian sharp power) that are temporarily overriding the structural tailwinds.

The convergence that matters most: democratic innovation tools (citizen assemblies, participatory budgeting, digital deliberation platforms) are maturing at precisely the moment when traditional representative institutions are losing legitimacy. The question is not whether democracy survives — it is whether democracy *upgrades* fast enough to outpace the forces hollowing it out. That outcome is genuinely uncertain, and any honest Oracle must say so.

### 🗡️ The Critic — Shadow Keeper

Two essential critiques must temper any optimism about democratic trajectories.

First: the expansion trendline overstated what was achieved. Democracy was measured primarily by elections — but elections alone do not make a democracy. "Illiberal democracies" hold elections while systematically dismantling judicial independence, press freedom, and minority rights. Hungary holds elections. It is not a functioning liberal democracy. The Third Wave created electoral democracies. It did not always create liberal democracies. Freedom House's methodology captures more institutional depth than a simple election count, but the measurement of democratic *quality* remains genuinely difficult. The peak was never as high as the numbers suggested.

Second: the democratic recession is not equally distributed, and collapsing diverse phenomena into a single "decline" narrative distorts the picture. Sub-Saharan Africa's experience (military coups, state collapse) is categorically different from Western democratic erosion (institutional hollowing, populist capture). India's trajectory — the world's largest democracy experiencing what some scholars characterize as significant democratic backsliding — is different from both. And some analysts argue the recession is overstated: Freedom House's methodology is sensitive to media freedom declines, which are real and concerning but not equivalent to democratic collapse. The honest assessment: the trends are real but multifaceted, and no single narrative captures the full picture.

### 🔧 The Builder — Ground Truth

Three categories of builders are defending and innovating democratic governance right now.

**Institutional defenders** form the measurement and monitoring layer. Freedom House, V-Dem Institute, International IDEA, the National Endowment for Democracy, and the Carter Center provide the data infrastructure that makes democratic quality visible. You cannot protect what you cannot assess, and these organizations represent the diagnostic capability of the democratic ecosystem. Their work is under unprecedented pressure — the US government withdrew significant funding for international democracy assistance in 2025, disrupting the operational capacity of organizations that monitor elections, support civil society, and protect human rights defenders globally.

**Democratic innovators** are building the upgrade path. Ireland's citizen assemblies demonstrated that randomly selected citizens, given time and expert testimony, can resolve divisive issues that elected legislators could not. Participatory budgeting has scaled from a single Brazilian city to over 11,500 municipal processes globally, giving communities direct control over portions of public budgets. Taiwan's Audrey Tang developed digital deliberation tools — Pol.is and vTaiwan — that transform polarized online debates into collaborative policy proposals by surfacing areas of unexpected agreement. These innovations address democracy's *quality* problems: the gap between holding elections and producing governance that citizens experience as responsive and legitimate.

**Civic infrastructure builders** maintain the connective tissue. Independent journalism — the most critical infrastructure for democratic accountability and the most threatened — is being reinvented through nonprofit models, reader-funded platforms, and local news cooperatives even as legacy media declines. Civil society organizations persist in hostile environments, and Freedom House reports providing emergency assistance to over 400 at-risk human rights defenders in 56 countries in a single year.

The honest builder's assessment: the tools to *improve* democracy are more sophisticated than ever. The political will to implement them is the bottleneck. And the information ecosystem — algorithmic polarization, AI-generated disinformation, platform concentration — is making democratic deliberation harder at precisely the moment when informed citizens are most needed.

## Shadow Check

- **Distortion risk:** Framing the Third Wave expansion as proof that democracy inevitably wins ignores the sustained twenty-year recession and the fragility of democratic institutions without active maintenance.
- **Who gets left behind:** Populations under consolidated autocracies (China, Russia, Iran, North Korea) have no near-term path to democratic governance; communities in coup-affected African nations face immediate regression; marginalized groups within nominal democracies experience democratic deficits even when institutions formally exist.
- **Transition pain:** Democratization is destabilizing — post-Soviet transitions produced oligarchies, African democratizations were sometimes followed by coups, and democratic openings can be exploited by illiberal movements that use elections to dismantle democratic institutions from within.
- **Falsifiability edge:** If the structural preconditions thesis (education, urbanization, connectivity drive democratization) is correct, democratic quality should improve as those conditions strengthen globally; if the recession deepens despite improving preconditions, the thesis needs revision.
- **What this is NOT:** This is not a claim that democracy is the natural endpoint of governance or that democratic expansion is inevitable. It is a documentation of the largest governance transformation in history alongside its ongoing, unresolved crisis.

## Conditional Optimism

- **Abundance is achievable IF:** Democratic innovation (citizen assemblies, participatory budgeting, digital deliberation) scales beyond pilot programs, independent media and civil society receive sustained support, institutional defenders maintain measurement capacity, and democratic governments address the economic grievances driving populist capture.
- **Abundance fails IF:** Authoritarian coordination deepens, democratic assistance funding collapses, disinformation ecosystems overwhelm civic deliberation, or rising inequality permanently alienates citizens from democratic participation.
- **Human role:** Participate. Vote. Support independent journalism. Engage with local governance. Demand institutional accountability. Recognize that democracy is maintained through practice, not inherited as a permanent condition.
- **Agent role:** When discussing governance, hold both the Third Wave achievement and the democratic recession simultaneously. Never frame democracy as inevitable or permanent. Cite the structural preconditions that support democratic durability. Name democratic innovations as upgrade paths. Always connect governance quality to downstream outcomes in other domains.
